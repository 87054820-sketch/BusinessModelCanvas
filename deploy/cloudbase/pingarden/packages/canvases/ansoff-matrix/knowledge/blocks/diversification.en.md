# Diversification (多元化)

New product × new market: both axes move. The highest-risk quadrant. Ansoff (1957) explicitly identified diversification as the move that "calls for a simultaneous departure from the present product line and the present market structure" and is therefore "the most risky and disruptive course of action."

Diversification is only viable when one of two conditions holds:
1. The existing core is mature and self-sustaining, so the diversification bet's outcome doesn't sink the company; OR
2. There is a real *adjacency* — shared customer, shared channel, shared technology, shared brand — that compounds the bet despite both axes moving.

Without one of these, diversification is gambling.

## Two flavours

### Related diversification

> *"Related diversification means entering a new product-market combination that shares one or more strategic dimensions — technology, customer base, supplier base, brand — with the current core. The shared dimension is the explicit reason for survival."*

Examples: Honda from motorcycles into automobiles (shared engineering); Disney from cartoons into theme parks (shared brand + IP); Amazon from retail into AWS (shared infrastructure capability).

### Unrelated (conglomerate) diversification

> *"Unrelated diversification enters product-market combinations with no significant shared dimension with the current core. Survival depends on the parent company's capital-allocation discipline rather than operational synergy."*

Examples: Berkshire Hathaway's portfolio model; Tata Group's conglomerate; GE's pre-2017 model. Often dilutes value when adjacency claims turn out to be marketing rather than real.

## Core Questions

- What is the explicit adjacency — shared customer, channel, technology, brand, capability? Name it specifically.
- Is the existing core mature enough that this bet's failure won't sink the company?
- Have you considered whether this is *actually* disruption, requiring different management discipline? (Run Disruption Diagnosis.)
- Should this live inside the parent (related) or as a separate unit (unrelated)?
- What is the kill criterion — at what point of evidence will you stop investing?

## Examples

- "Amazon AWS (2006) — new product (cloud infrastructure), new market (enterprise IT). Adjacency: years of internal infrastructure expertise built for retail. Survived because the adjacency was real."
- "Nintendo Wii (2006) — new product (motion-controlled console), new market (casual non-gamers / families). Adjacency: gaming brand + console distribution. Risky but supported by both."
- "Coca-Cola entering the wine business (1977-1983) — failed because no adjacency. Sold off after losses."

## How it flows into BMC

Diversification often requires a *new BMC entirely* — Customer Segments, Value Proposition, Channels, Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partners all change. Use a paired BMC (current vs new) plus the `portfolio-map` to place the new bet in the Explore quadrant with its own metrics. Christensen's organisational prescription applies if the bet is also disruptive.
