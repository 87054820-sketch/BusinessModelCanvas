# When to use the Ansoff Matrix

Use the Ansoff Matrix when a single business has multiple plausible growth directions and the team must name which one it is choosing. The canvas places each candidate move on a 2×2 grid — existing/new product × existing/new market — yielding four routes ordered by execution risk: market penetration (lowest), market development, product development, and diversification (highest).

It is also useful as a discipline against *accidental diversification*: teams often slide from market penetration into diversification one feature at a time, without naming the shift in risk profile.

## Reach for it when

- The business has 2-4 plausible growth bets and the team disagrees on which to commit to.
- The team is sliding from penetration into diversification feature-by-feature and must name the shift in risk.
- A board is comparing growth bets and needs an explicit risk-ordered framing.
- The team is preparing to map a growth move onto the BMC and needs the directional choice locked in first.

## Skip it when

- You have a multi-business portfolio (not a single business) — use **BCG Growth-Share Matrix** first to allocate across SBUs, then Ansoff per-business.
- The question is about the time horizon of growth (today / 1-3y / 3-10y) — use **McKinsey Three Horizons**; Ansoff is direction, Three Horizons is timing. They pair, not substitute.
- The candidate is a disruptive bet — use **Disruption Diagnosis** to test the disruption hypothesis before placing it as Ansoff diversification.
- The growth move is so close to current operations that "penetration" is obvious — Ansoff adds little; go straight to BMC + Design Criteria.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **BCG**: classifies *existing* businesses by share × growth (snapshot, multi-business). Ansoff picks the *direction* of growth for one business (forward, single-business). Per-BCG-quadrant: Cash Cow → Ansoff penetration; Star → market or product dev; Question Mark → Ansoff is the decision tool; Dog → harvest or attempt diversification.
- **Three Horizons**: time-portfolio layer. H1 work is usually penetration; H2 is often product or market dev; H3 is often diversification or disruption.
- **Business Model Portfolio Management**: Explore/Exploit organisational framing. Ansoff names what the next bet looks like; Portfolio Management decides where to put it on the Portfolio Map.
- **Disruption Diagnosis**: a diversification bet might be disruptive — pass the disruption test to find out.

## From the source

> *"Diversification is distinguished from the other three strategies by the fact that whereas market penetration, market development, and product development usually represent gradual departures from past patterns, diversification generally calls for a simultaneous departure from the present product line and the present market structure. Inevitably this is the most risky and disruptive course of action."*
>
> — H. Igor Ansoff, *Strategies for Diversification*, Harvard Business Review, September 1957, p. 114.

Ansoff's 1957 HBR article introduced this 2×2 as a corporate-strategy decision tool. The risk ordering is *explicit* in the original text — diversification was always the highest-risk corner, not a "let's also do this" addition.

## What good looks like

- Current position (existing × existing) is marked, with candidate moves placed in each candidate's natural quadrant.
- Each candidate has 3 stickies: what stays the same (blue), what must change (green), the riskiest assumption (pink).
- At least one candidate is *explicitly killed* on the canvas — picking by deselection matters more than picking by selection.
- The Growth Rationale zone names BMC blocks that must change for the chosen quadrant.
- Capacity constraints (purple) are made explicit if multiple quadrants are pursued in parallel.
- Risk arrow (from penetration to diversification) is *acknowledged*, not ignored — diversification is not "the bold choice"; it is the highest-risk choice.
