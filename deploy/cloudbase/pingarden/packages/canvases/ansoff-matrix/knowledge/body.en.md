# How to use

1. **Plot the current position.** Mark "where this business is today" at existing-product × existing-market — usually the bottom-left corner. Anchor everything else relative to this.
2. **Brainstorm candidate growth moves.** Generate 2-4 specific candidates — not "expand internationally" but "launch in Brazil through partnership with X distributor, targeting segment Y, by Q3 2027".
3. **Place each candidate in its natural quadrant.** Honestly. A "new feature for current customers" is product development, not penetration. A "same product, new buyer type" is market development, not penetration.
4. **For each candidate, fill 3 stickies.** Blue: what stays the same (BMC blocks, customers, capabilities). Green: what must change (new channels, resources, partners). Pink: the riskiest assumption with a falsifiable metric.
5. **Compare candidates on expected return × risk × strategic fit.** Use the risk arrow as a check: are you committing to a higher-risk quadrant than you intend?
6. **Pick one quadrant per growth horizon.** Do not stack two quadrants on the same business in the same period without explicit capacity (purple stickies).
7. **Write the Growth Rationale zone.** Name the BMC blocks that must change, the next-12-month milestones, and the explicit decision to *not* pursue the other quadrants for now.

# How to interpret each quadrant

- **Market Penetration** — Lowest-risk. Levers: usage frequency, repeat purchase, share-of-wallet, win-back, pricing optimisation, loyalty. Defend with churn reduction and existing-customer engagement.
- **Market Development** — Medium risk. Product is proven, customer is not. Riskiest assumption: does the new segment / geography / occasion actually need the product, in the form you currently deliver it?
- **Product Development** — Medium risk. Buyer is proven, product is not. Riskiest assumption: can you actually build the new product without breaking the existing one — does it share the cost base, channels, brand?
- **Diversification** — Highest risk. Both axes move. Only viable when there is a clear adjacency (shared customer, shared channel, shared technology, shared brand) OR when the core is mature enough that the bet's outcome doesn't sink the company.

# Quality checklist

- Each candidate is in a *specific* quadrant — no "between penetration and market dev" cop-outs.
- Each candidate has its own riskiest assumption, falsifiable with a metric.
- Diversification candidates explicitly name the adjacency that makes them survivable.
- The Growth Rationale zone closes the loop with BMC block changes — not "we will figure it out".
- At least one candidate has been deselected explicitly (or all candidates have a "go / no-go" status).
- Capacity is acknowledged — multiple parallel quadrants require visible capacity reservation.

# Common mistakes

- Calling a small feature addition "diversification" — most are product development.
- Treating Ansoff as a one-shot decision rather than a recurring one (Spotify, Amazon walked through all 4 over 15-25 years).
- Picking diversification because the other quadrants feel "boring", without naming the adjacency.
- Confusing market development (same product, new buyer) with product development (new product, same buyer) — the riskiest assumption differs.
- Skipping the "what must change in the BMC" mapping, so the team plans growth without provisioning resources.
- Pursuing 3 quadrants in parallel without acknowledging the capacity required (especially management attention).
