# When to use the Customer Journey

The Customer Journey Canvas (Design A Better Business; closely related
to journey maps in service design) is a one-page picture of **what a
specific person experiences over time** as they interact with your
product, service, or organisation. It splits the journey into stages
along a horizontal timeline, and at each stage names what the customer
**needs**, what the **key moment** looks like, and how **satisfied**
they feel.

The whole point is that the picture has a **left-to-right shape**.
When you read the satisfaction row across the timeline, you see the
emotional curve of the experience — and the dips are where redesign
attention is most valuable. A list of pains never gives you that;
only a journey does.

## Reach for it when

- A team needs to understand friction across an **end-to-end
  experience**, not just a single feature or screen.
- You suspect the experience drifts (great onboarding, drab middle,
  panicky renewal) and want to **make the shape visible**.
- Multiple teams own different stages, and decisions made in one stage
  silently break a later stage. The journey is the **shared map**
  between them.
- You're moving from research synthesis (Empathy Map / interviews) to
  design priorities and need an artefact that says **"fix this here,
  in this order, because of this dip."**

## Skip it when

- The decision is about a single touchpoint with no time dimension.
- You haven't observed real customers yet — a journey map invented
  from the team's seat is fiction. Do the interviews, watch the
  sessions, read the support tickets first.
- You're trying to map several distinct personas at once on a single
  canvas. One persona per journey, or the picture stays vague.
- You're documenting an internal workflow. Internal flows want a
  service blueprint or a swimlane diagram, not a customer journey.
