# How to fill it out

The journey canvas reads top-to-bottom for **one stage**, and
left-to-right for **the shape of the whole experience**. Both axes
matter; both have to be filled deliberately, in this order:

## The order

1. **Persona** — _Whose journey is this?_ Pick **one** specific
   protagonist. Name them, sketch their situation and the goal that
   makes them care about this experience in the first place. If you
   have an Empathy Map for this person, the persona band is just a
   one-line summary of it; otherwise, write the situation and goal
   here as a working hypothesis.
2. **Stages** — _What are the chapters of this journey?_ Five is the
   default; tighter or wider is fine. Common shapes: Discovery →
   Consideration → Onboarding → Use → Renewal/Advocacy. Name them at
   the top of each column (a sticky on the persona band, or the
   leftmost sticky in each column). Don't make stages equal-width in
   time — they almost never are.
3. **Customer Needs** (per stage) — _What does the customer need to
   get done **at this stage**?_ This is **stage-specific**. "Decide
   which tool to buy" is a need at Consideration; "get value on day
   one" is a need at Onboarding. If a need is identical across all
   five columns, you've written it too generically.
4. **Key Moment** (per stage) — _What does this moment look like as
   a snapshot?_ Where, when, who, on which channel, with what
   artefact in hand. The "polaroid" metaphor: if you took a photo
   right now, what would be in frame?
5. **Customer Satisfaction** (per stage) — _How does the customer
   feel here?_ Happy / neutral / frustrated. One sticky per stage,
   placed visually high (😊), middle (😐), or low (☹️) so the row
   reads as a curve.

## Why the order matters

Persona first, because every later sticky belongs to one specific
person — drift away from that person and the journey becomes generic.
Stages next, because the columns frame what counts as "this stage."
Then needs (functional anchor), key moments (concrete texture), and
satisfaction last (the synthesis layer). Reading the satisfaction row
across is the punch line — that's where the design opportunity lives.

## Quality habits

- **One persona per journey.** A canvas trying to cover three personas
  at once becomes a fuzzy compromise. Duplicate the canvas instead.
- **Feed the canvas with research.** Quotes, support tickets, session
  recordings — every sticky should trace to a real observation. If
  the source pile is empty, you're writing fiction.
- **Stages are not time-uniform.** "Discovery" might span 6 weeks;
  "First login" might span 90 seconds. Don't let the equal-width
  columns trick you into pretending otherwise.
- **The dips are the work.** Where satisfaction drops, draw an arrow
  in the discussion: "what would it take to lift this?" That's how
  the journey graduates from artefact to roadmap input.
- **Date and refresh.** A stale journey misleads. Re-open after every
  research cycle.

## Common failure modes

- **No research input** → the journey is the team's vibe, not the
  customer's experience. Recover by going back to the source material.
- **Inventing happiness.** A row of all-😊 stickies is suspicious.
  Customers usually have at least one valley; the canvas's job is to
  name it.
- **Stages-as-features.** Stages should be **what the customer does**
  ("compare tools"), not **what your team builds** ("pricing page").
  When the stage names match your sitemap, you've drawn an internal
  flow, not a journey.
- **Multiple personas on one map.** One map per persona, always. The
  emotional curve of an enterprise admin and a frontline user are
  different curves.

## What to do with a finished journey

- **Pump unmet needs into the Value Proposition Canvas.** The needs
  row, especially the ones marked "frustrated" in satisfaction, feeds
  directly into VPC's Pains and Gains.
- **Pump emotional valleys into the product roadmap.** The dips in
  the satisfaction row are the highest-leverage places to invest.
  Number them, sequence them.
- **Check whether Bain value elements actually appear.** If VPC tags
  `[emotional-reduces anxiety]` or `[social-badge value]`, the journey
  must show which touchpoint creates it; a value element with no
  touchpoint is probably marketing copy.
- **Pair with the Empathy Map.** The map gives you depth on the
  person; the journey gives you breadth across time. Together they
  explain both **who** and **when**.
- **Pair with Jobs To Be Done.** Per-stage needs are stage-scoped
  jobs; JTBD then sharpens the highest-priority ones into something
  actionable.
