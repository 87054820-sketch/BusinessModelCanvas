# When to use the Experiment Canvas

The Experiment Canvas turns risky assumptions into observable, measurable, reviewable experiments. Use it when you already have a value proposition, business-model direction, or design criteria, but still need evidence for the assumptions that matter most.

## The three risks an experiment validates

Per **Bland & Osterwalder · *Testing Business Ideas* · Wiley · 2019**, every business idea carries three risk types — **classify your riskiest assumption against these before designing the experiment**:

- **Desirability** — do customers actually want it? (Most early-stage assumptions live here.)
- **Feasibility** — can we build / deliver it? (Tech, IP, partners, capabilities.)
- **Viability** — can we earn money from it? (Pricing, willingness to pay, unit economics.)

## Discovery vs Validation

A second axis from TBI: **Discovery** experiments are cheap, weak-evidence, and meant to course-correct fast (Customer Interview, Online Survey, Storyboard, Boomerang). **Validation** experiments cost more but produce stronger evidence (Smoke Test, Wizard of Oz, Concierge, Letter of Intent, Pre-Sale). Default to Discovery first; jump to Validation only when (a) Discovery has pointed in the direction and (b) you need stronger evidence to commit engineering or capital.

## Reach for it when

- You have identified the most dangerous uncertainty (one of D / F / V).
- The team wants to learn before investing significant build or operating effort.
- You need to define method, metrics, sample, timeframe, and ownership.
- You want interviews, landing pages, prototype tests, A/B tests, or sales trials to become a repeatable learning loop.

## Skip it when

- You do not yet have a clear problem or assumption to test — Discovery work (interviews, forums) comes first.
- You are planning delivery work rather than validating uncertainty.
- You already have sufficient evidence and only need execution.
