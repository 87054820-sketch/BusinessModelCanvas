# When to use Porter's Value Chain

Use Porter's Value Chain when a team has a strategic intent ("compete on quality" / "lowest cost" / "service-led") but cannot yet point at *which internal activities must change* to make that intent true. The canvas decomposes the firm into 9 activities — 5 primary (Inbound Logistics → Operations → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service) and 4 support (Firm Infrastructure, HRM, Technology Development, Procurement) — plus the resulting Margin. It locates where competitive advantage actually forms and where the linkages between activities reinforce or fight each other.

## Reach for it when

- The strategy intent is known ("we will be the lowest cost" / "we will compete on quality") but the team cannot agree on which capabilities to invest in.
- Margin is shrinking and no single activity owner can explain why — the leak is usually a *linkage* between two activities, not inside one.
- A team is preparing to fill the BMC's Key Activities / Key Resources / Key Partners blocks and needs the higher-resolution view first.
- An incumbent is asking which of its current capabilities are real moats vs. which are commodity.
- A challenger is asking which activity it must out-execute to win, not just which one to do "well".

## Skip it when

- The question is "what business are we in" — use **Business Model Canvas** first, then come to Value Chain for execution detail.
- The question is "how the industry is structured" — use **Porter's Five Forces**, which sits above the firm, not inside it.
- The firm is a platform / two-sided marketplace where the load-bearing activity is network coordination, not physical activity — use **Platform Strategy** + **Platform Ecosystem Map** as the primary lens.
- The question is "can the organisation actually execute this strategy" — use **McKinsey 7-S**, which checks soft-side coherence; Value Chain locates the activities, 7-S checks whether the org runs them.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **BMC Key Activities / Resources / Partners** are higher-level summaries of the same 9 activities; Value Chain is the high-resolution version. Use both: BMC for the one-pager, Value Chain when "Key Activity = Operations" is too vague.
- **McKinsey 7-S** asks about coherence of soft + hard elements. Value Chain asks about advantage at the activity level. A strategy depending on activity X but mismatched in 7-S will fail.
- **Platform Strategy** is the right primary lens for multi-sided businesses where network effects dominate. The Value Chain still applies but most weight shifts to Technology Development + Procurement.

## From the book

> *"Competitive advantage cannot be understood by looking at a firm as a whole. It stems from the many discrete activities a firm performs in designing, producing, marketing, delivering, and supporting its product. Each of these activities can contribute to a firm's relative cost position and create a basis for differentiation."*
>
> — Michael E. Porter, *Competitive Advantage*, Free Press, 1985, p. 33.

The book introduces the value chain as a diagnostic. Porter's central claim is that "competitive advantage" is an aggregate that hides which specific activities actually create it. The canvas inherits that diagnostic stance: do not say "we compete on quality"; say "Operations + Service + HRM jointly create quality, and Procurement reinforces it."

## What good looks like

- Every activity has 2-4 concrete sub-activities written, not just a category label.
- At least 2 linkages (purple) are visible — most real differentiators are linkage-based.
- Support activities are filled with the same density as primary activities, not skipped.
- Cost-advantage vs differentiation-advantage colours are present and considered, not omitted because "we'll figure it out later".
- The Margin zone names where money actually comes from, with rough numbers.
- Every advantage-bearing activity is mapped onto a BMC block via a follow-on sticky.
