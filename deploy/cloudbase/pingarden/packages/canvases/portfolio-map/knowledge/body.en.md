# Reading the map

Two portfolios, four axes, one rule: a business model's place on the map
is a hypothesis about its **risk** (X) and its **return** (Y). The job is
to move bubbles in the direction those axes reward — and to keep moving
new bubbles into the picture so the company always has a next act.

## The two portfolios

![The Portfolio Map template — Explore (bottom-left) and Exploit (top-right) with their respective axes. Source: *The Invincible Company*](assets/pm-template.png)

### Exploit (top-right block)

What you already run.

- **X-axis — Disruption Risk.** Right is *less* risk: the model is
  protected by moats, hard to copy, riding tailwinds. Left is high risk:
  the model is being eaten by a substitute, a new entrant, a regulatory
  shift, or its own cost structure.
- **Y-axis — Returns.** Up is real money you book today.

A healthy Exploit portfolio is concentrated in the upper-right and is
**actively pushing models rightward** as their disruption risk grows. It
is *not* simply "high return = good." A high-return business slipping
left (rising disruption risk) is the most dangerous bubble on the map.

### Explore (bottom-left block)

What you're testing for the future.

- **X-axis — Innovation Risk.** Right is less risk: the idea has been
  tested for desirability (do customers want it?), feasibility (can we
  build it?), viability (can it make money?), and adaptability (can it
  survive context change?). Left is unproven — pure hypothesis.
- **Y-axis — Expected Return.** Up means *if* this idea works, it
  matters. Down means it would be a small win at best.

A healthy Explore portfolio looks like a funnel — many cheap, small
bubbles on the left (early experiments), fewer larger ones in the
middle, a small number of well-tested bubbles approaching graduation on
the right.

![Potential Steps in the Exploration Journey — four quadrants based on expected return and innovation risk. Source: *The Invincible Company*](assets/pm-exploration-journey.png)

## The actions on each side

Strategyzer names a small vocabulary of **portfolio actions**. Use them
explicitly so a decision on the map produces a clear next step.

**Explore actions** — applied to one bubble at a time:

- **Ideate / Test** — design experiments to reduce one of the four
  innovation risks.
- **Invest** — give a bubble more resources to test faster or scale.
- **Pivot** — change the underlying business model when evidence
  contradicts a load-bearing assumption.
- **Kill** — retire the bubble when testing shows the assumption is
  wrong and no pivot saves it.
- **Transfer** — when innovation risk has been reduced enough, the
  bubble graduates from Explore into Exploit and becomes a new bet you
  scale.

**Exploit actions** — applied to one bubble at a time:

- **Improve** — squeeze more return out of a stable model.
- **Manage / Defend** — maintain the moat against rising disruption
  risk.
- **Reinvent** — when disruption risk crosses a threshold the moat can't
  hold, redesign the model itself (the bubble may move back into
  Explore).
- **Divest / Retire** — close down or sell a bubble that cannot be
  reinvented profitably.

## Movement, not position

The single most important habit: **track the bubbles over time**. A
Portfolio Map at one moment is a snapshot; the strategic information
lives in *direction* and *speed* of movement. Two questions to ask every
quarter:

1. Is anything in **Exploit** drifting left (disruption risk rising) and
   not getting a Manage / Defend / Reinvent decision? That's where the
   company will get hit.
2. Is anything in **Explore** stuck — same place for two reviews in a
   row? Stuck means under-tested. Either invest, pivot, or kill, but
   don't let it sit.

## Common failure modes

- **All-Exploit, no-Explore.** The classic incumbent mistake. The map
  has six big bubbles in the top-right and the bottom-left is empty.
  When today's cash cow declines, there is no next act. The Portfolio
  Map exists primarily to make this asymmetry visible.
- **All-Explore, no-Exploit.** The classic startup mistake. Lots of
  experiments, no scaled, profitable model. Burns until investors stop
  funding.
- **Bubbles that never move.** Either the team isn't running real
  experiments (Explore) or isn't tracking disruption signals (Exploit).
  Static maps are unread maps.

![The Innovation Journey — from chaotic exploration to clear exploitation. Source: *The Invincible Company*](assets/pm-innovation-journey.png)
- **Treating bubble size as ego.** Bubbles should encode something
  decision-relevant — invested capital, head-count, expected revenue —
  not "how much our team likes this idea."
- **Skipping the transfer rule.** If a bubble graduates from Explore but
  the operating org has nowhere to put it, exploration produces orphans.

## The "Invincible Company" thesis (one paragraph)

Strategyzer's argument is that the rare companies that endure across
multiple S-curves do **two** things at once: they squeeze every drop of
return out of today's businesses *while* systematically incubating the
businesses that will replace them. The Portfolio Map is the artefact
that forces both conversations onto the same page so neither one is
silently starved by the other.
