# Upmarket migration path (向上迁移路径)

The disruption story is incomplete without the trajectory. A foothold by itself is just a niche; what makes it disruption is that the entrant *moves upmarket* over time, improving along the mainstream dimension while preserving its foothold-side advantage. By the time the incumbent notices, the disruptor has crossed the mainstream "good enough" line and captured the customer relationship.

Without a credible upmarket migration path, you have a niche business, not a disruption.

## Core Questions

- What are the next 2-3 customer segments after the foothold? Name them specifically.
- What product capabilities does each next-step segment require? (Concrete features, performance levels, certifications.)
- How quickly can you improve along the mainstream dimension? Cite a performance-trajectory rate.
- When does your performance trajectory cross the mainstream "good enough" line for each next-step segment?
- Does each next-step migration preserve or erode your foothold advantage (price, simplicity, accessibility)?

## The performance-trajectory crossing

Christensen's chapter 9 framework: customers have a "good enough" line for performance on each dimension. Incumbents' performance trajectory often *exceeds* the mainstream's good-enough line (overshooting). The disruptor's trajectory starts below the good-enough line but rises. When it crosses, the disruptor becomes competitive in the mainstream — and the incumbent's overshoot becomes a liability.

## Examples

- **Nucor steel (1970-1995)**: "Rebar → bar → structural steel → flat-rolled. 25 years, one steel-grade migration per ~5 years. By 1995, mini-mills competed for premium flat-rolled, the heart of Bethlehem's business."
- **Transsion Africa (2010-2025)**: "Feature phones → entry Android → mid-tier Android → smart-feature hybrid for emerging mainstream. Now expanding to Latin America + South Asia with the same playbook."
- **Carvana (2013-2025)**: "Pre-2017 trade-in + entry-level used cars → certified pre-owned → near-new inventory → fleet-quality vehicles. Vending-machine + 7-day return preserved at every step."
- **Honda Supercub upmarket (1960-1985)**: "50cc → 90cc → 250cc → 500cc → CB750. Twenty-five years to reach Harley's mainstream. By the time Harley noticed, Honda owned the customer."

## How it flows into the canvas

The upmarket migration path becomes a `three-horizons-map` overlay (H1 = foothold, H2 = next-segment, H3 = mainstream challenge) and a `portfolio-map` pin trajectory. If the path is missing, the candidate should be reclassified as new-market entry or niche, not disruption.
