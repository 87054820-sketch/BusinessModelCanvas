# Worse-on-dimension (起步更差的维度)

This is the *load-bearing* test of the disruption label. If the disruptor is not initially inferior on any mainstream dimension, you do not have disruption — you have sustaining innovation, a new-market entry, or direct competition. All three are valid moves; none are disruption in the Christensen sense.

The point is to be *honest* about the inferiority. Mainstream customers, mainstream metrics, mainstream evaluations: where does the disruptor lose?

## Core Questions

- What is the *specific* mainstream dimension on which the disruptor is initially inferior? (Resolution, range, capacity, depth, latency, quality, brand prestige.)
- How much worse, in measurable terms? ("30% lower resolution", "5dB worse signal-to-noise", "$50K vs $200K specialist depth".)
- Does the mainstream customer notice the inferiority? (If not, this is not disruption — it's just better.)
- Is the gap shrinking, stable, or growing? (Disruption requires the gap to shrink over time as the disruptor moves upmarket.)
- Have you tested this with mainstream customers (e.g., would they choose the disruptor over incumbent today)?

## Examples

- **MP3 vs CD (1998)**: "MP3 player sound quality measurably below CD's 16-bit 44.1kHz fidelity at typical 128kbps bitrates."
- **Digital camera vs SLR (1995)**: "Early consumer digital cameras at 0.3 MP vs 35mm film's effective 20 MP."
- **Telehealth vs in-person specialist (2015)**: "Tele-consult limited to verbal symptom description + camera-visible inspection; cannot palpate, percuss, or order in-clinic tests."
- **Mini-mill steel vs integrated (1970)**: "Mini-mill rebar quality at 80% of Bethlehem Steel's, with 30% more inclusions and weaker tensile strength."

## How it flows into the canvas

Worse-on-dimension must be paired with Acceptable-on-dimension — the trade only makes sense if both sides are filled. If you cannot honestly write a real inferiority here, restart from Foothold customer; you probably do not have disruption.
