# Incumbent's rational reason to ignore (在位者理性忽视理由)

This is the analytical pivot of Christensen's theory: incumbents are not stupid; they are *rational*. Their resource-allocation process correctly says "ignore this foothold" — based on margin, market size, and cannibalisation risk. The disruption succeeds *because* the incumbent's customer interview correctly told it to look the other way.

If the incumbent both *can* and *will* respond, you are not disrupting — you are competing.

## Core Questions

- What gross margin does the incumbent make today on its core business? What gross margin would the disruption-target market offer?
- How small is the disruption market today, compared to the incumbent's smallest investable unit?
- Would responding to the disruption cannibalise the incumbent's current high-margin product? By how much?
- Does the incumbent's customer interview / sales pipeline currently mention the foothold? Or is the foothold genuinely invisible?
- How long can the incumbent rationally remain inactive — 12 months? 36 months? 60 months?

## The value network trap

Christensen's central concept: each firm operates inside a **value network** — customers, suppliers, capital markets, and internal metrics that all reward serving the current mainstream. The new technology starts in a low-end or non-consumption foothold with worse mainstream performance, smaller margins, and a smaller market. The firm's resource-allocation process correctly says "ignore it" — and is *right* in the short term. The trap is that the disruption's trajectory eventually crosses the mainstream "good enough" line, and by then the incumbent's customer relationships are gone.

## Examples

- **Bethlehem Steel ignoring Nucor (1970-1985)**: "Rebar margins were 5% vs Bethlehem's 25% on structural steel. The minimum investable unit was a $100M integrated mill; mini-mill rebar was a $5M sub-segment. Rationally not worth defending."
- **Department stores ignoring discount retailers (1970-1990)**: "Discounters had 23% margin vs department stores' 40%; matching discount pricing would destroy the showroom-and-service model. Rationally not worth defending."
- **Cable excavator makers ignoring hydraulic (1950s)**: "Hydraulic excavators had 1/3 the bucket capacity. Mainstream construction customers wanted larger buckets, not smaller. Rationally ignored — until residential sewer-line work created a new mainstream."

## How it flows into the canvas

This zone tests whether the disruption hypothesis is plausible. If you cannot write a *rational* reason for the incumbent to ignore, then the incumbent will respond — and you are competing. The strict-test note must explicitly grade: "Will the incumbent rationally remain inactive for ≥ N months? Y/N."
