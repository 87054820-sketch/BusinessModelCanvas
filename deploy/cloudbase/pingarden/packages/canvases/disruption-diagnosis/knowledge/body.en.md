# How to use

1. **State the candidate hypothesis.** "We believe [product / service] disrupts [incumbent / industry] from a [low-end / new-market] foothold." Be concrete about who the foothold customer is.
2. **Fill Foothold customer.** Overshot mainstream (low-end disruption) or non-consumer (new-market disruption)? Be specific about segment, geography, and occasion. If you can't name a real, identifiable foothold, this is not disruption.
3. **Fill Worse-on-dimension.** Name the *specific* mainstream dimension on which the disruptor is initially inferior. Quantify the gap. If the disruptor is better than incumbents on mainstream dimensions, this is sustaining innovation — stop.
4. **Fill Acceptable-on-dimension.** What does the foothold customer get instead that compensates for the inferiority? Price, simplicity, accessibility, convenience, context fit? Their willingness to trade is the foothold's load-bearing assumption.
5. **Fill Incumbent's rational reason to ignore.** Why will incumbents *correctly* tell themselves not to respond? Typical: thin margin, small market, cannibalisation risk against current high-margin business. If the incumbent both can and will respond, you are competing, not disrupting.
6. **Fill Upmarket migration path.** Concrete next-step customer segments and product milestones. Not "expand over time" — name segment B, segment C, and the product capabilities each requires.
7. **Fill Riskiest assumptions.** Pick from: foothold willingness, improvement trajectory, incumbent inattention duration, organisational insulation. Each must be falsifiable with a metric.
8. **Apply the strict-test note (orange).** Mark whether all 3 Christensen criteria hold. If any one fails, relabel honestly: sustaining / new-market entry / direct competition. All are valid moves — just not disruption.

# How to interpret the result

- **All 3 strict-test criteria pass.** You have a real disruption candidate. Open `portfolio-map` and place the bet deep in Explore; open `three-horizons-map` and position it as H3; insulate the unit organisationally from the parent's mature-business metrics.
- **Foothold passes, inferiority fails (you're already better).** Sustaining innovation. Use BMC + VPC to refine the value proposition; compete head-on with incumbent through superior product.
- **Foothold passes, upmarket migration unclear.** New-market entry but not disruption (yet). Stay in the foothold, grow there, defer the upmarket migration story until customer evidence allows.
- **No foothold (you're competing for the incumbent's current mainstream).** Direct competition. Use Five Forces and Value Chain to find your advantage; do not pretend the disruption playbook applies.
- **Riskiest assumption is "incumbent will not respond".** That assumption is the most fragile. Plan for the day they do respond, including the organisational insulation lever from Christensen ch. 5-6.

# Quality checklist

- Each zone has explicit specifics (segment, dimension, number, deadline), not abstractions.
- The strict-test note is filled — orange stickies grading each Christensen criterion against the candidate.
- Worse-on-dimension is honest. If you cannot write a real inferiority, you do not have disruption.
- Upmarket migration has at least 2 concrete next-step segments named.
- Riskiest assumptions are falsifiable, not aspirational.
- The candidate has been compared explicitly against Blue Ocean / sustaining / new-market entry — and disruption was chosen for stated reasons.

# Common mistakes

- Calling everything novel "disruptive". The Uber case is the canonical misuse; Christensen himself argued it is sustaining innovation in taxis.
- Confusing new technology with disruptive innovation. New technology applied to mainstream customers along existing dimensions is sustaining, no matter how novel.
- Skipping the foothold test — without an overshot or non-consumption customer, what you have is direct competition.
- Filling Upmarket Migration as wishful thinking; ground it in customer-segment evidence.
- Putting a disruptive bet under the parent company's mature-business margin metrics. Christensen's organisational prescription is insulation.
- Forgetting that incumbents may also respond by acquiring you. The strict test still applies, but the exit path is different.
