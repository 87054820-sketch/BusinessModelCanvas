# When to use the Disruption Diagnosis Canvas

Use this canvas when a team is about to call a candidate "disruptive" — to test whether the label actually fits Christensen's strict 3-part test (foothold + initial inferiority + upmarket trajectory), or whether it is really sustaining innovation, new-market entry, or direct competition. Getting the label wrong leads to wrong strategy: separating the venture from the parent when it shouldn't be, or vice versa.

## Reach for it when

- A team is launching a product that is *worse* than incumbents on conventional dimensions but better on a different one — to predict whether incumbents will rationally ignore it long enough for the foothold to grow.
- An incumbent is wondering whether to take a new entrant seriously — disruption theory says the deepest danger is the entrant the dominant customer interview *correctly* tells them to ignore.
- A team is choosing between sustaining and disruptive bets in its innovation portfolio — they require different metrics, different organisation, and different success criteria.
- A board is asked to fund a venture labelled "disruptive" — apply the strict test before committing to a separate-unit operating model and patience capital.

## Skip it when

- The new product is *better than incumbents on mainstream dimensions* — that is sustaining innovation. Use **BMC** + **Value Proposition Canvas** instead; do not waste the disruption frame on it.
- The team is exploring an *entirely uncontested market space* — use **Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas**; the disruption frame requires an incumbent.
- The candidate is a direct competitor offering the same value at a lower price with no foothold story — use **Porter's Five Forces** + **BMC**; disruption is not the right diagnosis.
- The question is "how to commercialise this proven new product" — disruption diagnosis is about *whether* the disruption hypothesis is true, not how to scale.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **Blue Ocean Strategy** creates new market space through value innovation (raise + reduce). Disruption *starts inferior* on mainstream dimensions. The two sometimes converge (new-market disruption can reshape the value curve) but the strict tests differ.
- **Ansoff Diversification** is a directional choice for a single business. Disruption is a competitive-dynamics theory about incumbent failure. A diversification move *might* be disruptive — pass the disruption test to find out.
- **Three Horizons** is the time-portfolio layer. Disruption diagnosis is the judgment applied to H3 candidates: is this H3 bet a sustaining better-mousetrap, a low-end disruption, or a new-market disruption?
- **Innovation Metrics** is the measurement layer for explore work. Disruption diagnosis tells you which assumptions to test first.

## From the book

> *"Disruptive technologies bring to a market a very different value proposition than had been available previously. Generally, disruptive technologies underperform established products in mainstream markets. But they have other features that a few fringe (and generally new) customers value. Products based on disruptive technologies are typically cheaper, simpler, smaller, and, frequently, more convenient to use."*
>
> — Clayton M. Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma*, Harvard Business School Press, 1997, p. xviii.

> *"In our experience, far too many people who speak of 'disruption' have not read a serious book or article on the subject. Too frequently, they use the term loosely to invoke the concept of innovation in support of whatever it is they wish to do."*
>
> — Christensen, Raynor & McDonald, *What Is Disruptive Innovation?*, Harvard Business Review, December 2015.

The 1997 book builds the theory inductively from the hard-disk-drive industry's 30-year failure pattern. The 2015 HBR article narrows the label — Uber, for instance, was argued *not* to be disruption in the strict sense. This canvas implements the strict test, not the loose usage.

## What good looks like

- Foothold customer is named with segment, geography, and occasion specificity (not "younger users").
- The worse-on-dimension zone names the specific mainstream dimension AND quantifies the gap (not "lower quality"; "30% lower resolution" or "8-bit colour vs 24-bit").
- Incumbent's rational reason to ignore is grounded in real margin or cannibalisation analysis, not "they were complacent".
- Upmarket migration path has at least 2 concrete next-step segments, not "expand later".
- Riskiest assumptions are falsifiable with a metric and a deadline.
- Strict-test note explicitly grades the candidate against Christensen's 3 criteria.
