# Innovation Culture Map

## Source and core definition

In *The Invincible Company*, the Culture Map is described as a practical, simple, and visual tool to understand, design, test, and manage the corporate culture an organization wants to bring to life. The book uses it to map and design innovation culture, so exploration culture and execution culture can live under the same roof.

The map has three core layers:

1. **Outcomes** — the concrete positive or negative consequences resulting from people’s behavior.
2. **Behaviors** — how individuals and teams act, speak, and interact; the repeated patterns you can observe.
3. **Enablers / Blockers** — the levers that make behaviors possible or prevent them. They include formal policies, processes, and reward systems, as well as informal rituals, leadership actions, meeting habits, and knowledge gaps.

## Culture is cultivated, not engineered

The book uses Dave Gray’s garden analogy: culture is not designed mechanically like a car; it is cultivated like a garden. Outcomes are the fruits. Behaviors are the heart of the culture. Enablers and blockers are like soil, water, fertilizer, and weather. Leaders cannot directly command a culture into existence, but they can create the conditions that make useful behaviors repeat.

## Recommended fill order

In practice, starting with **behaviors** is often easiest because behaviors are concrete. Do not write abstract judgments like “we do not innovate.” Write evidence, for example: “Last year we ran two growth-idea workshops, but nobody made time to explore the ideas after the workshop.”

1. **Map current behaviors** — observe neutrally like an anthropologist; capture positive and negative behaviors.
2. **Capture current outcomes** — what results did those behaviors produce? If new outcomes appear, add missing behaviors.
3. **Identify current enablers / blockers** — which processes, incentives, rituals, leadership behaviors, resource gates, fears, or knowledge gaps caused those behaviors?
4. **Design the desired culture** — define the outcomes you want to harvest, the behaviors required, and the mechanisms to build or remove.

## Relation to exploration culture

The book stresses that many companies already have a strong execution culture but lack exploration culture. Building exploration culture usually means working on three levers:

- **Leadership support** — whether leaders genuinely protect exploration and review evidence rather than theater.
- **Organizational design** — whether Explore projects have the right structure, funding, metrics, and transfer mechanism.
- **Innovation practice** — whether teams know how to state assumptions, run small tests, learn quickly, and honestly retire weak ideas.

## When to use it

- Use it when the issue is “not enough Explore projects move through the Portfolio Map.”
- Use it when the Experiment Canvas exists but experiments are not actually run.
- Use it when a BMC is designed but the organization cannot execute, protect, or scale it.

A good Culture Map does not contain value slogans. It contains observable behaviors and the mechanisms that shape them.
