# So I can (outcome / progress)

> When ___, I want to ___, so I can **___**.

The right-hand slot. The **progress** the customer is trying to make.

Christensen's word for it is "progress":

> *"Customers seek to execute jobs as fast as possible without any
> errors and without it going off track — quickly and accurately."*

The Make Us Proud team's refinement: **write outcomes as action
verbs**, not passive states. "Decide which vendor to call back"
beats "have a clear shortlist." Customers actively engage with their
progress; they don't passively wait to receive it.

## What goes here

- The **concrete progress** the customer would brag about getting
  done. Not a feeling vaguely, not a state vaguely — a definite
  *thing accomplished*.
- The **standard of done** they'd accept. "Cut the close from 10
  days to 3" beats "make the close faster."
- The verb that captures the customer **acting on** that progress —
  *deciding*, *finishing*, *handing off*, *defending*, *being seen
  as*.

## Verb-first examples

- "**Decide** which two of five vendors to call back." (vs. "have
  a shortlist")
- "**Walk into** the exec review with a one-page summary already
  done." (vs. "have a summary ready")
- "**Hand off** the weekly numbers in my CFO's words, with proof I
  can defend if challenged." (vs. "produce a weekly report")
- "**Be seen as** the PM whose product reviews are always on
  schedule." (social outcome — verb-first too)
- "**Stop dreading** Friday afternoons." (emotional outcome — also
  verb-first)

## Outcome ≠ feature

The strongest test of a JTBD outcome is: *would the customer brag
about this in their own words to a peer?*

| Feature-shaped (skip) | Outcome-shaped (keep) |
|---|---|
| "Real-time sync" | "Stop emailing 'is this the latest version?'" |
| "AI-generated summary" | "Walk into the meeting having already digested the deck" |
| "Custom dashboards" | "Spot the variance that matters before my CFO does" |

Customers don't pay for features; they pay for the progress those
features make possible.

## Failure modes

- **State-shaped outcomes.** "Have a shortlist", "be efficient",
  "be productive". Add the verb. *What does the customer DO with
  that state?*
- **Outcome same as motivation.** If your `So I can` sticky reads
  the same as the `I want to` sticky, the story is incomplete. The
  outcome is what the motivation **unlocks** — it sits one step
  beyond it.
- **Aggregating outcomes.** "So I can be successful" is the team
  ducking. Push for the specific brag the customer would make.

## Discovery questions

- *What would success look like? What would you tell a peer about it?*
- *What would change for you if this got done?*
- *What's the first thing you'd do once you have ___?*
  (forces the next-action verb out)
- *What would you stop having to do?* (surfaces emotional outcomes)
- *Whose praise — or relief — would you notice?* (surfaces social
  outcomes)
