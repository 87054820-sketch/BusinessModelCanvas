# When (situation / circumstance)

> When **___**, I want to ___, so I can ___.

The leftmost slot of every Job Story. Christensen's central insistence
is that **the circumstance is fundamental to defining the job**:

> *"A job represents the progress that a person is trying to make in
> a particular **circumstance**."*
> — Clayton Christensen, *Competing Against Luck*

Without a circumstance, you don't have a job — you have a feature
description.

## What goes here

- **The trigger.** What just happened to make this need urgent
  *now*? "My CFO just asked for the variance report by Monday" beats
  "reporting needs to happen."
- **Time and place.** Friday 4pm vs. Monday 9am vs. on a train.
  These shape what counts as "good enough."
- **Who else is in the room.** Alone with a laptop, on a call with
  three vendors, presenting to the exec team. The audience changes
  the job.
- **The constraint.** Time pressure, budget, a manager looking over
  the shoulder, low connectivity. The constraint explains the
  friction.

## Examples

- "Friday 4pm, my CFO just asked for next quarter's variance report
  by Monday."
- "Monday 9am stand-up, I open the dashboard and the export is
  empty for the third week running."
- "On a train with patchy 4G, a contract I haven't read needs
  approving before the next stop."
- "First evening of onboarding a new direct report, they ask why we
  use this tool and I realise I don't have a clean answer."

## Failure modes

- **Generic circumstances.** "When I'm working" is the team ducking.
  Push for an observed scene.
- **Inventing the trigger.** If no real customer was ever in this
  situation, this is hypothesis, not finding. Mark it `?` and verify
  in the next research cycle.
- **Mixing the circumstance with the motivation.** "When I want to
  compile the report" — that's already the motivation slot. The
  circumstance is what made compiling the report necessary *now*.

## Discovery questions

- *Tell me about the last time you ___.*
- *What was happening right before you decided you needed this?*
- *Where were you? Who else was there?*
- *What had you tried just before this?*

The first question — "tell me about the last time you ___" — is the
JTBD interview's workhorse. It anchors the response in a real
remembered moment, where the circumstance is concrete and observable.
