## When to use

Use the Strategy Canvas (W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne, **Blue Ocean Strategy**) to **plot competitive positioning visually** — your "value curve" against competitors' across the factors of competition in your industry. The goal: spot where everyone is bunched (a Red Ocean) and where a meaningfully **differently-shaped** curve could create uncontested space.

The four strategic moves the canvas drives are summarised as **ERRC**: **Eliminate / Reduce / Raise / Create**.

## When NOT to use

- For internal product roadmaps — too coarse. Strategy Canvas is for industry-level competitive positioning.
- Without competitor data. The whole point is contrast; a curve drawn alone is not a strategy.
- For an early-stage product before you have a competitive landscape — first map the landscape (e.g. via desk research), then plot.

## Fill order — landscape first, then yourself, then ERRC

This is a chart canvas: the X-axis lists factors of competition (xAxisItems), the Y-axis is offering level (0 = absent, 5 = dominant). Each competitor (and you) is a `pinClass`; each pin places that competitor at one X factor with a Y level. Connect the same colour pins → that's a value curve.

1. **List the factors of competition** (X axis). Start from what customers _actually compare_ — not what you wish they compared on. Look at trade press, customer reviews, win/loss interviews. The defaults (Price, Quality, Service, Speed, Brand, Innovation) are starter set; replace with industry-specific ones.
2. **Plot incumbents first.** The two or three biggest competitors. Score each at every factor. Note your evidence — published prices, NPS reports, feature comparisons.
3. **Plot any disruptors / niche players.** Their curves often hint at where the industry is moving.
4. **Plot yourself, honestly.** No optimistic scoring. If you'd score yourself a 4 on Service but customers score you 2, you're a 2.
5. **Look for the differentiated shape.** A profitable Blue Ocean curve has visibly DIFFERENT geometry — peaks where competitors dip, dips where competitors peak. If yours has the same shape as a competitor's, you're in their Red Ocean.
6. **Apply ERRC** to draft your future-state curve:
   - **Eliminate**: which factors should be DROPPED (currently played but unnecessary)?
   - **Reduce**: which should be played BELOW industry standard (over-served)?
   - **Raise**: which should be played ABOVE industry standard (under-served)?
   - **Create**: which factors are missing entirely from the industry that you could pioneer?

## Cross-block consistency

- **Every competitor's curve must be plotted on the SAME factors.** Don't add factors that only one competitor offers — those go in **Create**.
- **Score honestly relative to industry**, not absolute. A 5 means "best in industry on this factor", not "perfect."
- **Your future curve should have a different SHAPE, not just higher scores.** Outscoring everywhere is fantasy AND it's expensive — it usually means you're playing on their terms.
- The factor list should be 5–10. Fewer = oversimplified; more = the curves become unreadable.

## Anti-patterns — refuse to ship

- ❌ **Outscoring fantasy.** Curves where you beat every competitor on every factor. That's not strategy; it's an expense statement waiting to happen.
- ❌ **Listing factors only YOU offer.** Those don't belong on the main chart — they go in the **Create** quadrant of ERRC.
- ❌ **Plotting without competitor data.** You're guessing at where they are. Get desk research first.
- ❌ **Optimistic self-score.** Use customer-facing data — reviews, surveys, churn. Internal opinion is the wrong source.
- ❌ **Treating ERRC as a brainstorm.** ERRC is forced by the curve shape — apply it AFTER plotting, not before.
- ❌ **Skipping the "Eliminate" move.** Most teams find Raise + Create easy and never Eliminate. The cost reduction from Eliminate is what funds the Create.

## Tone

Each pin's annotation should cite the source ("from G2 review average", "from feature matrix in trade publication X"). The chart's value depends entirely on the legitimacy of the scores; opaque numbers undermine the whole exercise.
