# Reading the canvas

The Ad-Lib Value Proposition turns a value proposition into a fill-in-the-blank sentence:

> Our **[products and services]** help **[customer segment]** who want to **[job to be done]** by **[reducing/avoiding customer pain]** and **[increasing/enabling customer gain]**, unlike **[competing alternative]**.

The point is not polished copy. The point is forcing clarity on six things: what you offer, whom you serve, what job the customer wants to get done, which pain you reduce, which gain you create, and how you differ from the alternatives.

## The six parts

1. **Products and services** — what the customer can actually buy, use, or experience.
2. **Customer segment** — one specific group, not "everyone".
3. **Job to be done** — the functional, social, or emotional job the customer hires a solution to do.
4. **Pain relief** — a verb that explains how you reduce, remove, or avoid a pain that matters.
5. **Gain creation** — a verb that explains how you increase, enable, or accelerate a gain the customer wants.
6. **Competing value proposition** — the alternative customers use today: a direct competitor, workaround, internal process, or doing nothing.

## How to use it

1. Pull material from the Value Proposition Canvas or customer discovery. Do not invent marketing language from scratch.
2. Use one customer segment and one primary job at a time.
3. Draft at least 3–5 versions by changing the segment, job, pain, gain, or competing alternative.
4. Compare versions: is it specific, important, differentiated, and testable?
5. Take the strongest one or two into interviews, landing pages, sales scripts, or small experiments.

## What a strong sentence does

- **Clear**: understandable in five seconds, without jargon or hype.
- **Specific**: the customer, job, pain, and gain can be observed or validated.
- **Focused**: it serves one segment instead of trying to cover everyone.
- **Comparative**: it names what customers use today and why this is different.
- **Testable**: it can become interview questions, experiment hypotheses, or willingness-to-pay tests.

## Relationship to the Value Proposition Canvas

The Value Proposition Canvas expands the logic: do the customer's Jobs / Pains / Gains match the Products & Services / Pain Relievers / Gain Creators? The Ad-Lib compresses that logic into one statement that a team, customer, or market can quickly understand.

If the Ad-Lib is hard to write, the issue is usually not wording. It means the customer, job, pain, gain, or differentiation is still unclear in the canvas.

## Common mistakes

- Writing the customer segment as a broad label like "business users".
- Writing the job as "needs a tool" instead of the result the customer wants.
- Mentioning only gains, which removes urgency.
- Mentioning only pains, which makes the proposition feel negative or incomplete.
- Forgetting the competing alternative, so customers have no reason to switch.
