# Salesforce CRM: making enterprise CRM accessible as a service

## Context and tension

Enterprise CRM used to mean expensive licenses, servers, consultants, long implementation cycles, and IT-led projects. Large enterprises could absorb that burden; many sales teams could not. The pain was not the idea of CRM but the delivery model.

This is why the case belongs in the Blue Ocean Strategy library. The company did not merely improve within the old rules. It changed which buyers mattered, which factors deserved investment, and which inherited assumptions could be removed. A newcomer should read the case as a market-reconstruction story rather than a normal product-success story.

## The strategic move

Salesforce moved CRM into the browser and sold it by subscription. It reduced installation and upgrade complexity, raised speed of adoption, and created a cloud delivery model that let business teams start without owning infrastructure.

The strategic move combines differentiation with a cost or complexity break. The company raises a few factors that matter strongly to overlooked buyers, while reducing or eliminating factors that incumbents treat as mandatory. That asymmetry is what makes the move teachable: the curve is not higher everywhere; it is meaningfully different.

## Then read the Strategy Canvas

::canvas[blue-ocean-strategy-canvas]{}

The Strategy Canvas should be read as a shape. The red-ocean baseline scores higher on legacy complexity and expertise-heavy factors. Salesforce CRM scores higher on access, usability, personalized value, cost efficiency, or new-demand creation, depending on the case. That shape is the strategic argument. If the company tried to score high on every traditional factor and every new factor, it would likely recreate the cost structure of the old industry.

## ERRC logic

- **Eliminate:** customer-owned CRM servers and painful version upgrades.
- **Reduce:** upfront license cost, implementation time, and IT bottlenecks.
- **Raise:** speed of deployment, accessibility, and continuous improvement.
- **Create:** enterprise SaaS CRM as an operating model and category.

## Risks and limits

The model depends on trust in cloud security, uptime, and integration. As SaaS became mainstream, competition intensified and the blue ocean became a crowded cloud-software category.

The case therefore should not be copied mechanically. The transferable insight is the discipline of fit: the offer, channel, operations, revenue model, and cost model must all support the same value curve.

## Transfer lesson

When customers value an outcome but reject the implementation burden, a delivery-model innovation can create new demand without changing the core job.
