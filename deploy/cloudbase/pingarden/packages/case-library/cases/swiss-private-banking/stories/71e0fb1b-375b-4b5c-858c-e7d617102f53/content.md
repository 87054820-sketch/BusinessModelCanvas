## The premise

Hagel & Singer (1999) argued that most large companies are actually three businesses tangled into one:

- **Customer relationship management** — built on scope economies and high cost of customer acquisition.
- **Product innovation** — built on speed and time-to-market.
- **Infrastructure management** — built on scale and unit-cost reduction.

These three are governed by *different* economic logics. Forced under one roof, they undermine each other: the speed-driven product team pulls advisors into selling, the scale-driven infrastructure team forces standardisation onto the relationship team, the relationship team makes long-cycle promises that the product team's quarterly cadence can't honour.

Osterwalder's *Business Model Generation* uses Swiss private banking as the canonical illustration of this tension and of two paths through it.

## The archetype

For most of the 20th century, a Swiss private bank was a **single integrated entity** that did all three jobs in-house. Outsourcing was costly. Secrecy concerns kept everything internal. The brand was 'Swiss banking' itself — opaque, discreet, vertically owned.

The archetype canvas captures this tangle: yellow stickies (relationship), green (product innovation), blue (infrastructure) all share the same Key Activities, Key Resources, and Cost Structure. The trade-offs are real but invisible to the outside.

## Path A: Unbundle (Maerki Baumann)

In the 2000s, the environment shifted. Specialty providers emerged: **transaction banks** focused exclusively on transactions, **financial-product boutiques** focused only on product design. Outsourcing became cheap and reliable.

Maerki Baumann, a Zurich-based bank, **spun off its transaction platform** as Incore Bank — which now sells transaction services to other banks too — and **stopped designing its own products**, instead curating from external boutiques. What remained is a tightly-focused relationship-business: a small team of senior advisors managing wealthy families, free of the cost burden and conflicts of the other two businesses.

The result: a much smaller balance sheet, no internal product-pressure conflicts, and a sharper value proposition ("independent advice"). The trade-off is gone because the trade-off-prone businesses are gone.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="maerki-baumann-en"}

## Path B: Stay integrated (Pictet)

Pictet, the largest Swiss private bank, took the opposite path. They kept all three businesses but learned to **explicitly manage the trade-offs**. The pink stickies on their canvas mark the conscious trade-off costs: compliance firewalls between advice and product divisions, HR practices that handle relationship-talent and platform-talent very differently, careful client communication about which products are Pictet-branded and which are not.

This is harder to execute but gives Pictet something Maerki Baumann doesn't have: **end-to-end control over the client experience**, including discretion (in-house transaction platform) and proprietary product IP (Pictet-branded structures with a 200-year track record).

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="pictet-en"}

## What it teaches

Unbundling is **not** automatically the right move. It's a strategic choice between two coherent positions:

- **Specialise** (Maerki Baumann): be the best at one of the three businesses. Lower cost, sharper VP, smaller addressable market.
- **Integrate consciously** (Pictet): own all three, but invest heavily in trade-off management — firewalls, distinct HR tracks, clear external positioning.

The failure mode is integrated-by-default: a bank that hasn't unbundled but also hasn't built the trade-off-management muscle. That's the archetype canvas — most institutions sit there until they choose.
