# Nespresso 1986-2014: bait-and-hook in luxury packaging

Nestlé filed the original Nespresso patent in 1976. Internal R&D took until 1986 to commercialise — and then the product nearly died. Through the 1980s and most of the 1990s Nespresso was a struggling office-coffee experiment that lost money quietly inside Nestlé. *Business Model Generation (BMG 2010)* notes (pp. 100-103) that the bait-and-hook pattern took Nespresso *almost twenty years* to start making money — Anderson cites this as the canonical example of how slow these models can be to bootstrap.

What changed in the 2000s wasn't the BMC structure (it was bait-and-hook from day one) — it was the *positioning*. Joachim Schoettli, who took over Nespresso in 2000, pushed the company out of office-coffee competition with traditional vending machines and into a luxury-brand consumer product positioned around boutique stores, premium aesthetics, and George Clooney's 'Nespresso. What else?' campaign starting 2006. The same machines, the same capsules, the same patents — but a different brand identity made it work.

## Two BMCs because the asymmetry IS the model

*(BMG 2010)* uses Nespresso alongside Gillette as the bait-and-hook paradigm. Like Gillette, Nespresso is taught with two BMCs because **mashing the bait and the hook into one canvas hides the cross-subsidy that makes the model work**.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="nespresso-machines-bait"}

The machine BMC reads strangely until you see the third sticky in `Revenue Streams`: **REAL revenue is from capsule business (cross-side)**. Nespresso's machine margin is low (10-20%, sometimes negative for promotional bundles in hotels and airlines). The interesting design is that Nespresso doesn't manufacture all its machines directly — it licenses Krups, De'Longhi, and Magimix to manufacture under Nespresso specifications, taking an OEM royalty. That's a clever move: the machine business operates at low margin OR breakeven, but the OEMs are the ones absorbing the manufacturing capital cost. Nespresso captures value at the patent + brand layer, not the manufacturing layer.

The pink sticky in `Key Activities` — *Patent enforcement against compatible-capsule makers* — is the structural defense, identical to Gillette's patent-vs-generic-blade fight. The key resource is *Patented capsule-machine interface*. Lose that patent and the entire bait-and-hook collapses.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="nespresso-capsules-hook"}

The capsule BMC is where the money is — reported gross margins above 80%. The blue sticky in `Customer Segments` (*Existing Nespresso machine owners*) and the cross-side dependency throughout `Key Resources` and `Key Partners` make the connection legible: capsules only sell because machines were distributed first.

Notice the deliberate `Channels` design: **(Deliberately NOT in supermarkets — price control)**. Nespresso fought a 30-year battle to keep capsule distribution out of mass retail and inside its own boutiques + Nespresso Club. The reason: in supermarkets, retailers price-discount capsules to drive footfall, and the brand's premium positioning collapses. The boutique + members-only club is the *price-discipline mechanism* that makes 80% gross margin sustainable.

## What goes wrong (the Original-line patent expiry)

*(Anderson 2009)* warns that bait-and-hook businesses are particularly vulnerable to **cheap-substitute attack** when the lock-in technology becomes legally accessible. Nespresso's Original-line capsule patents started expiring around 2012-2014. Within months, dozens of compatible third-party capsules launched (Mondelez's Tassimo competitor, Sara Lee's L'OR EspressO, Migros + Coop branded capsules, Aldi private-label, plus startup brands). By 2015 the compatible-capsule market was eating into Nespresso's gross margins — third-party capsules at 40-50% lower price points took 15-25% volume share in some markets.

Nestlé's response was the **Vertuo line, launched 2014**. New capsule geometry (the dome with barcode for centrifugal extraction), fresh patents extending to ~2034. Vertuo machines and capsules can't be used with Original system — and vice versa. This is the bait-and-hook pattern's textbook counter-attack: when the patent treadmill catches up, change the geometry, restart the patent clock, and migrate consumers to the new platform.

Fifteen years after Vertuo's launch, Nespresso runs both ranges in parallel. Original is the legacy + entry-level (and where compatible competitors live); Vertuo is the premium + protected line. Nestlé reports Nespresso revenue in the CHF 6-7B range annually. The pattern survived patent expiry by re-patenting.

## Why this case sits next to Gillette

Nespresso is the modern-luxury reading of the bait-and-hook pattern, alongside Gillette (the urtype) and HP printers (the cited descendant in Free pattern):

- **`gillette`** — the 1901 urtype. Razor as bait (often given away), blades as hook. The mechanism Nespresso copied.
- **`nespresso`** — the modern luxury-brand reading. Premium boutique aesthetics + capsule subscription mechanics. Same bait-and-hook structure under a CHF 200 espresso machine and CHF 0.70 capsules.
- HP printers (mentioned in the Free pattern but no dedicated case) — the same mechanism in the office-equipment category. Printers cheap, ink cartridges notoriously expensive.

Reading Nespresso alongside Gillette makes one thing clear: **the bait-and-hook pattern works regardless of category, but the BMC's asymmetry between the two sides is constant**. Bait side: low margin, customer acquisition. Hook side: high margin, recurring. The pattern label is what unifies them; the categories — razors, capsules, ink — are the implementation.
