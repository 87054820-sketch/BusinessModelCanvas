# InnoCentive: outside-in open innovation, sold as a service

In 2001, Eli Lilly's CIO at the time, Alpheus Bingham, ran an internal experiment: post real R&D problems publicly, anonymously, with cash prizes for the best solutions. The experiment worked well enough that Lilly spun it out as InnoCentive in 2005 to serve other companies. P&G — already running its Connect & Develop programme — became the anchor enterprise customer. By the 2010s InnoCentive was running challenges for Eli Lilly, P&G, GSK, NASA, the Rockefeller Foundation, the US Department of Defense, and dozens more. *Business Model Generation (BMG 2010)* uses InnoCentive (sidebar p. 114) to make the point that **the connector platform that runs open-innovation as-a-service is itself a multi-sided platform**.

## One BMC because the structure is the platform

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="innocentive-bmc"}

Unlike the P&G and GSK cases (which use two BMCs because the *boundary direction* is the model), InnoCentive uses one BMC because the *platform structure* is the model. The key visual:

- **Customer Segments** has FIVE entries split between two halves: three seeker types (yellow — F500 R&D departments, government/NGO, foundations/academia) and two solver types (blue — PhD-level scientists, amateur cross-disciplinary problem-solvers). The split into yellow/blue makes the multi-sided structure legible at a glance.
- **Value Propositions** has parallel halves: 3 seeker-side props (yellow), 3 solver-side props (blue). Read them together and you see why both sides show up — and how the asymmetry works (seekers pay; solvers don't pay, but earn through prizes).
- **Revenue Streams** has the canonical MSP zero-side sticky in blue: *(Solver-side: $0 — classic MSP zero-side)*. That's literally what makes this a multi-sided platform — one side is priced at zero (or negative, since solvers earn) and the other side carries the entire revenue.
- **Cost Structure** has the cold-start risk sticky in pink — *Cold-start risk during early years (chicken-egg)*. Without enough solvers, seekers don't post; without enough seekers, solvers don't sign up. The Eli Lilly anchor + the P&G anchor were the structural fix.

## Why this case carries two pattern tags

InnoCentive is tagged with both `multi-sided-platforms` *and* `open-business-models / outside-in`. Each tag tells you something different:

- **`multi-sided-platforms`** describes the *structure*. Seekers + solvers + platform-as-matchmaker is mechanically the same shape as Visa (cardholders + merchants + issuers + network) or Google (searchers + advertisers + content sites). The MSP failure modes apply: cold-start risk, chicken-egg dynamics, network-effect lock-in once you're winning.
- **`open-business-models / outside-in`** describes the *strategy* the platform serves. InnoCentive isn't just *an* MSP — it's specifically the MSP that companies use to run their outside-in open-innovation programmes. P&G's Connect & Develop programme uses InnoCentive as one of its three bridges. GSK uses it for selective NTD challenges. NASA uses it for tournament-style technology challenges.

The two tags together capture why InnoCentive sits at the structural overlap of two patterns. Tagging it as MSP only would lose the strategic context (this platform exists *because* big firms want outside-in capability and need the search-cost reduction). Tagging it as Open only would lose the structure (the platform side IS multi-sided — the unit-economic logic comes from MSP, not from Open).

## What InnoCentive actually solved

A small sample of public InnoCentive wins:

- **NASA solar storm forecasting (2009)**. NASA had been working internally on better solar-storm prediction for decades; an InnoCentive challenge brought in a retired radio-frequency engineer who solved it in 3 months for a $30K prize. The problem and the solution both required deep domain expertise — but expertise that wasn't *inside NASA*.
- **Oil Spill Recovery Institute (2007-2010)**. A series of challenges to clean up frozen Alaskan oil spills more efficiently. A Chicago-based concrete engineer (no oil-industry background) won by adapting a vibration technique used in concrete pouring.
- **Prize4Life ALS biomarker (2011)**. A $1M prize for ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease) biomarker discovery; won by an Israeli team applying methods from electrical engineering to neuroscience.

What unifies these: the solution required a *cross-disciplinary* match that internal R&D, by definition, was structurally bad at finding. The platform's value isn't generic crowdsourcing — it's the specific search-cost reduction *(Chesbrough 2006)* names as outside-in's structural barrier.

## Why this case sits next to P&G and GSK

P&G is the *outside-in* paradigm of Open run from inside one company. GSK is the *inside-out* paradigm. InnoCentive is the connector platform that sells outside-in capability *as a service* to many seekers. Reading them together makes the structure of Open Business Models legible:

- Outside-in *inside one firm* (P&G Connect & Develop): firm builds its own bridges + integration team
- Outside-in *as a service* (InnoCentive): platform aggregates seekers + solvers; firm rents the bridge
- Inside-out (GSK Patent Pools): idle internal IP exposed to outside, captured via license + reputation
- Bilateral (mature firms): all three patterns running in parallel
