# How the Wii won by serving the side PS3 ignored

In November 2006 three new game consoles launched within weeks of each other: Microsoft's Xbox 360 (already a year old by then), Sony's PlayStation 3 ($599 launch price, sold below cost, cutting-edge Cell processor + Blu-ray), and Nintendo's Wii ($250 launch price, off-the-shelf parts, no HD output, motion controls). Industry analysts wrote off the Wii. By 2008 the Wii had outsold the PS3 by a factor of two and was on track to become a top-3 best-selling console of all time. The reason isn't 'casual gaming was a fad' — it's that Nintendo had read the multi-sided platform pattern correctly and Sony had not.

## The two BMCs make the choice legible

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="wii-gamers"}

For gamers, Nintendo did three things at once: (1) addressed an audience PS3/360 ignored — older and family / casual gamers — with motion controls and Wii Sports; (2) priced the hardware at half of PS3 by skipping HD and using off-the-shelf parts; (3) leaned hard on first-party IP (Mario, Zelda) the platform owned outright. Crucially, Nintendo's hardware was *actually profitable per unit* — not a loss-leader like the PS3 — even at $250. The subsidy wasn't 'lose money on hardware'; it was 'price the hardware where the casual / family side will show up'.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="wii-developers"}

For third-party game developers, the Wii's value proposition was different from PS3/360: a 100M-user base that the other consoles couldn't reach, lower dev cost (modest hardware specs), and motion controls as a brand new gameplay category. In return, developers paid Nintendo a per-disc royalty (~$8-10) plus dev-kit license fees. *That royalty stream was Nintendo's hidden gold* — the money side that funded Nintendo's choice to keep gamer hardware profitable rather than charge gamers PS3 prices.

The contrast with PS3 is sharp. Sony chose to (1) charge gamers $599 for cutting-edge hardware (the gamer side as money side); (2) ship Blu-ray drives at a hardware loss (subsidising hardcore gamers willing to pay PS3 prices for AAA games); (3) lean on third-party AAA developers who needed the cutting-edge specs. Sony was effectively running a one-sided business — selling premium hardware to hardcore gamers — *while pretending to run a multi-sided platform*. PS3's third-party developer side stayed small in the early years because the hardware was hard to develop for and the user base wasn't large enough yet to justify it.

## What Nintendo got right that PS3 got wrong

*(Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne 2006)* tells you to pick the **subsidy side** carefully — the side that's most price-sensitive *and* the side the other side wants more of. Nintendo correctly identified that the casual / family audience was the side they could uniquely serve (because PS3 and Xbox 360 weren't trying to) and the side game developers wanted access to (because nobody else was serving them). Sony's mistake was assuming the two sides were the same hardcore gamer they had served on PS2.

The Wii's economics held until ~2011, when the hardware aged out and the audience moved to smartphones. By then Nintendo had captured most of the value the platform was going to capture. The Wii U (2012) reverted to charging premium prices and never recovered the audience. Nintendo Switch (2017) re-applied the same MSP playbook — a less powerful, cheaper console serving an audience that PS5/Xbox One ignored — and reached the same scale.

## Why this case sits next to Google in the library

Google is the digital, data-driven 3-sided MSP. The Wii is the physical, hardware-anchored 2-sided MSP. Reading them side by side is the cleanest way to see what's universal in the pattern (asymmetric pricing across sides, careful subsidy-side selection, cross-side network effects) and what changes with medium (digital MSPs scale faster but face envelopment risk; hardware MSPs scale slower but lock in physical distribution and IP). When the user asks 'is this a multi-sided platform?', having both shapes in the library makes the answer concrete.
