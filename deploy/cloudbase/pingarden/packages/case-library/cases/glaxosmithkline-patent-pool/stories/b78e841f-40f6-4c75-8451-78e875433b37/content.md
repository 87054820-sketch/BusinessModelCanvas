# GSK Patent Pools: inside-out where the strategic capture isn't license fees

In February 2009, Andrew Witty — six months into his job as GSK's CEO — gave a speech at Harvard Medical School announcing that GSK would pool patents relevant to neglected tropical diseases (NTDs) and open them to external researchers. The pharma industry treated this as either heretical or a PR move. *Business Model Generation (BMG 2010)* used it (pp. 112-113) as the inside-out paradigm case for Open Business Models. With 15 years of hindsight, the BMG framing is the right one.

## Why pooling NTD patents was rational, not charitable

Big Pharma R&D produces lots of compounds with potential activity against neglected diseases — malaria, leishmaniasis, schistosomiasis, Chagas disease, sleeping sickness — diseases that affect about a billion people worldwide and almost none of them in OECD markets. The compounds get screened, sometimes get to early-stage data, and then get *abandoned*. Not because they don't work, but because **there is no profitable market**: GSK's pricing infrastructure assumes patients can pay $100+/dose; NTD-affected populations cannot.

These compounds sit in the IP portfolio generating zero revenue. A patent costs roughly $50K to file and maintain over its 20-year life — so idle IP isn't free, it's a small persistent drain. *(Chesbrough 2006)* names this exact pattern as the inside-out opportunity: assets the firm will never commercialise, accumulating carrying cost, where outside parties might extract real value if the IP were available.

## Two BMCs because the *boundary direction* IS the model

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="gsk-traditional-pharma"}

The traditional Big Pharma BMC reads like every blockbuster-drug company. Internal scientists discover compounds; clinical trials prove them; regulators approve them; sales reps sell them. The pink stickies in `Customer Segments` and `Revenue Streams` mark what's *missing*: poor-country patients aren't served; idle-IP licensing revenue is zero — IP is defended, not licensed. The sticky in `Key Resources` — *(Idle NTD-relevant compounds — economic dead weight)* — names the asset that's about to move.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{variant="gsk-patent-pool"}

The patent-pool BMC keeps almost all the left-side blocks (Key Resources, Key Activities, Cost Structure) the same — inside-out isn't about changing how you do R&D, it's about changing what you do with the R&D that didn't pay off. The transformation lives in `Revenue Streams`, `Customer Segments`, and especially `Key Partners`.

The blue "cross-firm dependency" stickies in `Key Partners` are the new structure: WIPO (the consortium operator), Pfizer / Novartis / other pharma in the Re:Search consortium, academic NTD researchers, NGOs like DNDi (Drugs for Neglected Diseases initiative) and MMV (Medicines for Malaria Venture). None of these existed as Key Partners on the closed BMC. The pool *only works* if external licensees show up — that's what "cross-firm dependency" captures.

## What the strategic capture actually is

*(BMG 2010)* makes the sharp argument that license-fee revenue is the *obvious* capture but not the *important* one. The strategic capture is three things:

- **Reputation in emerging markets.** GSK's blockbuster business depends on getting drug approvals in countries it doesn't sell to today but might in 10-20 years (India, Brazil, Indonesia). The patent pool builds reputation and goodwill with regulators in those countries that the closed model couldn't.
- **Follow-on R&D leads.** External researchers using pooled IP sometimes find activity GSK's screens missed. Several pool licensees have published findings on compounds in the pool that re-opened internal R&D programmes.
- **Cannibalization fear DOES NOT actually materialize.** This is the big quiet finding. The compounds in the pool are genuinely idle; opening them up did not create competitors to GSK's commercial business. Note the pink sticky in `Key Activities` — *Cannibalization-fear management (internal)* — that fear was the dominant internal objection, and it turned out to be overstated for assets that were *actually* idle.

## What goes wrong (and what GSK learned)

*(Chesbrough 2006)* warns that inside-out breaks down in three places, all of which GSK navigated:

- **Cannibalization fear (overstated for idle IP).** Internal voices argue licensing-out IP creates a future competitor. For genuinely idle assets — defined as assets not commercialised internally for 5+ years with no roadmap to commercialisation — the fear is structurally overstated. The discipline is being honest about what's actually idle vs. what's nominally on the strategic roadmap but never moving. GSK's 800-patent pool was the result of that disciplined assessment.
- **Underpriced licensing on first deals.** Without licensing benchmarks, first deals tend to be one-shot fees with no royalty or equity. The pink sticky in `Revenue Streams` — *(Underpriced licensing risk on first deals)* — was real. GSK's later pool deals (post-2013) included royalty triggers that the earliest 2010-2012 deals didn't.
- **Strategic-vs-financial capture confusion.** If you measure inside-out only in license-fee revenue, the programme looks small (probably <$50M/yr at GSK). That's the wrong measurement. The right measurement is reputation + access + internal-R&D leads + idle-IP carrying cost reduction. Andrew Witty's defense of the programme to GSK's board, throughout his tenure, was always strategic, never financial.

## Why this case sits next to P&G and InnoCentive

GSK Patent Pools is the *inside-out* paradigm of Open. P&G Connect & Develop is the *outside-in* paradigm. InnoCentive is the connector platform that runs outside-in *as a service* for many seekers. Reading them together makes Open's two-sub-type structure legible and forces the right question when a user proposes a new "open" business: **which direction is value flowing across the firm boundary?**

- *External ideas / IP / talent flowing IN to be used internally* → outside-in (P&G, GE Ecomagination, LEGO Ideas).
- *Idle internal IP flowing OUT to be used externally* → inside-out (GSK, IBM patent licensing, Tesla open patents).
- *Both directions in significant volume* → bilateral; most mature open-innovation firms (GSK, P&G, IBM) actually do both.
