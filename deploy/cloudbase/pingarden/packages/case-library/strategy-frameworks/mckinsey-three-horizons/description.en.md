# McKinsey Three Horizons

McKinsey Three Horizons is a growth-portfolio framework for managing the present and future at the same time. It separates **Horizon 1** core businesses, **Horizon 2** emerging growth engines, and **Horizon 3** future options so leaders can see whether the company is over-optimizing today or under-investing in tomorrow.

## When to use

Use it when the decision is about **growth sequencing**: how much to fund the core, which emerging businesses deserve scale investment, and which future options should keep learning. It is especially useful for corporate innovation portfolios, group-level strategy, and companies facing S-curve renewal.

Do not use it to design a single business model, classify market share, or rank features. Use BMC for one model, BCG Growth-Share Matrix for market growth/share, and Portfolio Map for risk/return position.

## How it maps to PinGarden canvases

- `three-horizons-map` is the working canvas. H1/H2/H3 become editable zones, while migration actions and evidence risks make movement explicit.
- `portfolio-map` shows the same items by risk and return. Use it when you need Explore/Exploit positioning.
- `business-model-canvas` expands any important horizon item into a complete model.
- `experiment-canvas` turns H2/H3 assumptions into tests.
- `evidence-scorecard` decides whether a bet has enough evidence to move, fund, pivot, pause, or retire.

## Workflow

1. Define the portfolio unit: business line, product, platform, geography, or venture.
2. Place H1 items that generate today’s cash, margin, data, channels, or capabilities.
3. Place H2 items that have early evidence but are not yet reliable core businesses.
4. Place H3 options that are mostly assumptions and learning bets.
5. For every H2/H3 item, name the evidence needed to move to the next horizon.
6. Add migration actions: fund, scale, transfer, protect, pivot, pause, or retire.
7. Route unresolved assumptions to experiments and scorecards before making funding decisions.

## How to interpret results

A healthy map has a strong H1 to fund the company, credible H2 bets that can become the next growth engine, and enough H3 options to keep future renewal alive. The most important signal is **movement**: H3 options should either learn and move toward H2 or be retired; H2 bets should either scale toward H1 or change course.

## What to notice in examples

- `ping-an-group` shows a financial-services core funding platform and technology options.
- `bosch-accelerator` shows the H3/H2 learning side: many ideas enter, evidence filters them, a few move forward.
- `alibaba-group` shows multiple growth engines around commerce, logistics, cloud, and platform infrastructure.
- `procter-gamble-cd` shows how external innovation and internal scaling can connect future options to operational businesses.

## Common misuses

- Treating H1/H2/H3 as fixed calendar years instead of maturity horizons.
- Filling H3 with fashionable technologies without customer or business-model hypotheses.
- Calling a project H2 when it has no repeatable evidence.
- Letting H1 metrics starve H2 and H3 investments.
- Forgetting the organizational handoff from venture teams to operating teams.
- Drawing the map once and never tracking movement.
