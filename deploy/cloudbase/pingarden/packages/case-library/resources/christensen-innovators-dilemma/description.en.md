# Reading map — The Innovator's Dilemma

Christensen's 1997 book is the *empirical* and *theoretical* foundation of disruptive innovation. Read it not as a business-press classic but as a piece of detective work: chapter 1 builds the entire theory from the hard-disk-drive industry's 30-year failure pattern, and the rest of the book defends and extends that induction to mechanical excavators, steel mini-mills, discount retail, and motorcycles.

## What problem it solves

- Why do well-managed companies, that listen to their best customers and invest in the most promising R&D, *rationally* fail when a new technology arrives?
- Why does "listen to your customer" become the wrong advice at the wrong moment?
- How can a successful firm avoid the trap, without abandoning the customers it serves today?

Christensen's answer: **value networks**. Each firm operates inside a network of customers, suppliers, capital markets, and metrics that all reward serving the current mainstream. A new technology that starts in a low-end or non-consumption foothold has worse performance on the mainstream dimensions, smaller margins, and a smaller market — so the firm's resource-allocation process correctly says "ignore it". The disruption succeeds *because* the incumbent's customer interview correctly told them to look the other way.

## Contents / reading map

**Part 1 — Why Great Companies Can Fail**

1. *How Can Great Firms Fail? Insights from the Hard Disk Drive Industry* — the empirical engine. 1965-1995, 5 generations of disk drives (14" → 8" → 5.25" → 3.5" → 2.5"), and the brutal pattern: the leader of each generation *never* won the next.
2. *Value Networks and the Impetus to Innovate* — the conceptual key. Why incumbents' rationality leads them into the trap.
3. *Disruptive Technological Change in the Mechanical Excavator Industry* — second proof point: hydraulic excavators disrupting cable-actuated giants.
4. *What Goes Up, Can't Go Down* — the asymmetry between moving upmarket (easy, profitable) and downmarket (hard, unattractive).

**Part 2 — Managing Disruptive Technological Change**

5. *Give Responsibility for Disruptive Technologies to Organizations Whose Customers Need Them* — the organisational prescription: separate units, separate metrics.
6. *Match the Size of the Organization to the Size of the Market* — why $50M markets cannot survive inside $5B P&Ls.
7. *Discovering New and Emerging Markets* — the Honda Supercub story (50cc motorcycle disrupting Harley-Davidson from non-consumption).
8. *How to Appraise Your Organization's Capabilities and Disabilities* — resources / processes / values (RPV) framework.
9. *Performance Provided, Market Demand, and the Product Life Cycle* — the steel mini-mill story: rebar → bar → structural → flat-rolled.
10. *Managing Disruptive Technological Change: A Case Study* — applying the lessons to electric vehicles.
11. *The Dilemmas of Innovation: A Summary* — closing synthesis.

## What to read first

- **If you have a candidate disruption to diagnose**: chapter 1 (the pattern) + chapter 7 (foothold discovery) + chapter 9 (performance trajectories). Then open the disruption-diagnosis canvas and apply the strict test.
- **If you're an incumbent worried about a new entrant**: chapter 2 (value networks) + chapter 5-6 (organisational prescription). The deepest danger is the entrant your dominant customer interview *correctly* tells you to ignore.
- **If you're sizing a corporate-innovation unit**: chapter 6 (match the size of the organisation to the size of the market) is the operational rulebook.
- **If you're teaching disruption to a team**: chapter 1 alone, followed by Nucor in chapter 9. Two examples, one theory.

## Key terminology

- **Sustaining innovation** — incremental or radical improvement *along dimensions valued by current mainstream customers*. Incumbents nearly always win.
- **Disruptive innovation** — initially-inferior on mainstream dimensions, but acceptable on a different dimension to a foothold customer the incumbent overshoots or doesn't serve.
- **Low-end disruption** — foothold is overshot mainstream customer; entrant moves upmarket.
- **New-market disruption** — foothold is a non-consumer the incumbent never served; entrant creates a new market that later overlaps with the mainstream.
- **Value network** — the customers, suppliers, capital markets, and metrics that shape what a firm sees and decides. Incumbents are not stupid; they are network-trapped.
- **Performance trajectory** — the rate at which technology improves along a dimension. Disruption succeeds when the disruptor's trajectory crosses the customer's "good enough" line.

## Caveats

- Christensen himself, in HBR December 2015 ("What Is Disruptive Innovation?"), **narrowed** the label and excluded many things commonly called disruption — Uber being the most-cited example. He argued Uber is a sustaining innovation in taxis, not disruption in the strict sense. Read both the book and the 2015 article before applying the label.
- The book is dense; the theory is not. Most of the value is in chapters 1, 2, 5-7, and 9. Skim the rest unless you're studying the field.
- The case studies are dated (1990s). The theory is not. Use the strict 3-part test, not the specific industry analogies, to judge new candidates.
