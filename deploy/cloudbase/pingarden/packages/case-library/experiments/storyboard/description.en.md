# Storyboard

A simple 6-12 panel narrative — sketches with one-line captions — walking a target customer through their day **with** your idea. Show, don't tell. Useful when the value proposition is hard to explain in abstract bullets but obvious once seen in context.

## When to use

- Value proposition is contextual ("ambient AI assistant", "background sync") and hard to verbalise.
- Early — no clickable prototype, no smoke test, just the *narrative* of the experience.
- Different customers might see different value paths in the same product; storyboards force you to commit to one.
- You need to align a team or a stakeholder on what the experience actually is, not what it could be.

Don't use:

- Solution-led teams who'll over-design the storyboard. Crayons-on-napkin is the right fidelity.
- When you actually have a working prototype — go to Clickable Prototype or Wizard of Oz instead.

## How to run

1. **6-12 panels.** Beginning (problem moment), middle (your product enters), end (resolution + emotion).
2. **Stick figures + captions.** Pretty drawings imply commitment; you want the user reacting to the idea, not the art.
3. **Show 5-8 target customers, one at a time.** Walk through panel-by-panel.
4. **Stop after panel 4 and ask "what happens next?"** Their guess is data.
5. **Probe the moment they react** — surprise, confusion, "that's me", "that's not me". Note exactly which panel triggered it.
6. **Iterate after every 2-3 sessions.** The storyboard you finish with should look different than the one you started with.

## What good looks like

- Customers can recount the storyboard in their own words after a single viewing.
- 4-of-6 react with "that's exactly my Tuesday" or similar self-recognition.
- At least one panel made several customers ask "wait, but what about X?" — that X is a missing pain or constraint to add to your VPC.
- The team can converge on one experience description after the round.

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ Pretty illustrations. Implies polish, makes feedback timid.
- ❌ Reading the storyboard *to* the user. They should drive the pace and questions.
- ❌ Over-narrating each panel. One sentence per panel max.
- ❌ Treating "I love this" as validation — it's compliance to a story you authored.
