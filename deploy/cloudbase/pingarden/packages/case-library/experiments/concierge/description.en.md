# Concierge

You deliver the value to a small set of paying customers entirely **by hand**. No frontend, no automation, often not even a website — direct relationship, manual fulfillment, real money changing hands. The strongest cheap-ish Validation experiment because it surfaces all three risks at once: desirability (they pay), viability (the price holds), feasibility (you can deliver the outcome).

## When to use

- The riskiest unknown is whether the *outcome* is valuable enough that someone pays for it — independent of your specific tech.
- Smoke Test passed but you suspect signups won't convert to retention.
- You're early enough that even 5 paying customers is meaningful.
- Domain expertise sits in your head; productising it is the question, not validating it.

Don't use:

- Product premise depends on automation that humans genuinely can't replicate (real-time, scale, deterministic).
- You can't legally or contractually deliver manually (regulated medical, financial, etc.).
- Team has no domain depth — Concierge requires you to actually be good at the job, not just simulate it.

## How to run

1. **Pick 3-7 customers.** Higher than that, you can't sustain manual delivery.
2. **Charge them.** Even a small fee makes the data valid; free Concierges don't validate viability.
3. **Define the outcome contract upfront.** "We will deliver X by Y date." This is what you're being paid for, not the means.
4. **Deliver. Iterate. Track time + complaints + repeat purchase.** Every cycle teaches you what you must automate vs what you can leave manual.
5. **Run for 4-8 weeks.** Anything less and you don't see retention or referral.
6. **Calculate would-be unit economics.** If automated 80% of the way, can the price/cost make sense? That's the Viability gate.

## What good looks like

- 3-7 paying customers, multiple cycles each.
- Price held — no significant pushback, ideally with one or two "can I get more?".
- Referrals appear unprompted by week 4-6 — the strongest desirability + viability signal there is.
- You can name the 2-3 manual steps that, if automated, would make the unit economics work.

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ Free Concierge. Doesn't validate viability; people use anything free.
- ❌ Scaling to 30 customers because demand looks good. Operationally collapses, learnings drown.
- ❌ Treating Concierge as a permanent business. It is the cheapest *test*; the next move is targeted automation, not "hire 50 people".
- ❌ Skipping unit-economics math. Concierge can feel successful while being structurally unprofitable forever.
