# Letter of Intent (LOI)

A signed, non-binding document from a target B2B buyer stating "if you build a product matching these conditions, we commit to purchase at this price." The strongest viability signal you can get in B2B before writing code, because it surfaces the actual buyer, the actual budget, and the actual procurement constraints.

## When to use

- B2B with deal sizes that justify a real procurement conversation (typically ≥ $5k ACV).
- Discovery + Smoke Test surfaced demand; the next gate is "will procurement actually buy?".
- You need to commit engineering for 3-12 months and want signed proof beyond verbal interest.
- Decision is between two candidate ICPs — LOIs from one but not the other settles it.

Don't use:

- B2C — buyers don't sign LOIs for $20/mo subscriptions; use Pre-Sale instead.
- Deals < $5k ACV — the procurement overhead exceeds the data value; use Smoke Test + Concierge instead.
- You have no plausible path to building the product within the LOI's stated timeframe — burning trust on undeliverable promises poisons future deals.

## How to run

1. **Identify 8-15 ICP-fit accounts.** Get warm intros to economic buyers (not just users).
2. **Map the buying committee.** User, champion, economic buyer, procurement, legal — all need to converge for a real deal.
3. **Draft a 1-page LOI.** Specifies: product description (3 sentences), success criteria (which problem solved, how measured), target price, target delivery date, conditions ("subject to product matching demo specs"). Explicitly non-binding so legal pushback is minimal.
4. **Pitch the economic buyer.** This is the conversation that surfaces real budget vs vapor budget.
5. **Iterate the offer.** Pricing, scope, timing — multiple no's tell you what version of the product would clear procurement.
6. **Track close rate per stage.** Pitched → engaged → LOI signed. The drop-off shape tells you where the real friction is (interest vs budget vs procurement).

## What good looks like

- 3+ signed LOIs from real economic buyers at ICP-fit accounts.
- The signed offers cluster around a coherent product description (not 7 different products to 7 buyers).
- At least one LOI has a date the buyer would be embarrassed to miss — "yes by Q3" is a real signal; "someday" isn't.
- The aggregate ACV × number of LOIs covers the build cost (the "math works" sanity check).

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ Pitching only users / champions, not economic buyers. Champions sign nothing; they advocate.
- ❌ Treating an LOI as a sale. It's not contractually binding; ~30-50% will walk when the real contract appears, even with strong intent.
- ❌ Vague conditions in the LOI. If it says "we'd consider X", that's a yes you'll never collect on.
- ❌ Running it before Discovery. Without interview/forum-grounded language, your pitch will mismatch the buyer's framing and you'll get polite no's.
