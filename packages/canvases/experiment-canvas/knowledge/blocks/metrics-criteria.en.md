# Metrics & Criteria

Define how the result will be judged before running the experiment. This combines Open Practice Library's Results and Litmus Test: what counts as success or failure, and what data must be collected.

## How to write

- Metrics must directly answer the hypothesis.
- Success, failure, and inconclusive thresholds should be defined in advance.
- Prefer numbers: conversion rate, bookings, willingness to pay, completion time, satisfaction score.

## Examples

- “Booking conversion ≥ 8% validates the test”
- “Fewer than 2 trial commitments invalidates the assumption”

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer defines success, failure, and inconclusive thresholds before the experiment starts. A weak answer collects whatever data is convenient and decides afterward whether the result "feels promising."

## Common Pitfalls

Do not choose vanity metrics that cannot change the decision. Also avoid thresholds so low that any result looks like success. If the sample is tiny, state what the result can and cannot prove.

## Sticky Examples

- "Success: at least 12 qualified founders book a pricing call from 150 targeted visits."
- "Failure: fewer than 3 signed LOIs after 20 qualified sales conversations."

## Transfer Questions

- What decision will this metric change?
- What result would make us stop or pivot?
- What evidence would still be too weak to justify scale?

## Source Grounding

Testing Business Ideas emphasizes explicit evidence and experiment design before running tests. The Open Practice Library Results/Litmus Test pattern keeps teams from interpreting noisy data after the fact.
