# Next Steps

Use the conclusion to decide whether to persevere, pivot, or redo the experiment. Avoid “keep watching”; define the action, owner, and timing.

## Three moves

- **Persevere**: the assumption is validated; expand sample, run the next experiment, or begin delivery.
- **Pivot**: the assumption is invalidated; change customer, value proposition, channel, pricing, or solution.
- **Redo**: the result is unclear; improve design, expand sample, or change measurement.

## Examples

- “Persevere: expand sample to 200 target customers, owned by growth lead.”
- “Pivot: test a lower-priced entry offer; landing page due next Friday.”

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good next step converts evidence into a decision with owner, deadline, and changed behavior. A weak answer says "continue learning" or "monitor results" without changing the business model, value proposition, channel, or experiment design.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not persevere because the team likes the idea; persevere because the threshold was met. Do not pivot everything at once; change the assumption that failed. Do not redo an experiment unless you know why the original evidence was inconclusive.

## Sticky Examples

- "Persevere: test willingness to pay at $49 with the same segment; owner: PM, by Aug 15."
- "Pivot: keep the job, change channel from ads to partner referrals; next test: 30 outreach messages."

## Transfer Questions

- What exact decision did the evidence support?
- Which part of the canvas changes now?
- Who owns the next test or implementation step?

## Source Grounding

Testing Business Ideas links experiments to learning loops. The next step should reduce uncertainty, not simply document that an experiment happened.
