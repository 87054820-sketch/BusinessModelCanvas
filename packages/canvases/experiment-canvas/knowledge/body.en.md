# Reading the canvas

The Experiment Canvas operationalises Bland & Osterwalder's TBI loop: **Hypothesize → Experiment → Learn → Decide**. Define the assumption (and which of D/F/V it belongs to), design the experiment and metrics, then record results and decide what to do next.

## Recommended order

1. **Riskiest Assumption**: which D/F/V risk is this? If it's wrong, does the idea fail?
2. **Falsifiable Hypothesis**: TBI's template — "We believe X. To verify, we will Y. And measure Z. We're right if [pre-set threshold]."
3. **Experiment Setup**: pick a method from the **Experiment Library** (skill: `experiments/<slug>.{en,zh}.md`) — Customer Interview / Smoke Test / Wizard of Oz / Concierge / Letter of Intent / Pre-Sale and 6 more. Match on theme (Discovery vs Validation), risk (D/F/V), and cost band.
4. **Metrics & Criteria**: define success / failure / inconclusive thresholds **before** running.
5. **Results & Conclusion**: record real evidence and decide validated, invalidated, or unclear.
6. **Next Steps**: persevere, pivot, or redesign the experiment.

## Strength of evidence — TBI's rules of thumb

A result earns "validated" status to the degree it meets these criteria. Weak experiments (e.g. interviews) Discover, they don't Validate.

- **Action > opinion.** A signed letter of intent beats "I'd buy this." A click beats a survey rating.
- **N ≥ 5 minimum, ≥ 12 better.** Below 5, "they all said X" is noise.
- **Quantitative + qualitative > one alone.** Numbers without verbatims hide why it worked / failed.
- **Live tests > recalled.** Watching someone in the moment beats asking them what they'd do.
- **Threshold pre-set > post-hoc.** "20% conversion is actually pretty good" — invalidates the test.

## What good looks like

- One experiment tests one critical assumption, not everything at once.
- The hypothesis can be proven wrong and has a timeframe plus measurable outcome.
- Metrics are defined before the experiment, not adjusted afterward.
- The sample is sufficient, or the qualitative evidence is clear enough.
- Every next step has an owner and a due date.
