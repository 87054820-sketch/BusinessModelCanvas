# Margin

The difference between the value the customer pays and the total cost of every activity in the chain. The question: where does margin actually come from — cost discipline, differentiation premium, or both?

## Examples

- "Premium pricing supported by Service + brand; 38% gross margin on hero SKUs."
- "Cost leadership from Operations scale; 12% margin sustained by 3× competitor volume."
- "Mixed: brand premium on flagship line (45% GM), cost discipline on long-tail (15% GM)."

## Flow

Margin synthesises the whole chain. If margin is shrinking, walk back: is a cost-advantage activity eroding, is a differentiation activity being copied, or is a linkage leaking? The margin zone is also the truth-test for "stuck in the middle" — firms claiming both cost and differentiation often have neither in the margin numbers.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer connects margin to named activities and linkages. A weak answer only reports the margin percentage without explaining why the business can keep it.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not treat margin as a finance afterthought. In Porter's logic, margin is the outcome of the entire activity system. If the activity explanation cannot account for the margin, the value chain story is incomplete.

## Transfer Questions

- Which activity most protects margin?
- Which cost increase or copied differentiator would hit margin first?
- What BMC change would protect the margin source?

## Source Grounding

Porter's value chain ends in margin because competitive advantage must show up as cost position, differentiation premium, or both. The margin block is the output test for the activity analysis.
