# How to use

1. **Define the unit of analysis.** One business, one BMC. Not a whole conglomerate. Industry boundary, customer segment, and time window must be explicit.
2. **Fill primary activities left-to-right.** Inbound Logistics → Operations → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service. Inside each, name 2-4 concrete sub-activities (not "operations" — "high-mix flexible assembly with weekly schedule changes").
3. **Fill support activities top-down.** Firm Infrastructure → HRM → Technology Development → Procurement. These look "boring" but are usually the most defensible — they enable everything below.
4. **Mark each activity by colour.** Yellow = cost-advantage activity. Blue = differentiation-advantage activity. An activity can be both (rare but real).
5. **Find the linkages.** Use purple stickies between activities that reinforce each other (e.g., Procurement quality → Operations consistency) or fight each other (e.g., Marketing promising 24h service when Service can only deliver 48h).
6. **Flag the leaks.** Pink stickies on activities where cost is rising, differentiation is eroding, or capability is below the bar the strategy requires.
7. **Pick the 2-3 load-bearing differentiators.** Capability investment concentrates here. Mark with green stickies.
8. **Translate to BMC.** Every advantage-bearing activity must land on a Key Activity, Key Resource, or Key Partner sticky in the BMC. Activities not on the BMC are not yet decisions.

# How to interpret

- **Cost-advantage dominates.** The firm wins through scale, standardisation, or process discipline. Defend it by improving the cost frontier (automation, capacity, supplier consolidation). Beware: a sustained price war erodes margin without strengthening the moat.
- **Differentiation-advantage dominates.** The firm wins through quality, service, or brand. Defend it by keeping the differentiating activities ahead of competitors and ensuring the customer continues to value the difference enough to pay the premium.
- **Mixed (both colours on multiple activities).** Possible but rare. Patagonia is an example — responsible sourcing is both cost discipline AND brand differentiator. Most attempts at "stuck in the middle" fail; verify the linkages actually compound.
- **Lots of pink leaks.** The strategy is described, not executed. Capability investment is the next-quarter agenda.
- **Linkages concentrate in support activities.** The moat is organisational. HRM and Firm Infrastructure are doing the load-bearing work. Hardest to replicate.

# Quality checklist

- Every activity has 2-4 concrete sub-activities, not just a label.
- At least 2 linkages (purple) are explicit — most real differentiators are linkage-based.
- Support activities are filled, not skipped because they "feel boring".
- For platform / service businesses, Inbound and Outbound Logistics may be near-empty — that is correct; Technology Development and Procurement of capacity dominate instead.
- Every load-bearing activity (yellow or blue) is mapped to a BMC block via a follow-on sticky or note.
- Margin zone names where money actually comes from — cost discipline, differentiation premium, or both.

# Common mistakes

- Drawing the 9 boxes as a generic process map with no advantage colouring.
- Treating each activity in isolation; skipping linkages.
- Skipping support activities because they feel less interesting than operations.
- For a service or platform business, pretending physical Inbound and Outbound Logistics still matter at meaningful scale.
- Leaving the analysis on the canvas without translating to BMC KA / KR / KP.
- Confusing "capability" with "current performance" — the team may be good at Operations today but unable to scale by 10×.
