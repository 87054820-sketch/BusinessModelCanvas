A concrete, named frustration the customer experiences today. Prioritize pains that are intense, frequent, and strong enough to affect choice or switching behavior. Make it observable and ideally measurable.

## Module Purpose

This slot gives urgency to the proposition. It should name an obstacle, risk, bad outcome, or friction that customers already recognize, not a problem invented by the team.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer is specific enough to validate: "three hours reconciling denied claims every Friday." A weak answer says "inefficiency", "lack of visibility", or "friction" without naming the experienced consequence.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not list every pain. Pick the pain that most influences choice, switching, willingness to pay, or adoption. Do not write a pain that only matters to the seller.

## Sticky Examples

- "Late carrier updates force dispatchers to call customers manually."
- "Finance teams cannot explain variance before the executive meeting."

## Transfer Questions

- How often does this pain happen?
- What does it cost in time, money, risk, status, or emotion?
- What workaround proves the pain already matters?

## Source Grounding

The Pain section in Value Proposition Design distinguishes severe, frequent pains from minor annoyances. This module keeps the Ad-Lib testable by naming the pain at evidence level.
