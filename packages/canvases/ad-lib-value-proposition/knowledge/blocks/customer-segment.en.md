The one specific customer segment this sentence targets. Do not say "businesses" or "users" — be precise about industry, size, role, situation, or behavior. Different segments should produce different ad-libs.

## Module Purpose

This slot prevents the sentence from becoming generic. A value proposition is only strong for a named customer context, so write the segment narrowly enough that its job, pain, and gain can be observed.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer combines role, situation, and context: "operations managers at 20-200 person logistics firms onboarding new carriers." A weak answer says "SMBs", "business users", or "everyone who needs productivity."

## Common Pitfalls

Do not mix buyers, users, and beneficiaries unless they are the same person. If a buyer signs the contract but a frontline user feels the pain, write separate ad-libs or state whose value is being tested.

## Sticky Examples

- "Independent pharmacy owners managing insurance claim denials."
- "Product managers at B2B SaaS firms preparing quarterly roadmap trade-offs."

## Transfer Questions

- Who feels the pain often enough to care?
- Who chooses or pays for the solution?
- Would another segment describe the job in different words?

## Source Grounding

Business Model Generation and Value Proposition Design both start from Customer Segments. This module keeps the Ad-Lib aligned with that source logic: no segment clarity, no value-proposition clarity.
