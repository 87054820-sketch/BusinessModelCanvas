Name what customers use today: a direct competitor, workaround, internal process, or doing nothing. This contrast creates differentiation and explains why customers should switch.

## Module Purpose

This slot makes the proposition comparative. Customers always have an alternative, even if it is a spreadsheet, a manual process, or postponing the decision.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer names the customer's real current behavior and why it is insufficient. A weak answer only names a rival brand or says "no competition", which hides switching friction.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not invent a weak competitor just to make the sentence flattering. If the real alternative is "do nothing", explain why that is rational today and what must change for customers to switch.

## Sticky Examples

- "unlike chasing updates across email, spreadsheets, and dispatch calls."
- "unlike hiring an agency for a one-off market scan."

## Transfer Questions

- What would the customer do if your offer did not exist?
- What cost, risk, or habit keeps the current alternative in place?
- What proof would convince them that switching is worth it?

## Source Grounding

Business Model Generation and Value Proposition Design both emphasize fit against actual customer behavior. The competing alternative keeps the Ad-Lib from becoming a one-sided claim.
