Name the concrete product or service the customer can buy, use, or experience. Specific beats abstract — "hosted PostgreSQL with point-in-time recovery" beats "data infrastructure" — and works better in a one-sentence proposition.

## Module Purpose

This slot anchors the sentence in something the customer can actually choose. It should come from the Product & Services side of the Value Proposition Canvas, not from a vague capability statement.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer names the offer at the level a customer recognizes and can compare. A weak answer names an internal technology, platform capability, or broad category that customers cannot buy directly.

## Common Pitfalls

Avoid product laundry lists. If the offer contains multiple services, choose the one that carries the proposition for this customer/job pair. Also avoid brand slogans; this slot is about the offer, not the promise.

## Sticky Examples

- "Managed invoice collection service for small clinics."
- "Point-in-time database recovery for fintech engineering teams."

## Transfer Questions

- What exactly can the customer buy, use, or experience tomorrow?
- Which offer element creates the strongest fit with this job, pain, or gain?
- If a customer compared alternatives, what category would they place this offer in?

## Source Grounding

Value Proposition Design separates products and services from pain relievers and gain creators. The Ad-Lib compresses that same structure into one sentence, so the offer must stay concrete before the sentence claims value.
