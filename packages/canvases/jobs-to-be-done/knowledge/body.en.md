# How to fill it out

The canvas is filled in two passes. First, write **complete Job
Stories** by reading left-to-right across the top three columns —
each row of stickies (one in `When`, one in `I want to`, one in `So
I can`) is a single Job Story. Then, for each functional Job Story
above, **pair it with at least one emotional and one social
companion job** in the bottom row.

## The canonical Job Story

> When **[circumstance]**, I want to **[motivation / action]**, so
> I can **[outcome / progress]**.

| Slot | Question it answers | Christensen's name |
|---|---|---|
| **When** | What circumstance triggers the need now? | The **circumstance**, "fundamental to defining the job" |
| **I want to** | What does the customer want to do? | The **functional objective** (action verb) |
| **So I can** | What progress is the customer making? | The **outcome** / progress sought |

A canonical example from the Make Us Proud article:

> When an important new customer signs up, I want to be notified, so
> I can start a conversation with them.

Read that sentence. The trigger (a sign-up), the action (be
notified), and the outcome (start the conversation) are all named.
A feature request like "add a Slack integration" omits all three.

## The fill order

1. **Name the executor** — *who* is the customer hiring (in this
   canvas, the implicit subject of every "I"). Write them at the top
   of the canvas in a sticky or in the canvas title. One executor
   per canvas.
2. **Pick a real circumstance** — anchor the leftmost column in
   something **observed**, not invented. "Friday 4pm before exec
   review" beats "when reporting is needed."
3. **Write the motivation as a verb-first sticky** — "compile this
   report" is a verb; "reporting" is a topic. Verbs travel further.
4. **Write the outcome as a verb-first sticky too** — Make Us Proud's
   refinement: outcomes start with action verbs ("decide", "feel
   confident", "be seen as"). Customers actively engage with
   progress; they don't passively wait for it.
5. **Pair every functional Job Story with emotional + social
   companions** — bottom row. If you can't name the emotional layer,
   you don't yet understand the customer.

## The functional / emotional / social trichotomy

Every job has all three layers:

- **Functional** — the practical thing the customer wants done.
  *"Compile this quarter's variance report."*
- **Emotional** — how they want to *feel* while or after doing it.
  *"Feel calm during close week instead of dreading it."*
- **Social** — how they want to *be perceived* by others.
  *"Be seen by my CFO as someone who actually owns the numbers."*

The top three columns will mostly hold functional jobs. The bottom
row is where the emotional and social jobs live. The discipline:
**every functional Job Story above needs at least one emotional and
one social companion below**. If yours doesn't, you haven't done the
work yet.

> *People don't just buy products, they buy experiences and the way
> those experiences make them feel.*
> — productsos.substack, *Why Emotional and Social Jobs Are Critical*

The article's banking example: a redesign that surfaced account
balance perfectly (functional ✓) failed because users wanted **to
feel reassured** their money was safe (emotional ✗). The functional
job was solved; the customer still churned.

## Quality habits

- **Feed the canvas with research.** Every sticky should trace to a
  real customer observation — interview transcripts, support tickets,
  session recordings. JTBD without research is fan fiction.
- **Anchor in the circumstance.** A Job Story without a *when* is a
  feature. Christensen's whole point is that the circumstance is
  what shapes "progress."
- **Verb-first in motivation and outcome.** Topics ("reporting",
  "communication") hide what the customer is actually doing.
- **One executor per canvas.** A second customer segment gets a
  second canvas, not extra stickies.
- **Color-code by layer.** Use the project's sticky-colour legend to
  distinguish functional / emotional / social. The header strip
  suggests one palette, but any consistent mapping works — the goal
  is that you can read coverage at a glance.

## Common failure modes

- **Solution-shaped motivations.** "A Slack integration" is an
  answer. The motivation behind it is "stay notified about important
  events without context-switching."
- **Skipping emotional and social.** The most common failure mode.
  Symptom: you ship a feature that "works" and customers shrug.
- **Inventing the circumstance.** If no observed customer was ever in
  this circumstance, the Job Story is hypothesis. Mark it with a `?`
  and re-verify.
- **Generic Job Stories.** "When I'm working, I want to be
  productive, so I can succeed." Re-write with specifics: who is the
  customer, what just triggered the need, what verb captures the
  motivation, what concrete progress would they brag about?

## What to do with a finished canvas

- **Pair with the Value Proposition Canvas** — every Job Story
  becomes a Customer Job in VPC; emotional jobs especially feed VPC's
  Pains; outcomes feed VPC's Gains.
- **Pair with the Empathy Map** — the empathy map gives you depth on
  the customer; the Job Stories distil that depth into hireable
  units.
- **Pair with the Customer Journey** — each stage of a journey hosts
  its own Job Story. Stage-specific motivations and outcomes are
  often the highest-leverage roadmap inputs.
- **Pair with Ad-Lib Value Proposition** — the Job Stories give you
  the raw material for the ad-lib's *circumstance* and *outcome*
  fields.
