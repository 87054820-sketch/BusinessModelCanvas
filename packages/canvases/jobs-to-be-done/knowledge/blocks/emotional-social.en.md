# Emotional + Social jobs

The bottom row. For every functional Job Story above, name **at least
one emotional and one social companion job**. Without them, you have
half the picture.

## Why this row exists

Customers don't just buy products to get a thing done — they buy
them for how it makes them *feel* and how others *see* them.
Skipping these layers is the most common failure mode in JTBD
practice. The cautionary tale from
[*Why Emotional and Social Jobs Are Critical*](https://productsos.substack.com/p/why-emotional-and-social-jobs-are):

> A bank redesigned its app to surface account balance more clearly
> — solving the **functional** job. But customers wanted to **feel
> reassured** their money was safe (an emotional job the design
> didn't address) and the redesign failed.
>
> *"People don't just buy products, they buy experiences and the
> way those experiences make them feel."*

The functional job was solved. Customers still churned.

## What goes here

- **Emotional jobs** — what the customer wants to *feel* while or
  after using the product. Or what they want to *stop* feeling.
  - "Feel calm during close week instead of dreading it."
  - "Feel reassured the numbers are right without re-checking."
  - "Stop being anxious about a deadline I can't influence."
- **Social jobs** — how the customer wants to *be perceived* by
  others. Status, credibility, identity.
  - "Be seen as the PM whose product reviews are always on schedule."
  - "Be the team member who actually understands what the numbers
    mean."
  - "Stop being the person who has to apologise for a missed update."

## Coverage check

For each functional Job Story in the top columns, ask:

1. **Emotional layer** — what feeling drove the customer here?
   Or: what feeling are they hoping to escape?
2. **Social layer** — whose opinion of them is in play? Manager,
   teammate, customer, family?

If you can name neither, you don't yet understand the job. Schedule
another interview.

## Failure modes

- **Skipping the row entirely.** Symptom: you ship a feature that
  technically works and customers shrug. The functional job was
  done; the emotional reassurance or social status they actually
  paid for wasn't.
- **Treating "user experience" as a substitute.** Saying "we focus
  on UX" is not the same as naming the specific emotion or social
  perception this customer is chasing. The latter is testable.
- **Inventing without evidence.** Emotional jobs especially are
  easy to project. If a real customer didn't articulate or display
  this feeling, mark the sticky `?` and verify.

## Discovery questions

For emotional jobs:

- *Was there a feeling you were chasing here, or trying to avoid?*
- *What would it feel like if this were done? What would it feel
  like if it weren't?*
- *Tell me about a time you were stressed about this. What was that
  like?*

For social jobs:

- *Whose opinion of you is in play here?*
- *Who would notice if you nailed this? Who would notice if you
  didn't?*
- *What do you tell people when you talk about your work? What do
  you wish you could tell them?*

## How this row feeds downstream

- Emotional pains are the **strongest** Pains in the
  Value Proposition Canvas — they tend to drive switching.
- Social aspirations are often the most powerful **Gains** — they
  drive advocacy and word-of-mouth.
- Together, the bottom row gives the marketing team the **language**
  they'll use in the value proposition. The functional job tells
  you what the product does; the emotional + social jobs tell you
  why anyone will care.
