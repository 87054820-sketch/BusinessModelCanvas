# I want to (motivation / action)

> When ___, I want to **___**, so I can ___.

The middle slot. The **action** the customer wants to take in that
circumstance. Lead with a verb.

## Verb-first, always

A motivation that starts with a verb travels much further than one
that starts with a noun:

| Topic-shaped (weak) | Verb-first (strong) |
|---|---|
| "Reporting" | "Compile this quarter's variance report" |
| "Communication" | "Hand off the weekly summary to my boss in their words" |
| "Collaboration" | "Pull two teammates into a fast decision before stand-up" |

Topics hide what the customer is actually doing. Verbs surface it.

## Functional, emotional, or social — name which

Christensen's framework says every job has all three layers, and so
does every motivation:

- **Functional** — *"compile this report."*
- **Emotional** — *"not feel blindsided in the exec meeting."*
- **Social** — *"look prepared in front of my CFO."*

A strong Job Story names the layer it's at. If you only ever write
functional motivations, you're missing the half of the job that
explains *why customers actually buy.* (See `body.md` for the
banking-migration cautionary tale.)

## Examples

- "Cut my reporting prep from 6 hours to under 1." (functional)
- "Stop dreading every Friday afternoon." (emotional)
- "Look prepared in front of my CFO without overexplaining." (social)
- "Spot the variance that matters before anyone asks about it."
  (functional, but with implicit emotional reassurance underneath)

## Failure modes

- **Solution-shaped motivations.** "A Slack integration" is an
  answer to a hidden motivation, not the motivation itself. Push
  back: "*so what does that let you do?*" The real motivation is
  usually two questions deeper.
- **Noun-only stickies.** "Notifications", "Dashboards", "Reports".
  Add the verb the customer wants to do *with* that thing.
- **Mixing the motivation with the outcome.** "I want to decide
  which vendor to call back" — that's already a *progress* statement
  for the third column. The motivation is the action: *"compare
  three shortlisted vendors."*

## Discovery questions

- *In that situation, what did you try to do?*
- *Walk me through what you actually did, step by step.*
- *Did you want to be doing something different — and what stopped you?*
- *Was there a feeling you were chasing here, or trying to avoid?*
  (surfaces the emotional layer)
- *Was there someone whose opinion you were thinking about?*
  (surfaces the social layer)
