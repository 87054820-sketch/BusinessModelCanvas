# When to use the JTBD Story canvas

The Job Story is a one-line statement of **what your customer is
hiring you for**, in a specific circumstance:

> When ___, I want to ___, so I can ___.

The framework comes from Clayton Christensen's *Competing Against
Luck* (Jobs To Be Done theory) and the *Job Story* refinement
popularised by the Make Us Proud team. Christensen's central claim is
that "your customers are not buying your products, they are hiring
them to get a job done" — and that "a job represents the progress
that a person is trying to make in a particular **circumstance**."
The circumstance is not optional. Without it, you have a feature
list, not a job.

The canvas has three top columns for the canonical sentence and a
fourth wide row underneath for the **emotional** and **social** jobs
that always sit beside the functional one. Most teams skip the
emotional and social layers and end up shipping technically correct
products that customers don't love; the bottom row exists to keep
the trichotomy honest.

## Reach for it when

- You're synthesising customer interviews and need to crystallise
  what they're really hiring for — distinct from the features they
  asked for.
- Multiple teams disagree on "what the customer wants" because
  everyone is naming a different layer (functional / emotional /
  social) of the same job.
- You're about to write a value proposition or positioning statement
  and want an upstream artefact that names the **progress** the
  customer is trying to make.
- A previous launch missed the mark even though it solved the
  functional problem — usually a sign the emotional or social job
  was ignored. The canvas surfaces that.

## Skip it when

- You haven't done any customer observation yet. JTBD without research
  is the team telling itself a story. Do interviews first.
- The decision is operational, not customer-facing.
- You're modelling internal stakeholder dynamics — JTBD is about a
  customer's progress, not a colleague's preference.
- A single canvas is being asked to cover several customer segments
  at once. One canvas per segment.

## Reading further

- *Competing Against Luck* — Clayton Christensen et al. The book that
  made JTBD canonical. Key idea: the **circumstance is fundamental**
  to defining the job.
- *The Jobs-to-be-Done Framework* — Make Us Proud (Medium). The Job
  Story format used in the three top columns of this canvas.
- *Why Emotional and Social Jobs Are Critical* — productsos.substack.
  The discipline behind the bottom row.
