# Key Moment

A **snapshot of this stage**. If you took a photograph at this moment
in the customer's life, what would be in the frame?

This is where the journey gets concrete. Needs are abstract ("decide
which tool to buy"); a key moment grounds the need in **where**,
**when**, **who**, **on what device**, **with what artefact in hand**.
The original Design A Better Business canvas frames these as small
"polaroid" pictures for exactly this reason — to force the team to
imagine the moment, not generalise it.

## What goes here

- **Where**: kitchen counter, open-plan office, on a train, in a
  customer's reception area.
- **When**: Friday 4pm before they sign off, Monday 9am before stand-up,
  late at night after the kids are in bed.
- **Who else is involved**: alone, with a teammate looking over their
  shoulder, on a call with three vendors.
- **What channel/surface**: web, mobile app, email, in-person, paper
  form, support chat.
- **What artefact** is in play: a comparison spreadsheet, a printed
  invoice, a screenshot they're about to paste into Slack.

## Per-stage examples

- **Discovery** — "Friday 4pm, laptop open on the kitchen counter, three
  vendor websites in tabs, kids watching TV in the next room."
- **Consideration** — "Slack DM to a peer: 'Have you used X? worth
  it?' — answer comes back two days later, after the moment has cooled."
- **Onboarding** — "Logs in Monday morning, lands on the wrong workspace
  by mistake, almost closes the tab before figuring out the toggle."
- **Use** — "Tuesday 10am, triggers the weekly export, drags the file
  into a Slack channel, captions it with one line."
- **Renewal** — "Renewal email arrives in finance's inbox, gets
  forwarded to her with 'is this still worth it?' as the only body."

## Why the snapshot framing matters

When a team brainstorms key moments without the snapshot framing, they
trend toward generalities ("the user evaluates options"). When you ask
"what was Maria physically doing on Friday at 4pm?", you get specifics
— and specifics surface design opportunities ("oh, this happens on a
phone, our pricing page is unusable on mobile").

## Failure modes

- **Generic moments** ("customer reviews features"). Push for the
  photo: who is in it, what device is in their hand, what's around them?
- **Inventing the moment.** If you haven't watched a real customer in
  this stage, the snapshot is a guess — mark the sticky as a hypothesis
  to verify.
- **Treating the moment as a touchpoint catalog.** A list of channels
  ("web, app, email") is not a key moment. The moment is one specific
  scene; the touchpoints are part of *what's in the photo*, not the
  whole photo.
