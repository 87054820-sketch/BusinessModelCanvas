# Theory + worked examples

## What "value curve" means

A value curve is the visual signature of a strategy. Read horizontally
across factors, it tells you what an offering invests in; read vertically
at one factor, it tells you who's competing how. Five things to look for
in a curve worth shipping:

1. **Focus.** Most factors at low or high — *not* a flat curve. A flat
   curve says "we're trying to win on everything," which costs more and
   wins less.
2. **Divergence.** Visibly different shape from the industry's average
   curve. If yours hugs theirs, you're competing on cost and brand
   and that's where the largest player wins.
3. **A compelling tagline.** A non-experts can read your curve and say
   "ah, that's the one that <verb> <noun>."
4. **Coherence with the BMC.** If your curve says "premium service,
   minimal price," your Cost Structure better make that math work.
5. **Customer-facing factors only.** Factors that customers don't
   evaluate (e.g. internal process metrics) belong in a different tool.

## Reading some real-world examples

These are commonly cited in *Blue Ocean Strategy* and follow-up
publications. Use them as calibration when you draft your own curves.

### Yellow Tail (wine, Casella Wines, 2001)

The Australian wine industry was clogged with cabernets, merlots, and
shirazes competing on **tannins, complexity, prestige, oak, and the
vineyard's history**. Yellow Tail's curve:

- **Eliminated** wine-snob factors entirely: complexity, tannins,
  age-worthiness, prestige.
- **Reduced** vintage variation, range of grape varieties, marketing
  spend on awards.
- **Raised** ease of drinking, ease of selection, fun and adventure.
- **Created** a shelf-friendly bottle, a brand that beer drinkers
  recognized, and an in-store taste-test.

The shape is unmistakable: low on the left, high on the right, with the
"low" factors precisely the ones the rest of the industry was screaming
about.

### Cirque du Soleil (live entertainment, late 1980s)

The traditional circus competed on **animal acts, multiple show rings,
star performers, aisle concessions, and discount pricing for kids**.
Cirque's curve:

- **Eliminated** animal acts and star performers.
- **Reduced** the number of rings, aisle concessions, fun-for-kids
  positioning.
- **Raised** unique venues and the artistic feel of the show.
- **Created** a theme per show, refined audience experience, world-class
  music and dance.

The result: a different category entirely, customers willing to pay
theatre prices for what looked like a circus reborn.

### Nintendo Wii (consoles, 2006)

In an industry competing on **graphics, processing power, online
multiplayer, hardcore-gamer titles, and price**, Wii:

- **Eliminated** cutting-edge graphics, hard-disk storage, hardcore
  niche titles.
- **Reduced** raw CPU/GPU specs, library depth at launch.
- **Raised** physicality (motion controls), party-game appeal,
  simplicity of setup.
- **Created** a console grandparents would buy. Family-living-room
  positioning. New buyers who'd never owned a console.

## Tips for using this canvas

- **Start with the customer's factors, not yours.** It's tempting to
  list factors that *you're* good at. Don't. List the factors customers
  use to choose between offerings — even ones where you currently
  score poorly.
- **Limit yourself to 5–10 factors.** More than 10 and the curve becomes
  a sawtooth nobody can read at a glance.
- **Score on a 0–5 scale.** The exact number doesn't matter; the
  *shape* does. 0 = "we don't offer this at all," 5 = "this is what
  we're famous for."
- **Keep the chart to curve data.** Points and lines should communicate the shape; ERRC rationale, noncustomer insight, trade-off logic, and conclusions belong in the Story, not in long stickies floating over the chart.
- **Pair with the BMC.** A great Strategy Canvas is unimplementable
  without a coherent Cost Structure / Key Resources story; build both
  in the same project.

## What this canvas is NOT

- **Not a competitor analysis matrix.** That has columns of features
  and rows of products. The Strategy Canvas plots a *shape*; the
  shape's *contrast* with rivals is the point.
- **Not a SWOT.** SWOT lists; Strategy Canvas draws.
- **Not a roadmap.** Once you know the shape you want, you still need
  to decide which factor moves first. Use a separate sequencing tool.
