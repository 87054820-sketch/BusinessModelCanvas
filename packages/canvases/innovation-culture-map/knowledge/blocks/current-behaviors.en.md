# Current behaviors

This is often the easiest place to start. Do not write abstract judgments like “we do not innovate” or “people fear failure.” Write observable evidence.

Good sticky format: **who repeatedly does what, in what situation, producing what signal**.

Examples:
- After growth workshops, nobody schedules customer interviews.
- In reviews, leaders ask for three-year revenue forecasts before asking what was recently validated.
- When evidence is bad, teams reframe it as “we need more time.”

Capture both positive and negative behaviors. The Culture Map is not for blame; it is for seeing patterns.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer is observable, repeated, and neutral: who does what, where, and what signal it creates. A weak answer judges motives, such as "leaders do not care" or "teams are lazy", without evidence.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not turn this into blame. Also do not write one-off anecdotes unless they reveal a repeated pattern. If a behavior happens only because of one person, note that dependency.

## Sticky Examples

- "In monthly reviews, teams show roadmap progress before customer evidence."
- "After failed tests, teams change the success metric instead of retiring the idea."

## Transfer Questions

- What behavior could an outsider observe in a meeting or project review?
- How often does it happen?
- Which outcome does this behavior produce?

## Source Grounding

Culture Map practice starts with behaviors because they are visible and can be redesigned. The Invincible Company uses observable behaviors to connect culture to innovation portfolio outcomes.
