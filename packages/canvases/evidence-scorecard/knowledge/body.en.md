# Evidence Manual

## Inputs

Bring one critical assumption, the experiment design, success and failure criteria, raw observations, behavior data, customer quotes, cost/time spent, and the decision that must be made. The scorecard is not a research archive; it is a decision instrument.

## Output

The output is a short investment decision: persevere, pivot, run one sharper experiment, transfer learning to another project, fund the next stage, or retire the idea. A good output also names the remaining risk and the next canvas or experiment.

## Filling Sequence

1. **Start with the critical assumption.** Write the assumption as it existed before the test. Avoid rewriting it after seeing the result.
2. **Then record current evidence.** Separate observed behavior from interpretation. Interview counts, meetings, prototype demos, and compliments are activity; evidence is what changed in the world.
3. **Score evidence strength.** Opinions are weak, behavior is stronger, and money or binding commitment is strongest. A large sample of weak tests is still weak evidence.
4. **Check learning velocity.** Ask whether the team is learning fast enough to reduce uncertainty before budget, morale, or timing runs out.
5. **Name risk reduction.** Say which risk fell and which risk remains untouched.
6. **Make the portfolio decision.** Do not end with "learn more" unless the next experiment has a precise decision trigger.
7. **Design the next experiment.** Pick the cheapest test that could change the decision.

## Recommended Cases

Use Uber or Airbnb to discuss why early marketplace evidence needs behavior from both sides. Use Nespresso or Gillette when evidence depends on willingness to adopt a repeated purchasing model, not only interest in a product.

## Common Misreads

- "We talked to 30 people" is not a result unless the conversations changed a decision.
- "People clicked" may show curiosity, not willingness to pay or switch.
- "The pilot worked" is not enough if the test ignored delivery cost, sales effort, or compliance risk.
- "More data" is not always the next step; sometimes the next step is a stronger test.
