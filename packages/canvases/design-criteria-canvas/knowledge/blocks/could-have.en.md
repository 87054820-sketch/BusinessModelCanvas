# Could Haves

These are useful extras that can wait. They may improve experience, add differentiation, or unlock future options, but they should not block current validation.

## How to write

- Include criteria that matter only if resources allow.
- Keep non-critical ideas out of Must and Should.
- Treat these as candidates for a later iteration roadmap.

## Examples

- “Could support multiple interface languages”
- “Could offer advanced analytics reports”
- “Could deeply integrate with third-party CRMs”

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good Could Have names a useful option that should not block learning or launch. A weak answer is a dumping ground for every idea the team was afraid to reject.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not let Could Haves distract from the riskiest assumptions. If the next experiment does not depend on it, keep it out of current scope. Also avoid promising Could Haves to customers before the core proposition is validated.

## Sticky Examples

- "Could add multi-language support after validating demand in one region."
- "Could integrate Salesforce once manual import becomes the adoption bottleneck."

## Transfer Questions

- What learning or customer value would this unlock later?
- What must be true before this becomes worth building?
- Does this option distract from validating the core proposition?

## Source Grounding

Design criteria help teams protect focus while preserving future options. Testing Business Ideas reinforces this by sequencing extras after the riskiest assumptions have evidence.
