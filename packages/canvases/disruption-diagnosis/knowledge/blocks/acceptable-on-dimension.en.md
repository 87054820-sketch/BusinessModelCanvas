# Acceptable-on-dimension (立足点接受的维度)

The disruption trade: what does the foothold customer get instead, that compensates for the inferiority above? "Cheaper" alone is rarely enough — what does that price *unlock* that the customer couldn't have otherwise? Convenience, simplicity, accessibility, autonomy, context fit, lower commitment, fewer prerequisites?

Christensen's insight is that the disruptor's offer is *worse on the wrong axis to count* for mainstream customers, but better on an axis that the foothold has been forced to ignore.

## Core Questions

- On which dimension is the disruptor better than the incumbent? (Price, simplicity, accessibility, convenience, autonomy, low commitment.)
- How much better? Quantify with a metric the foothold customer cares about.
- Why does the foothold customer *accept* the inferiority above? What does this "better" unlock for them — speed, dignity, time, money, agency?
- Is the "better" dimension growing in importance for the foothold over time?
- Could the incumbent offer the same "better" without breaking its margin model?

## Examples

- **Transsion in Africa**: "$30-50 price (vs Apple/Samsung's $300+), 5-7 day battery (vs 1 day), dual-SIM (vs single), dark-skin camera tuning. The foothold gets a phone they can actually own and use across power-grid gaps."
- **Carvana**: "Zero negotiation + 7-day return policy. The foothold gets dignity and certainty — at the cost of test-driving."
- **Ping An Good Doctor**: "Instant access + no commute + Mandarin-fluent AI triage. The foothold gets agency over their own care."
- **Honda Supercub (1960s US)**: "$250 price (vs Harley's $1500+), 50cc displacement, light weight, easy to ride. The foothold gets motorcycling at a price + skill level that wasn't accessible."

## How it flows into the canvas

Acceptable-on-dimension is the upside half of the disruption trade. Together with Worse-on-dimension, it defines the value proposition for the foothold customer. The Incumbent's-rational-ignore zone usually rests on the incumbent's *correct* assumption that mainstream customers don't value this dimension enough.
