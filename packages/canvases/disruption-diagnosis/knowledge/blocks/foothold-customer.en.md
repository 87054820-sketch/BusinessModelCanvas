# Foothold customer (立足点客户)

The foothold customer is the load-bearing assumption of disruptive innovation theory. Without an identifiable foothold — either overshot mainstream customer or non-consumer — what looks like disruption is usually direct competition or sustaining innovation.

Christensen distinguishes two types of foothold:

### Overshot mainstream (low-end disruption)

> *"As established firms innovate, they often add features and performance their best customers don't actually use or value, but charge premium prices for them. The 'overshot' customer at the bottom of the market is willing to accept 'good-enough' performance at a much lower price."*

### Non-consumer (new-market disruption)

> *"In a new-market disruption, the entrant competes against 'nonconsumption' — customers who could not previously afford, access, or operate the existing product. The entrant creates a new value network by enabling people to do for themselves what was previously available only through expensive specialists or unavailable altogether."*

## Core Questions

- Who exactly is the foothold? (Segment, geography, occasion — not "young users".)
- Are they overshot mainstream or non-consumer? Name the *current* product they over-pay for OR the *current* alternative they cannot access.
- How many of them are there today, and how much will they pay?
- Why is this foothold *invisible* to the incumbent — what does the incumbent's customer interview tell it about this segment?
- What is the cost of acquiring + serving the foothold per unit, vs the price they will pay?

## Examples

- **Overshot mainstream**: "U.S. used-car buyers (~10M/year) who refuse to set foot in a dealership — they pay full sticker plus dealer markup, but will pay 5% MORE for a no-negotiation experience."
- **Non-consumer**: "Sub-Saharan African mobile users with <$50 to spend on a phone — invisible to Apple/Samsung's premium analytics; ~700M people circa 2007."
- **Non-consumer**: "Chinese rural / secondary-city patients without easy access to specialist consultation — invisible to top-tier hospital systems; queue times measured in months."

## How it flows into the canvas

Foothold customer must be filled before Worse-on-dimension and Acceptable-on-dimension — those two zones depend on knowing who is making the trade. The Riskiest Assumptions zone almost always has a foothold-willingness assumption at the top.
