# Riskiest assumptions (最致命假设)

The single assumption that, if false, would falsify the disruption hypothesis and kill the bet. There are usually 3-4 candidates; pick the riskiest one and design the first experiment around it. Christensen's organisational prescription (separate unit, separate metrics, patience capital) only makes sense if these assumptions are tested early.

## The four classic riskiest assumptions

1. **Foothold willingness** — Will enough of the foothold customer actually buy at the offered price? Without this, the foothold is fictional.
2. **Improvement trajectory** — Can you actually improve along the mainstream dimension fast enough to reach next-step segments? Without this, you stay in the niche.
3. **Incumbent inattention duration** — Will the incumbent rationally remain inactive long enough? If they respond at month 18, the upmarket window closes.
4. **Organisational insulation** — Can the disruptive unit survive inside the parent without being killed by mature-business metrics? Christensen ch. 5-6 makes this the management discipline.

## Core Questions

- Which of the four classic assumptions is riskiest for *this* candidate? Why?
- What metric and threshold would falsify it? ("If <40% of n=200 foothold customers convert at the offered price by Q2.")
- What experiment can test it within 3 months for under $X?
- What is the recovery path if the assumption breaks — pivot, kill, or persevere with a different foothold?

## Examples

- **Tata Nano (low-end car disruption, ~2008)**: "Riskiest assumption: the Indian foothold customer would accept the 'cheapest car' brand positioning. **Falsified** — owning a Nano was experienced as embarrassing, breaking the foothold acceptance. The strict test theoretically passed; execution killed it. Worth keeping in the library as the failure case."
- **Transsion Africa (~2010)**: "Riskiest assumption: African mobile users were genuinely non-consumers of premium phones AND would commit ~$30 to a Transsion-branded phone. **Confirmed** — 200M+ units shipped by 2018."
- **Ping An Good Doctor (~2014)**: "Riskiest assumption: Chinese patients would trust mobile-app triage enough to use it as the first touch. **Confirmed** in tier-3+ cities; tier-1 cities slower."

## How it flows out

Each riskiest assumption becomes an `experiment-canvas` if the team commits to the disruption. The disruption-diagnosis canvas pairs with `innovation-metrics` to track whether evidence is accumulating fast enough to justify continued investment.
