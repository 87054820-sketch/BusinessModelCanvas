# How to use

1. **Define comparable units.** Use businesses, product lines, brands, or geographies at the same altitude.
2. **Estimate market growth.** Use category growth, segment growth, or a defensible proxy. Record the source.
3. **Estimate relative market share.** Compare each unit against the largest competitor or relevant leader.
4. **Place each unit.** Put the unit in Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, or Dogs.
5. **Add evidence.** Use market-data stickies to show why each placement is credible.
6. **Choose portfolio actions.** Translate position into invest, harvest, select, divest, or reposition.
7. **Cross-check with BMC and environment.** A quadrant position is not a strategy until the business model and market assumptions are tested.

# How to interpret quadrants

- **Stars** need investment to defend leadership in a growing market. They may become future Cash Cows if growth slows and leadership remains.
- **Cash Cows** generate cash in slower-growth markets. They should be defended efficiently and used to fund selected future bets.
- **Question Marks** consume cash in attractive markets where the company lacks share. Select a few to invest in; do not fund all of them equally.
- **Dogs** have weak share in slow markets. They may be divested, harvested, repositioned, or retained only for strategic reasons.

# Quality checklist

- Market growth and relative share use named sources or explicit assumptions.
- Units are comparable and not mixed across corporate, product, and feature levels.
- Cash Cow does not mean “ignore”; it means manage for cash while protecting the economics.
- Question Marks have explicit selection criteria.
- Dogs have a real exit, harvest, or reposition decision.
- The action row explains what management will do next.

# Common mistakes

- Using the matrix without data and calling opinions “market share.”
- Treating quadrant labels as automatic decisions.
- Funding every Question Mark because the market is growing.
- Harvesting Cash Cows so aggressively that they lose the advantage that funds the portfolio.
- Keeping Dogs because teams are emotionally attached.
- Ignoring profitability, defensibility, strategic fit, or business-model constraints.
