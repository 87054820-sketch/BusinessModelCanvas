# Dogs

Dogs have low relative share in low-growth markets. They are not automatically worthless, but they require a clear reason to keep.

## Examples

- “Legacy product has low share and flat demand.”
- “Regional offer consumes support resources without strategic value.”

## Flow

Choose harvest, divest, close, reposition, or keep for a named strategic reason.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer explains both dimensions: low market growth and weak relative share. It also names the strategic reason for keeping, harvesting, or exiting. A weak answer calls any disliked or small business a Dog without checking market growth, relative share, or portfolio role.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not assume Dogs are worthless. Some provide customer retention, regulatory access, capability learning, or bundling value. The mistake is to keep them by inertia without a clear strategic role or cost of capital logic.

## Sticky Examples

- "Standalone hardware add-on: declining category, no share advantage, keep only if bundled retention is proven."
- "Regional service line: low growth and weak share, but provides required support for top enterprise accounts."

## Transfer Questions

- Is this unit weak because the market is unattractive, our position is weak, or both?
- What would be lost if we exited?
- Is the strategic reason for keeping it worth the capital and management attention?

## Source Grounding

In the BCG matrix, Dogs sit in low-growth markets with low relative share. The matrix does not mandate automatic divestment; it forces an explicit portfolio decision about harvest, reposition, exit, or strategic retention.
