# Stars

Stars have high relative share in high-growth markets. They usually need investment to defend leadership and capture growth.

## Examples

- “Category leader in a market growing 25% per year.”
- “Platform has high share and strong demand expansion.”

## Flow

Expand major Stars into `business-model-canvas` and check funding requirements before assuming they become Cash Cows.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer names the leadership evidence, the market growth driver, and the investment needed to defend or expand share. A weak answer labels a business "Star" because it is popular internally, has high revenue, or uses a fashionable technology.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not assume a Star is already profitable. Fast growth often consumes cash for capacity, customer acquisition, distribution, or product investment. Also avoid ignoring the next S-curve: a Star can lose leadership if the growth driver shifts.

## Sticky Examples

- "Cloud security product: top-two share in a 30% growth segment; funding needed for enterprise sales coverage."
- "Marketplace category: high demand and high supply liquidity, but fulfillment quality is becoming the bottleneck."

## Transfer Questions

- What proof shows this unit has relative share, not just absolute revenue?
- Which activity must be funded to defend leadership?
- What would make this Star fail to become a future Cash Cow?

## Source Grounding

BCG's original matrix treats Stars as high-share businesses in high-growth markets. Henderson's cash-flow logic warns that Stars can require substantial reinvestment before they produce surplus cash.
