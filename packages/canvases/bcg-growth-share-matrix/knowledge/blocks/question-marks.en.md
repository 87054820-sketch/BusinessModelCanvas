# Question Marks

Question Marks sit in growing markets where the company lacks relative share. They need selection, not automatic funding.

## Examples

- “Fast category growth, but our share is below the leader by 5x.”
- “Early brand has traction but no scale advantage.”

## Flow

Write the evidence that this unit can become a Star; otherwise select out early.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer explains why the market is growing, why the unit lacks relative share today, and what evidence would justify investment. A weak answer only says "the market is hot" or "this could be big" without naming the share gap, investment requirement, or path to leadership.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not treat every early-stage idea as a Question Mark. The BCG logic is about competitive position in a market, not novelty. Also avoid funding all Question Marks equally; portfolio discipline means selecting the few that can plausibly become Stars.

## Sticky Examples

- "EV fleet software: category growing fast, but our share is less than 10% of the leader."
- "GenAI support tool: strong pilot demand, unclear channel advantage, needs proof of repeatable acquisition."

## Transfer Questions

- What evidence shows this market is growing rather than merely fashionable?
- What would have to change for this unit to gain relative share?
- Which assumption should be tested before committing growth capital?

## Source Grounding

BCG's Growth-Share Matrix uses market growth and relative market share as a portfolio lens. Henderson's portfolio logic treats Question Marks as selective investment candidates: some deserve cash to become Stars, while others should be exited before they consume the resources Cash Cows generate.
