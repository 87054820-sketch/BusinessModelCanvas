# Cash Cows

Cash Cows have high relative share in slower-growth markets. They fund the portfolio, but they still need efficient defense.

## Examples

- “Mature category leader generates repeat cash flow.”
- “Installed-base business funds emerging platform bets.”

## Flow

Name what cash is harvested, what advantage must be protected, and which future bets it funds.

## Good Answer vs Weak Answer

A good answer identifies the relative-share advantage, the low-growth market context, and the cash that can be harvested without damaging the franchise. A weak answer simply says "profitable mature business" without explaining why it keeps its share or how much reinvestment is still required.

## Common Pitfalls

Do not over-harvest Cash Cows until service quality, brand trust, or operational resilience erodes. Also avoid treating every mature unit as a Cash Cow; weak share in a mature market is closer to Dog logic than funding engine logic.

## Sticky Examples

- "Legacy payment gateway: low market growth, high share, strong retention, funds two platform experiments."
- "Maintenance software: stable renewal cash, but needs minimum security investment to prevent churn."

## Transfer Questions

- What protects the unit's share in a low-growth market?
- How much cash can be redeployed without weakening the model?
- Which future bets should this Cash Cow deliberately fund?

## Source Grounding

The BCG matrix positions Cash Cows as high-share units in low-growth markets. Henderson's portfolio view uses their cash generation to fund selected Question Marks and support Stars, while keeping enough reinvestment to preserve the advantage.
