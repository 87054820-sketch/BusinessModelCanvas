# How to use

1. **Define the focal issue.** Name the strategic decision, business model, portfolio, or market question the scenarios must inform.
2. **Start from environment signals.** Use `business-model-environment` to collect trends, market forces, industry forces, macro forces, and weak signals.
3. **Separate trends from uncertainties.** Trends may shape every future; critical uncertainties create different futures.
4. **Choose two independent uncertainties.** Each should materially change strategic choices and have two credible end states.
5. **Build four scenarios.** Each scenario should describe market structure, customer behavior, competitive dynamics, operational constraints, and BMC pressure points.
6. **Wind-tunnel choices.** Test the current BMC, new business options, portfolio bets, and design criteria inside each scenario.
7. **Capture robust moves and early signals.** Robust moves are useful across several futures; early signals tell you which future may be forming.
8. **If this is an organizational project, assess it.** Record what changed in decisions, assumptions, and follow-up monitoring.

# How to interpret

A strong Scenario Matrix changes what the team would do. If every scenario leads to the same generic strategy, the uncertainties are probably weak or the implications are underdeveloped.

# Quality checklist

- The focal issue is explicit.
- The two uncertainties are independent.
- Each scenario has a different strategic logic, not just a different mood.
- Every scenario pressures at least one BMC block or portfolio choice.
- Robust moves and early signals are actionable.
- Follow-up monitoring is assigned to an owner.

# Anti-patterns

- Writing optimistic, base, pessimistic, and disaster versions.
- Creating vivid scenarios that do not change strategy.
- Choosing uncertainties that describe the same force twice.
- Ignoring early signals, so scenario planning cannot be updated over time.
- Treating the workshop output as the end instead of deploying scenarios into decisions.

# Inputs and Outputs

Inputs include the focal decision, environmental signals, trends, potential uncertainties, current BMC assumptions, and the strategic options being considered. Outputs include four named futures, implications for the model, early signals to monitor, and robust moves that remain useful across more than one future.

# Related Learning Path

Read *The Art of the Long View* for scenario logic, driving forces, wind-tunneling, implications, and early indicators. Read *Scenario Planning in Organizations* when scenarios must become an organizational routine rather than a one-off workshop. HBR's strategy-under-uncertainty framing is useful for deciding when scenarios are more appropriate than a single forecast.
