# How to fill it out

The Empathy Map's superpower is the **fill order**. It is not
free-form, and it is not aesthetic. Gray's 2017 update is explicit:
**start with the goal, walk the OUTSIDE circuit first (everything
observable), then move INWARD (everything inferred)**. Grounding
internal inference in external evidence is the whole point of the
canvas.

## The order

1. **Goal / Persona** — _Who are we empathising with, and what are
   they trying to do?_ Name the specific person and the situation.
   Frame this as understanding them, not changing them.
2. **See** — _What's in their environment?_ The stimuli reaching them
   each day: physical surroundings, what's on their screen, what
   competitors are doing, what they witness peers do.
3. **Say** — _What do they say in public?_ The phrases they articulate
   in meetings, posts, and conversations. Their stated viewpoint.
4. **Do** — _What do they actually do?_ Observable behaviour — how
   they spend their time, the rituals, the routines, the actions
   (which often don't match what they say).
5. **Hear** — _What do they hear from others?_ Direct feedback from
   peers and authority figures, advice from their network, ambient
   signals from their podcasts / inbox / feeds.
6. **Think & Feel** — _What's in their head?_ Now — and only now —
   infer the internal state from everything you collected on the
   outside. Thoughts, emotions, worries, aspirations.
7. **Pains** — _Fears, frustrations, obstacles._ The blockers and
   the unresolved costs.
8. **Gains** — _Wants, hopes, success._ What "winning" looks like
   to them.

## Why the order matters

> "Only AFTER you have made the circuit of outside elements do you
> focus on what's going on inside their head."
> — Dave Gray, *Updated Empathy Map Canvas*

Start inside the head, and you'll fill the map with team assumptions
projected onto the user. Start outside, and you have actual evidence —
direct quotes, observed actions, ambient signals — to anchor the
inference. The Pains and Gains at the bottom of the map then carry
real weight, because they're rooted in what the team has actually
seen.

## Quality habits

- **Feed it with research.** Tape interviews, paste the quotes, mark
  which sticky came from which transcript. The map is synthesis; if
  the source pile is empty, the map is opinion.
- **One map per segment.** A single map covering "all our users" is
  fuzzy by construction. Duplicate the canvas instead.
- **Mark inferences.** A sticky in Think & Feel that doesn't trace to
  external evidence is a hypothesis. Tag it as such.
- **Date and refresh.** Empathy maps are living artefacts — re-open
  after every research cycle and update the stickies that have moved.
- **Mind the gap between Say and Do.** When the words and the actions
  diverge, that gap is usually the most informative thing on the map.
  "He says he wants speed but his calendar is 80% governance meetings"
  is a finding.

## Common failure modes

- **No research input** → the map becomes a brainstorm. Recover by
  going back to the source recordings before any further synthesis.
- **Filling Think & Feel first.** Skipping the outside circuit means
  the inside is just hypothesis. The order isn't decoration — it's
  epistemic discipline.
- **Mixing observation and inference in the same sticky.** Keep "we
  saw X" (observable) separate from "we think they feel Y" (inferred).
  The map's structure already enforces this — don't undermine it with
  a single-sticky shortcut.
- **One-and-done.** The map's value is in repeated reference and
  update, not in delivery to a stakeholder.

## What to do with a finished map

- Pair it with the **Value Proposition Canvas** (see "Pairs with"
  below): the empathy map's *Pains* and *Gains* feed directly into the
  customer-profile side of the VPC.
- Pair it with **Jobs To Be Done**: *Say + Do* surface the functional
  job; *Think & Feel* surface the emotional and social jobs.
- Pair it with the **Bain value lens**: only tag *Gains* as emotional,
  social, or higher-order value elements when the outer-ring evidence
  supports them. Bain tags are post-evidence classification, not
  pre-interview assumptions.
- Print or screenshot it onto the team-room wall. The whole point is
  shared reference, not a one-time deliverable.
