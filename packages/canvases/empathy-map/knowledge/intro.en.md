# When to use the Empathy Map

The Empathy Map (Dave Gray, **XPLANE**, 2009; updated canvas 2017) is a
one-page picture of **another person's experience** — what they see, say,
do, and hear externally, and what they think, feel, struggle with, and
want internally. It is the team's shared reference for "the human we're
designing for," and it is meant to be built, pointed at, and updated as
you learn.

The 2017 update — the version this canvas implements — adds an explicit
**goal / persona** anchor at the top, and an explicit fill order that
forces teams to reason from observation **outward → inward**. Inferences
about the internal state come AFTER the external circuit is complete,
not before.

## Reach for it when

- You're about to start a design / research effort and want a **shared
  mental model** of the user, not five different mental models held by
  five different people.
- A team keeps arguing "what the user wants" without grounding the
  answer in anything observed.
- You have raw research material (interview transcripts, support
  tickets, sales calls, ride-alongs) and need to **synthesise it into
  something a designer can act on**.
- You're about to write a value proposition or a job-to-be-done and want
  an upstream artefact that names the human's pains and gains in their
  own terms.

## Skip it when

- The decision is purely operational and doesn't depend on user
  understanding.
- You haven't done **any** observation yet — an empathy map without
  research is fiction. Do the interviews first; the map is the
  synthesis, not the source.
- You're modelling internal stakeholder dynamics. The empathy map is
  about an external user; for cross-team flow use a different tool
  (service blueprint, RACI, etc.).
- A single map is being asked to cover several customer segments at
  once. One map per segment, or the picture stays vague.
