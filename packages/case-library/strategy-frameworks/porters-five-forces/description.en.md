# Porter’s Five Forces

Porter’s Five Forces analyzes industry structure through five sources of competitive pressure: rivalry among existing competitors, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of new entrants, and threat of substitutes. The point is to understand profit pressure before choosing a business-model position.

## When to use

Use it when a team needs to understand why an industry is attractive or unattractive, why margins are pressured, or which force a new business model must neutralize. It is useful before entering a market, repositioning a model, or pressure-testing environment assumptions.

Do not use it as a competitor list. Competitors are only one force. Also do not use it to design customer value directly; pair it with VPC or BMC after the industry pressure is clear.

## How it maps to PinGarden canvases

- `business-model-environment` captures industry forces, market forces, trends, and macro forces around a BMC.
- `business-model-canvas` shows how the company will respond through activities, resources, partners, channels, costs, and revenue.
- `design-criteria-canvas` turns industry pressure into design rules for a defensible model.

## Workflow

1. Define the industry boundary and unit of analysis.
2. Identify direct rivalry and the basis of competition.
3. Analyze buyer power and supplier power separately.
4. Identify new entrants and barriers to entry.
5. Identify substitutes that solve the same job differently.
6. Translate each force into BMC pressure points.
7. Define strategic responses: avoid, reshape, defend, partner, differentiate, or change the model.

## What to notice in examples

- `swiss-private-banking` shows how unbundling pressure changes industry structure.
- `mobile-telco-unbundling` shows infrastructure, product, and customer-relationship pressures.
- `carvana` shows how a new model changes rivalry and substitutes in used-car retail.
- `aliexpress` shows platform power, supplier access, buyer choice, and cross-border substitution.
- `patagonia` shows how differentiation and values can reduce direct price rivalry.

## Common misuses

- Treating Five Forces as a competitor checklist.
- Ignoring substitutes because they are outside the formal industry.
- Assuming a growing market is attractive without checking power and rivalry.
- Listing forces without translating them into business-model implications.
- Confusing industry attractiveness with company capability.
