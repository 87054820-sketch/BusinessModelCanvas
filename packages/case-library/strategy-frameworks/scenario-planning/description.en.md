# Scenario Planning

Scenario Planning extends Business Model Environment work. Instead of listing external opportunities and threats, it builds several plausible futures around driving forces and critical uncertainties, then compares whether strategic choices still hold.

## Core idea

Single forecasts push teams to bet too early on one future. Scenario planning asks teams to identify driving forces, isolate the uncertainties that would actually change strategic choices, and combine them into several distinct but plausible futures. The goal is better strategic thinking, not prediction accuracy.

## When to use

Use Scenario Planning when a business model, portfolio, or strategic choice faces high uncertainty from technology, regulation, customer behavior, industry structure, macro conditions, or social change.

Do not use it when the problem is operational scheduling, simple forecasting, or a low-uncertainty decision. Do not create good/base/bad versions; that only disguises a single forecast.

## Canvas connections

- Use `business-model-environment` to collect trend, market, industry, and macro signals.
- Use `scenario-matrix` to turn critical uncertainties into several futures.
- Use `business-model-canvas` to inspect pressure on the current or proposed model.
- Use `portfolio-map` to compare which bets survive multiple futures.
- Use `design-criteria-canvas` to turn robust moves into design criteria.

## Practical workflow

1. Define the focal issue and time horizon.
2. Collect external signals through environment scanning.
3. Separate predetermined trends from true critical uncertainties.
4. Select the uncertainties that would most change strategic choices.
5. Build several internally consistent scenarios.
6. Wind-tunnel BMCs, portfolio bets, and design criteria in each future.
7. Identify robust moves, contingent moves, options to preserve, and early signals to monitor.
8. If the work must affect an organization, run it as a `performance-based-scenario-planning` project with preparation, implementation, and assessment.

## What to notice in examples

`patagonia` is the current high-confidence example because the case includes scenario-matrix work and shows how external environmental, market, and social forces pressure a business model and strategic posture.

## Quality bar

Good scenarios are not optimistic/base/pessimistic forecasts. They force the team to see different strategic logics. The output should say which options to preserve, which signals to monitor, which moves are robust, and which decisions change under each future.

## Common misuses

- Writing vivid futures that do not change decisions.
- Treating scenarios as forecasts with probabilities.
- Choosing uncertainties that are not independent.
- Ignoring weak signals and early warning indicators.
- Finishing at scenario stories instead of using them to test BMCs, portfolios, and design criteria.
