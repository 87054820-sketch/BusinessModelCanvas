# Porter's Value Chain Analysis

Porter's Value Chain (Porter, 1985) decomposes a firm into nine internal activities and asks of each: does this activity create cost advantage, differentiation, both, or neither? Five **primary activities** move the product from input to customer (inbound logistics → operations → outbound logistics → marketing & sales → service). Four **support activities** enable them (firm infrastructure, human resource management, technology development, procurement). The point is to stop talking about "the company" as one block and start locating where margin and differentiation are actually created — and where they leak.

## When to use

Use the Value Chain when the team has a strategic intent ("we will compete on quality" / "we will be the lowest cost") but cannot yet point at which internal activities must change to make that true. It is also the right lens when capability looks fine on paper but margin keeps eroding — the leak is almost always in a linkage between two activities, not inside one.

Do not use it as a generic process diagram. The discriminating question is always *competitive advantage per activity*, not *what does this activity do*.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **McKinsey 7-S** asks whether strategy, structure, systems, shared values, style, staff, and skills are aligned — the *soft and hard* alignment lens. Value Chain is activity-granular, focused on *where competitive advantage forms*. 7-S asks "is the organization coherent?"; Value Chain asks "where do we actually win?"
- **Platform Strategy** is the multi-sided ecosystem lens. Value Chain is the internal-activity lens. A platform business has a value chain too — but its load-bearing differentiator is usually network effects rather than activity-level cost or differentiation.
- **BMC Key Activities / Key Resources / Key Partners** are the BMC's three blocks that the Value Chain decomposes. The mapping is direct: BMC KA ≈ primary activities currently in-house; BMC KR ≈ what assets/people each activity demands; BMC KP ≈ activities deliberately externalised. The Value Chain is the higher-resolution view of these three BMC blocks.

## How it maps to PinGarden canvases

- `business-model-canvas` — each Value Chain activity translates into Key Activities, Key Resources, or Key Partners stickies (the three blocks together account for the nine activities). Differentiating activities should also appear as Value Proposition support.
- `design-criteria-canvas` — encode the activity-level rules ("inbound logistics must support same-day inventory rebalancing", "operations must hold ≥30% gross margin") that the next business-model iteration must respect.

## Workflow

1. Draw the nine boxes for the unit of analysis (one business, not a whole conglomerate).
2. Inside each box, list the concrete sub-activities (e.g., for Operations: production scheduling, quality control, packaging).
3. For each activity, mark: cost level, differentiation contribution, capability strength.
4. Highlight **linkages**: where two activities reinforce each other (e.g., procurement quality enables operations consistency) or fight each other (e.g., marketing promises faster service than service can deliver).
5. Pick 2–3 activities that are the load-bearing differentiators — these are where capability investment should concentrate.
6. Translate findings into BMC KA / KR / KP changes and design-criteria rules.

## What to notice in examples

- `patagonia` — Inbound logistics (responsibly-sourced materials) + Operations (Fair Trade Certified factories) + Service (Worn Wear repair program) form a reinforcing chain. The differentiator is not any single activity but the *linkages* between them and the values-led HR substrate. Cutting any one activity collapses the proposition.
- `cainiao` — The entire business is a logistics value chain offered as a service: inbound (merchants' warehouses) → operations (cross-dock sortation, customs) → outbound (last-mile network) is the product. Marketing and service activities feed back data that re-optimises operations — a textbook tight-linkage chain.
- `gillette` — Operations (precision blade manufacturing) + Marketing & Sales (heavy brand investment) + Procurement (specialised steel supply) jointly defend the razor-and-blades model. The cost advantage on blades comes from operations scale; the price defence comes from marketing.
- `nestle-portfolio` — At portfolio level, the chain runs across many SBUs sharing Procurement and Firm Infrastructure (single global treasury, single R&D), with each SBU running its own primary chain. Useful illustration of how the support activities are the load-bearing connective tissue in a multi-business firm.

## Common misuses

- Drawing the nine boxes as a generic process diagram with no advantage assessment.
- Treating each activity in isolation and missing the linkages — most real differentiators are linkage-based.
- Skipping support activities (especially HRM and Firm Infrastructure) because they feel "boring" — they are usually the most defensible.
- Forgetting that for a service or platform business the "physical" inbound/outbound logistics shrink to near-zero, while Technology Development and Procurement of capacity dominate.
- Failing to translate Value Chain findings into BMC KA/KR/KP changes — the analysis stays on a slide instead of changing the model.
