# Blue Ocean Strategy

> Blue Ocean Strategy is not a business-model pattern. It is a strategic analysis framework for reconstructing market boundaries and designing a value curve that makes direct competition less relevant.

## Why this framework matters

Blue Ocean Strategy asks teams to stop treating the current industry playbook as fixed. Instead of trying to outperform rivals on the same dimensions, it looks for a new value curve: a focused, divergent profile that creates new demand while lowering or removing costs that customers no longer value. The official definition is the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and low cost to open new market space and create new demand (BOS official site).

For PinGarden, the framework is especially useful because it bridges strategy and business-model design. The Strategy Canvas shows the current competitive curve; the ERRC grid explains the strategic moves; the Business Model Canvas then tests whether the new value curve has a coherent operating model behind it. BMG 2010 makes this connection explicit: changes on the value side must be checked against resources, activities, partners, and cost structure.

## Core tools

1. **Strategy Canvas** — plot competitors across the same factors of competition. The goal is not to score higher everywhere, but to create a visibly different shape.
2. **ERRC grid** — ask what to Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, and Create. The cost saved by eliminate/reduce funds the value created by raise/create.
3. **Noncustomers** — look beyond current customers to people who refuse, avoid, or never considered the existing industry offer.
4. **Market-boundary reconstruction** — challenge the industry's assumptions about buyer groups, functional vs emotional orientation, substitute industries, complementary products, and time.

## What a good Blue Ocean case contains

A good case needs more than a slogan. It should show:

- the red-ocean logic of the incumbent industry;
- 5–10 factors of competition that customers actually compare;
- at least two value curves: industry norm and blue-ocean move;
- a clear ERRC explanation;
- the noncustomers or underserved buyers the move unlocked;
- the Business Model Canvas implications on channels, activities, resources, partners, revenue, and cost.

## First PinGarden examples

- `cirque-du-soleil` — eliminated animals and star performers, raised artistic theatre-like value, and created high-end live entertainment for adults and corporate buyers.
- `yellow-tail` — simplified wine choice and taste to pull beer/cocktail drinkers into wine.
- `qb-house` — stripped the barbershop to a functional 10-minute haircut and redesigned operations around speed and certainty.
- `nintendo-wii` — avoided the console hardware arms race and created family-friendly motion-controlled play for casual gamers.

## What goes wrong

- **Outscoring fantasy.** A curve that is higher than rivals on every factor is usually a cost explosion, not strategy.
- **No evidence for competitor scores.** Strategy Canvas scores must come from customer reviews, public pricing, feature comparisons, or credible case sources.
- **ERRC before plotting.** ERRC should be forced by the value-curve comparison, not used as a brainstorm detached from the market.
- **Innovation without business model coherence.** A new value curve is not enough; the BMC must show how the model can actually deliver and capture value.
