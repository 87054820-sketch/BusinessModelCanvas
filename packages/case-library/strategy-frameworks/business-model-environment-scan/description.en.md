# Business Model Environment Scan

> Business Model Environment Scan is a strategic analysis framework for asking whether a business model still fits the world around it. It is not a business-model pattern; it is a way to read external pressure before redesigning the model.

## Why this framework matters

Many business model failures are not caused by a broken canvas in isolation. They happen because the environment changes: regulation shifts, customer behaviour moves, capital dries up, infrastructure improves, substitutes become good enough, or a new technology changes the cost curve. The Business Model Environment map keeps those forces visible.

For PinGarden, this framework matters because it connects directly to the Business Model Canvas. Each environment sticky should point back to one or more BMC blocks: which segment is changing, which channel is weakening, which partner is becoming critical, which cost is exposed, or which revenue logic is under pressure.

## The four forces

1. **Key Trends** — technology, regulation, social and cultural shifts, demographic changes, and other long-fuse forces.
2. **Market Forces** — customer needs, demand shifts, switching costs, segment growth, and buyer behaviour.
3. **Industry Forces** — competitors, new entrants, substitutes, suppliers, stakeholders, and adjacent players. Porter Five Forces is useful here, but it is only one quadrant.
4. **Macro-Economic Forces** — capital availability, commodity prices, infrastructure, labour markets, currency, and broader economic cycles.

## What a good environment-scan case contains

A good case should show:

- the specific BMC being pressure-tested;
- 3–6 external signals, not generic trend headlines;
- the affected BMC blocks for each signal;
- whether each signal is a threat, opportunity, constraint, or ambiguity;
- the strategic response: reinforce the current model, adapt it, or design a new option.

## First PinGarden examples

- `patagonia` — environmental values, regulation, and consumer expectations pressure-test growth, sourcing, repair, and brand trust.
- `carvana` — online buying behaviour, used-car supply, financing conditions, logistics, and trust determine whether the model scales.
- `uber` — smartphone adoption created the opening, while labour regulation, city policy, insurance, and driver supply continue to shape the model.
- `airbnb` — urban regulation, trust infrastructure, travel behaviour, and host economics define the platform's room to operate.
- `alibaba-group` — e-commerce adoption, logistics infrastructure, payments, SMEs, and platform regulation shape the ecosystem.
- `cemex` — construction cycles, logistics constraints, infrastructure demand, and capital intensity drive the model.
- `transsion-africa` — local retail, repair access, power/connectivity constraints, and African consumer needs explain the model's fit.

## What goes wrong

- **Trend scrapbook.** A list of AI, regulation, demographics, and competitors is not analysis unless each signal is tied to a BMC block.
- **Competitor-only scan.** Industry Forces is one quadrant, not the whole environment.
- **No time horizon.** Some signals matter this quarter; others matter over five years. Mix them without labeling and the map becomes vague.
- **No strategic response.** The scan should lead to choices: adapt channels, add partners, change pricing, build options, or exit an exposed segment.
