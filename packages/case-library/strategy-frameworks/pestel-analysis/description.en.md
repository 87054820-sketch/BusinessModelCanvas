# PESTEL Analysis

PESTEL maps the macro environment into six categories of long-running forces: **P**olitical, **E**conomic, **S**ocial, **T**echnological, **E**nvironmental, **L**egal. It is the upstream scan that produces signals — not yet decisions. The point is to see what is changing in the world before you decide which industry pressure to fight and which business-model move to take.

## When to use

Use PESTEL at the start of a strategy iteration: a new market entry, a periodic environment review, the preparation phase before scenario planning, or whenever leadership senses the ground is shifting but cannot yet name which forces matter. It is also useful when teams keep arguing about "the future" without naming whose future — PESTEL forces you to anchor on observable forces, not hot takes.

Do not use PESTEL as a checklist of "things we mentioned in the deck." Each cell must carry a concrete signal (a regulation, a demographic shift, a technology curve) and a `so-what` for the business model.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **Business Model Environment Scan** (Strategyzer's four-force version) is the BMG-flavoured downstream of PESTEL: it directly tests the BMC against trends, market, industry, and macro forces. PESTEL is broader and more granular on the macro side; Env Scan is tighter and BMC-coupled. The natural flow is **PESTEL signals → Env Scan four forces → BMC pressure points**. See the dedicated section below for the coverage map.
- **Porter's Five Forces** is industry-structure analysis at the same altitude as Env Scan but focused on competitive economics, not macro forces. Run PESTEL first to find what is changing; run Five Forces to ask why margins are pressured.
- **Scenario Planning** consumes PESTEL output: pick the two most uncertain and most impactful PESTEL forces as the scenario axes.

## Relationship with Business Model Environment Scan

PESTEL and BMEScan are not competing tools — they are different altitudes of the *same* upstream-to-BMC pipeline. The difference is structural and worth mapping carefully:

| Dimension | PESTEL | Business Model Environment Scan |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Buckets | 6 (P · E · S · T · E · L) | 4 (Trends · Market · Industry · Macro) |
| Altitude | Broad macro environment | Tighter, BMC-adjacent |
| Output | Raw observable signals | BMC pressure points |
| Industry-specific layer | None | "Industry Forces" (competitors / new entrants / substitutes / suppliers / stakeholders) |
| Customer-specific layer | Implicit, via Social/Economic | Explicit "Market Forces" (segments / needs / switching costs / revenue attractiveness) |
| When to start | When the BMC is still being shaped | When the BMC exists and needs pressure-testing |

**Coverage map** — PESTEL → BMEScan:

- PESTEL **Political** + **Legal** → BMEScan **Macro Forces** (regulation, public infrastructure) and partially **Industry Forces** (entry barriers).
- PESTEL **Economic** → BMEScan **Macro Forces** (capital markets, economic infrastructure) and **Market Forces** (revenue attractiveness, willingness-to-pay).
- PESTEL **Social** → BMEScan **Key Trends** (sociocultural trends) and **Market Forces** (segment needs, demands).
- PESTEL **Technological** → BMEScan **Key Trends** (technology trends) and **Industry Forces** (substitutes, new entrants enabled by tech).
- PESTEL **Environmental** → BMEScan **Key Trends** (sociocultural / regulatory trends) and **Macro Forces** (commodities, supply-chain externalities).

What PESTEL does NOT cover and BMEScan does: **direct competitors and stakeholders inside the industry**. So if you only run PESTEL, you'll see macro winds but miss the rivalry inside your own ring. If you only run BMEScan, you'll see the close-in pressures but miss long-running macro tides (e.g., demographic inversion, climate transition timelines).

**Recommended workflow**:
1. Run PESTEL first at the strategy iteration's start — produces 20–40 macro signals.
2. Score signals on impact × uncertainty; keep the top 8–12.
3. Open BMEScan with the BMC anchored in the centre. Distribute the kept PESTEL signals into the four BMEScan zones (Trends absorbs PESTEL S/T/E-Environmental rising signals; Macro absorbs PESTEL E-Economic/P/L; Market and Industry add the missing buyer/competitor layer).
4. Translate each BMEScan sticky into a "this pressures BMC block X" pin so the team can see which blocks must change.

Skipping PESTEL and going straight to BMEScan is fine for periodic check-ins on a known business; skipping BMEScan after PESTEL is only fine when you do not yet have a BMC to pressure-test.

## How it maps to PinGarden canvases

- `business-model-environment` is the working canvas: each PESTEL force becomes one or more stickies under the trends / market / industry / macro zones.
- `scenario-matrix` picks the highest-uncertainty PESTEL forces as the two axes.
- `business-model-canvas` is where each PESTEL signal must finally land — as a pressure on a specific block.

## Workflow

1. Define the unit of analysis (industry + geography + time horizon).
2. For each of the six PESTEL categories, list 3–5 observable signals — facts, not adjectives.
3. For each signal, mark direction (rising / falling / volatile) and time horizon (now / 1–3y / 3–10y).
4. Score impact × uncertainty; highlight the high-impact-high-uncertainty cells for scenario planning.
5. For each high-impact signal, write a one-line `so-what` mapped to a BMC block.
6. Carry the open `so-what` items into Env Scan, Five Forces, or Scenario Matrix as the next move.

## What to notice in examples

- `patagonia` — Environmental and Social forces (climate, supply-chain ethics) are not external context but the load-bearing structure of the business model.
- `nickel-bank` — Political/Legal (PSD2 banking liberalisation in France) + Economic (financial-exclusion underclass) unlock a model that was previously illegal and uneconomic.
- `transsion-africa` — Social (multi-SIM behaviour, dark-skin photography), Economic (sub-$50 price point), and Technological (feature-phone-first then Android-first) forces define an entirely separate macro context from Apple's.
- `ping-an-group` — Political/Legal (Chinese financial regulation) and Technological (mobile-first health/finance integration) jointly enable the ecosystem play.

## Common misuses

- Listing trends without converting them to BMC implications.
- Treating PESTEL as a one-time deck slide rather than a periodic scanning routine.
- Confusing macro forces (PESTEL) with industry rivalry (Five Forces) — they are different altitudes.
- Over-weighting Technology (because it is glamorous) and ignoring Social/Environmental/Legal.
- Pretending uncertainty is risk: PESTEL signals are not probabilities to discount but uncertainties to plan against.
