# Disruptive Innovation

Clayton Christensen's Disruptive Innovation theory (Christensen, 1997; Christensen, Raynor & McDonald, 2015) is a **diagnostic** framework, not a synonym for "any clever startup". It distinguishes:

- **Sustaining innovation** — a better product for existing customers along dimensions they already value. Incumbents nearly always win here, because they have the resources, channels, and brand to deliver on those dimensions.
- **Disruptive innovation** — a product that is *initially inferior* on the dimensions mainstream customers value, but acceptable enough on a different dimension (price, simplicity, accessibility, convenience) to serve customers the incumbent over-serves or ignores. Over time the new entrant improves along the mainstream dimension and moves upmarket; by the time incumbents notice, the new entrant has the customer relationship.

Two sub-patterns:

1. **Low-end disruption** — the entrant serves *overshot* mainstream customers who would happily take a "good enough" product at a lower price.
2. **New-market disruption** — the entrant serves *non-consumers* who previously could not access the category at all (because of price, skill, location, regulation, or context).

The 2015 HBR clarification matters: Christensen narrowed the label to exclude many things commonly called "disruptive" (Uber, for instance, he argued was *not* a disruption in the strict sense). The discipline of the framework is the test — not the vibe.

## When to use

Use this lens when:

- A team is launching a product that is *worse* than incumbents on the conventional dimensions but better on a different one — to predict whether incumbents will rationally ignore them long enough to grow.
- An incumbent is wondering whether to take a new entrant seriously — disruption theory says the deepest danger is the entrant the dominant customer interview *correctly* says to ignore.
- A team is choosing between sustaining and disruptive bets in its innovation portfolio — they require different metrics, different organisation, and different success criteria.

Do not use this label loosely. The strict test is: (1) does the entrant start in a low-end or non-consumption foothold; (2) is the entrant's performance *initially inferior* on mainstream dimensions; (3) does the entrant improve over time along those dimensions while preserving the foothold advantage. If the answer is "no" to any of these, what you have is a sustaining innovation, a new-market entry, or just a competitor — all still valid moves, but not "disruption" in the Christensen sense.

## How it differs from neighbouring frameworks

- **McKinsey Three Horizons** is the time-portfolio layer (today's core / scaling / future bets). Disruption theory is the *judgment* applied to H3 candidates: is this H3 bet a sustaining better-mousetrap, a low-end disruption, or a new-market disruption? They require different commercialisation timelines, metrics, and organisational separation.
- **Innovation Metrics** measures Explore work by evidence, learning, and risk reduction — it is the *measurement* layer for H3 bets. Disruption theory tells you which assumptions to test first: for low-end disruption, the riskiest assumption is whether the foothold customer will actually buy a "worse" product at the lower price; for new-market disruption, the riskiest assumption is whether the non-consumer can be converted into a consumer at all.
- **Business Model Portfolio Management** decides where to place a bet on the Portfolio Map. Disruption theory tells you what *class* of bet it is, which informs the risk axis and the Explore vs. Exploit allocation.
- **Blue Ocean Strategy** also creates new market space, but through value innovation (simultaneously raising buyer value and lowering cost via the ERRC grid). Disruption typically *lowers* mainstream performance to find a foothold. They sometimes converge — but Blue Ocean is a market-creation choice, Disruption is a competitive-dynamics theory.

## How it maps to PinGarden canvases

- `business-model-canvas` — disruption is rarely just a product story; it almost always requires a different BMC (different channels, cost structure, key activities, partners). The case is built by sketching the incumbent's BMC alongside the disruptor's BMC and naming where they diverge.
- `portfolio-map` — disruptive bets sit deep in the Explore quadrant with high risk and uncertain return; their movement over time (foothold → mainstream → upmarket) becomes a path of pin migrations.
- `value-proposition-canvas` — for new-market disruption, the customer side is *non-consumers* — pains and jobs that have never been served. For low-end disruption, the customer side is *overshot* mainstream customers — pains specifically created by the incumbent's over-engineering.

## Workflow

1. State the candidate disruption hypothesis: who is the foothold customer, what dimension are we worse on, what dimension are we acceptable on?
2. Classify: low-end or new-market? If neither cleanly applies, the candidate is probably not a disruption.
3. Identify which incumbent customer-base the foothold sits adjacent to — the upmarket migration path.
4. Name the incumbent's *rational* reason to ignore: typically thin margin and small market today, paired with cannibalisation risk against current high-margin products.
5. List the riskiest assumptions: foothold customer willingness, upmarket improvement trajectory, incumbent inattention duration.
6. Decide commercialisation: protect the disruptor as a separate unit with separate metrics (per Christensen's organisational prescription), or it will be killed by the parent's pursuit of margin.

## What to notice in examples

- `transsion-africa` — Textbook **new-market disruption**. African feature-phone users were non-consumers from Apple/Samsung's perspective (wrong price point, wrong charging infrastructure, wrong skin-tone optimisation). Transsion built for that non-consumption foothold (dual-SIM, multi-day battery, dark-skin photography), and is now moving upmarket with Android smartphones in the same regions.
- `carvana` — **New-market disruption** in used cars. Carvana served buyers who avoided used-car dealerships entirely (the "I would rather pay more than negotiate" non-consumer). Initially the experience was inferior to driving-and-test-driving, but the foothold customer accepted that for the absence of negotiation. Now expanding into mainstream used-car buying.
- `ping-an-good-doctor` — **New-market disruption** in healthcare access. The foothold was Chinese users who could not easily access in-person specialist consultations (urban congestion + regional disparity). The product is initially inferior to seeing a specialist face-to-face, but acceptable enough for an underserved population — and now moving upmarket via specialist tele-consult and partnered in-person care.
- `tata-nano` — **Attempted low-end disruption** that failed instructively. The Nano targeted Indian families overshot by full-size cars but underserved by two-wheelers. The framework predicts the move; the case shows why the move failed (brand positioning broke the "good enough" foothold by making the car embarrassing to be seen in). A worked example of how the *theory* is necessary but not sufficient — execution along all four BMC dimensions matters as much as the strategic class.

## Common misuses

- Calling every startup "disruptive" — the Uber case is the most-cited example of misuse (Christensen himself argued Uber is a sustaining innovation in taxis, not a disruption).
- Confusing "new technology" with "disruptive innovation" — a new technology applied to mainstream customers along their existing dimensions is sustaining, no matter how novel.
- Skipping the foothold test — without an overshot or non-consumption customer base, what you have is direct competition.
- Forgetting Christensen's organisational prescription — disruptive bets must be insulated from the incumbent's metrics, or they will be killed.
- Treating disruption as the only innovation mode — most innovation in mature firms is sustaining, and that's correct and valuable.
