# Pre-Sale

Take real money — credit card, deposit, Kickstarter pledge — for a product that doesn't exist yet. The strongest desirability + viability signal short of an actual launch, because money is the only filter that screens out polite intent. People who say "I'd buy this" routinely don't; people who hand over $50 usually do.

## When to use

- B2C or prosumer with deal sizes the segment will actually transact ($20-$500 typically).
- Smoke Test passed; the next gate is "will the signups *actually pay*?".
- You can deliver within the promised timeframe (this is essential — non-delivery is fraud territory and burns trust catastrophically).
- The product genuinely benefits from a launch moment (crowdfunding social proof, scarcity, founder narrative).

Don't use:

- You can't deliver in the promised timeframe. The risk-to-reputation ratio is awful.
- B2B with deal sizes that need procurement (use Letter of Intent).
- The price would be set by enterprise sales (not a posted price). Pre-Sale assumes a single sticker price.
- Regulated category where pre-payment is legally fraught (medical, financial advice).

## How to run

1. **Pick a deliverable scope.** "We will ship X by Y date with these features." Vague is fatal.
2. **Set the price.** Slightly below your eventual price — early-supporter discount is fair, also moves conversions.
3. **Build a Pre-Sale page.** Hero, problem, solution, price, deliverable scope, refund policy (yes you need one).
4. **Drive 1000-5000 targeted visits** — paid ads, niche newsletters, communities where the segment lives.
5. **Define success threshold BEFORE running.** Conversion rate AND minimum revenue total. Both must clear.
6. **Honor the date.** Even if late, communicate aggressively, refund cleanly. The Pre-Sale's reputation cost is your next product's launch capital.

## What good looks like

- ≥ 1-3% conversion of targeted traffic (varies by category) AND your absolute revenue threshold met.
- Buyers cluster around ICP — not "anyone with a credit card", but the segment you targeted.
- Refund rate < 10% in the first month — buyers stay convinced.
- A waitlist forms after the cap — informs sustainable pricing post-launch.

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ Promise more than you can ship. Late or missing delivery is reputational napalm.
- ❌ No refund policy. Looks shady, conversion drops, regulators get interested.
- ❌ Untargeted ads. You'll convert curiosity-clickers who refund as soon as the email arrives.
- ❌ Treat Pre-Sale revenue as profit. It's working capital with a promise attached.
