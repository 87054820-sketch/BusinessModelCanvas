# Customer Interview

A 30-60 minute one-on-one conversation with someone who matches your **target customer profile**. You're not pitching a solution — you're listening to how they describe their world, the jobs they're trying to get done, the pains they hit, and the gains they wish were better. Discovery, not validation.

## When to use

Reach for it when:

- You have a **Desirability hypothesis** but no data — "we believe small-team CTOs lose hours per week reviewing PRs."
- You're at the start of a Value Proposition Canvas, Empathy Map, or JTBD canvas and the right column (customer profile / jobs / pains) is mostly TBD.
- You need to understand the *problem framing* before you commit to a solution direction. Solutions you build on a wrong frame get politely declined.
- The user just landed on a "riskiest assumption" sticky and has no evidence either way.

Don't reach for it when:

- The user already has 5+ interviews done and needs **stronger** evidence — jump to a Smoke Test, Concierge, or Letter of Intent.
- The hypothesis is about willingness to pay or pricing — interviews famously over-estimate buying intent. Use Pre-Sale or Letter of Intent instead.
- The user can't access target customers (B2B with no warm intros, gated communities). Switch to Discussion Forums / Search Trend Analysis as a passive substitute.

## How to run

1. **Pick the segment.** One segment per round; don't mix early adopters and laggards in the same notes.
2. **Recruit 5-12.** Five is the floor for any pattern to emerge; twelve is past the point of diminishing returns for a single round.
3. **Use a discussion guide, not a script.** 5-7 themes, each with 1 opener and 2-3 follow-up probes. Discard the guide the moment a thread becomes interesting — this is qualitative, not a survey.
4. **Open with their world, not your idea.** "Walk me through the last time you [job-to-be-done]." Then "What was hardest about it?" before you ever describe what you're building.
5. **Probe specifics, not opinions.** "When did you last hit that?" beats "How often does that happen?" Specific recent incidents are real data; frequency estimates are confabulation.
6. **Take notes literally — record their phrasing.** Their language is what your VPC stickies and marketing copy will use later. Don't paraphrase into your own jargon.
7. **Debrief within an hour.** Memory of nuance evaporates fast. Capture the 3-5 most surprising quotes verbatim, plus the meta-pattern you think you saw.

## What good looks like

- Five conversations produced **at least 2-3 recurring jobs/pains** across different participants — not all unique to one person.
- You can quote at least one verbatim line per pain. ("If I miss the 3pm sync I'm reading 80 messages on my phone in bed.")
- You can describe the customer's *day* before and after the moment your product would intervene — without using your product's name.
- Your VPC right column (jobs / pains / gains) feels populated by **observations**, not assumptions.

## Sample size & criteria

- **N ≥ 5** for any pattern claim. Below 5 and "they all said X" is noise.
- A theme is "validated for further testing" when **3 of 5 (or 4 of 8)** independently surface it without prompting.
- A pain that never came up despite probing for it is real evidence that **it's not a pain** — record the absence as data.

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ **Pitching the solution mid-interview.** Ten minutes in, you describe what you're building and ask "would you use this?" Now you're just collecting compliments. Save the pitch for a different test (Smoke Test, Pre-Sale).
- ❌ **Leading questions.** "Don't you find it frustrating when…?" elicits agreement, not data. Open-ended "what was that like" or "tell me more" instead.
- ❌ **Talking to friends and your existing customers only.** They love you. They're not the early adopters of a new direction.
- ❌ **Calling 5 interviews "validated."** They Discover. The strongest claim you can make from interviews is "the direction looks plausible enough to invest in stronger validation."
- ❌ **Interviewing 50 people.** If the first 8 didn't produce any pattern, stop. The pattern isn't there; reformulate the segment or the hypothesis.
