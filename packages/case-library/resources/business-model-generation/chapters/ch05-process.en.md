# Process: From Design to Implementation

> **Core argument:** Business model innovation is a continuous five-phase cycle — Mobilize, Understand, Design, Implement, Manage — not a one-time canvas-filling exercise. Each phase has a distinct goal and a primary danger. Knowing the danger is half the prevention.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Having great tools and techniques means nothing without a process for applying them. This chapter provides the operational framework: when to assemble the team, when to research, when to design, when to commit, and — critically — when to step back and adapt. The process is deliberately circular because business models are never "done." The most successful companies treat the canvas as a living map that evolves as the market evolves, not as a document to be created once and framed.

## Core Concepts

### Mobilize — Phase 1

Assemble all the elements for a successful project. Activities: frame project objectives, test initial ideas, plan the effort, assemble the team. Success factors: right mix of people (multi-disciplinary), experience in business model innovation, and clear but not rigid objectives. Primary danger: overestimating the value of the initial idea — the first idea is a starting point, not the answer.

### Understand — Phase 2

Research and analyze everything needed for the design effort. Activities: scan the environment, study potential customers through interviews and observation, interview experts, study previous attempts (what was tried before and why did it fail?), collect ideas and opinions. Success factor: deep understanding that goes beyond surface data to underlying patterns and motivations. Primary danger: analysis paralysis — over-researching and never starting to design.

### Design — Phase 3

Generate and test viable business model options. Activities: brainstorm many model concepts using the canvas, prototype the most promising, test assumptions with customers, select the best for implementation. Success factor: avoiding premature commitment to one idea — maintaining a portfolio of possibilities long enough for evidence to guide selection. Primary danger: watering down bold ideas to make them "acceptable" to the existing organization.

### Implement — Phase 4

Execute the chosen business model in the real world. Activities: communicate the model to everyone who needs to support it, involve key stakeholders in the execution, actually deliver it. Success factor: generating genuine buy-in across the organization, not just top-down mandate. Primary danger: momentum fading after the initial excitement — the "project launched, then nothing happened" syndrome.

### Manage — Phase 5

Set up ongoing management to monitor, evaluate, and adapt the business model over time. Activities: continuously scan the environment for changes, regularly assess each building block's performance, rejuvenate or rethink the model when evidence demands it. Success factor: long-term institutional commitment to the model as a living object. Primary danger: becoming a victim of your own success — the model that made you great becomes the model you can't change, even when the market has moved on.

## Key Arguments

### Business model innovation is a continuous cycle, not a one-time project

The five phases form a loop, not a line. After Manage, new understanding feeds back to new Design iterations. A company that fills in the canvas once and files it away has missed the entire point. The canvas should be revisited, revised, and sometimes torn up completely — not because you did it wrong the first time, but because the world changed since you drew it.

### Each phase has a distinct primary danger — knowing it is half the prevention

Phase 1 (Mobilize): overvaluing the initial idea. Phase 2 (Understand): analysis paralysis. Phase 3 (Design): watering down bold ideas. Phase 4 (Implement): momentum fading. Phase 5 (Manage): victim of your own success, refusing to adapt. These dangers are predictable, which means they are preventable — if you name them upfront and check for them at each phase.

## Logic Flow

The process moves: Mobilize (assemble team, frame objective) → Understand (research customers + environment + prior attempts) → Design (brainstorm → prototype → test → select) → Implement (communicate → execute → measure) → Manage (monitor → evaluate → adapt → back to Design when needed). The flow is logical but not rigid — real projects loop back from Implement to Design, from Design to Understand, and from Manage to anything that needs rethinking.

## Connecting to Practice

When starting a business model innovation project, explicitly name which phase you are in. Don't jump to Design before you've done Understand — the most expensive design mistake is solving the wrong problem. Don't skip to Implement before testing prototypes — the most expensive execution mistake is building something nobody wants. And after implementation, schedule a quarterly "canvas review" where the team revisits every building block and asks: does this still hold? The answer should sometimes be no.

## Key Takeaways

1. The five-phase process (Mobilize → Understand → Design → Implement → Manage) turns business model innovation from a creative act into a repeatable discipline.
2. Every phase has a primary danger. Name it, watch for it, and you've already done half the prevention.
3. If your canvas hasn't changed in 12 months, either your business environment is unusually stable or you're not paying attention.
