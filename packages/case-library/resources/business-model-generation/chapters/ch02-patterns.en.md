# Patterns: Five Business Model Patterns

> **Core argument:** Business model innovation doesn't require reinventing from scratch — five recurring patterns abstract the core structural logic behind superficially different businesses, making innovation teachable and repeatable.

## Why This Chapter Matters

After mastering the canvas, the natural next question is: what makes a great business model? This chapter answers by introducing five repeatable patterns — Unbundling, Long Tail, Multi-Sided Platforms, FREE, and Open Business Models — that appear across industries. Understanding these patterns gives you a mental library of structural options. Instead of asking "what should we sell?", you can ask "could our industry work as a multi-sided platform? Could we unbundle? Could we offer a free tier?" The patterns are not recipes to copy — they are lenses that reveal possibilities.

## Core Concepts

### Unbundling Business Models

Unbundling separates a company into three fundamentally different types of businesses — Customer Relationship management, Product Innovation, and Infrastructure management — because each has different economic, competitive, and cultural imperatives. Customer Relationship businesses want to serve every customer need (scope); Product Innovation businesses want speed and creativity; Infrastructure businesses want scale and efficiency. These three imperatives pull in different directions and cannot all be optimized simultaneously within a single P&L. The signal that unbundling is needed: internal tensions between departments that seem to fight each other more than competitors.

### The Long Tail

Selling a large number of niche items in relatively small quantities, alongside a smaller number of popular items in bulk. Total revenue from the "tail" of many niche products can equal or exceed revenue from the "head" of blockbusters. Two enablers make Long Tail models viable: democratized production tools (anyone can create and publish) and democratized distribution (digital platforms carry near-zero marginal cost per additional SKU). The third requirement is a strong filtering and recommendation engine — without discovery, the tail is just noise.

### Multi-Sided Platforms

Bringing together two or more distinct but interdependent groups of customers, creating value by facilitating interactions between them. The platform's value grows as it attracts more users on each side (network effect). Typically, one side is subsidized (free or cheap) to attract the side that the paying side values most. Examples: Google connects searchers with advertisers (searchers free, advertisers pay), Visa connects merchants with cardholders (cardholders get rewards, merchants pay fees).

### FREE as a Business Model

At least one substantial Customer Segment can continuously benefit from a free-of-charge offer, financed by another part of the business model. Three patterns: multi-sided platform (advertising-based, e.g., Google), freemium (basic free, premium paid — typically 5-10% pay), and bait-and-hook (low-priced initial purchase + high-margin repeat purchases, e.g., Gillette, Nespresso, printers/ink).

### Open Business Models

Systematically collaborating with outside partners to create and capture value. Two directions: outside-in (exploiting external ideas and technologies internally, i.e., open innovation) and inside-out (providing unused internal ideas and assets to external parties, i.e., licensing and spin-offs). The core insight: in a world of widely distributed knowledge, organizations should access external R&D rather than relying solely on internal innovation.

## Key Arguments

### Patterns help designers avoid reinventing the wheel — they are reusable, configurable templates

By studying patterns across industries, designers can recognize which structural logic might apply to their situation, adapt it, and avoid known pitfalls. Patterns abstract the common structural logic behind superficially different businesses — a multi-sided platform pattern applies equally to newspapers (readers + advertisers), credit cards (cardholders + merchants), and operating systems (users + developers).

### Unbundling is inevitable once a company reaches certain scale

Customer Relationship, Product Innovation, and Infrastructure businesses are in fundamental conflict. Private banking illustrates this: relationship managers want to offer clients any product they need (breadth), product developers want speed and creativity, and IT/operations wants standardized efficiency. Trying to optimize all three within one organization necessarily sub-optimizes each. The solution: separate them into distinct business units — or distinct companies — each with its own metrics, culture, and leadership.

### Long Tail models represent a structural shift enabled by technological foundations that didn't exist before the internet

Before digital distribution, shelf space was expensive and limited. Physical stores can stock a few thousand SKUs; Amazon carries millions. The Long Tail is profitable when the platform bears near-zero marginal cost per additional item listed AND when strong discovery tools help customers find niche items they wouldn't have known to search for otherwise.

## Real-World Cases

### Netflix / Amazon / eBay: Long Tail in Practice

Netflix's streaming catalog, Amazon's marketplace with millions of third-party sellers, and eBay's auction listings all demonstrate that the sum of many niche items' revenues competes with — and can exceed — blockbuster revenues. Each platform invested heavily in recommendation engines precisely because discovery is the binding constraint on Long Tail value. Without filters, customers drown in options; with good recommendations, the tail becomes an asset.

### Google / Facebook / Visa: Multi-Sided Platforms

Google connects information seekers with advertisers. Facebook connects social users with advertisers. Visa connects merchants with cardholders. In each case, one side is subsidized (search users, social users, cardholders) and the other side pays (advertisers, merchants). The key strategic question for any platform is: which side is more price-sensitive and needs to be subsidized? And which side most values access to the other side and will pay for it?

### Private Banking: Unbundling

A traditional private bank simultaneously manages client trust relationships, develops new financial products, and runs the IT and transaction backbone. These three businesses conflict: relationship managers want to serve any client need, product innovators want speed, and operations wants standardization and efficiency. Recognizing this as a bundling problem — not a management problem — is the strategic insight.

## Connecting to Practice

When looking at your own business model, ask: does our industry look like one of these patterns? Could it? Specifically: are there two or more distinct customer groups that need each other (multi-sided platform)? Do we have three conflicting priorities pulling in different directions (unbundling candidate)? Can we lower the cost of serving niche segments (Long Tail)? Does one customer segment get value from another segment's participation (free pattern)? Do we have unused IP or capabilities that others would pay for (open business model)?

## Key Takeaways

1. Five patterns — Unbundling, Long Tail, Multi-Sided Platform, FREE, Open Business Models — recur across industries because they solve universal structural problems.
2. Patterns are configurable templates, not recipes. Applying one still requires validation of your specific customers, economics, and competitive context.
3. The most powerful business model innovations often come from recognizing that your industry SHOULD be one pattern but is currently organized as another.
