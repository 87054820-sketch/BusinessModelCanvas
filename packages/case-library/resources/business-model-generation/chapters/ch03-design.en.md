# Design: Techniques and Tools

> **Core argument:** Business model design is a skill, not a talent — six structured techniques (Customer Insights, Ideation, Visual Thinking, Prototyping, Storytelling, Scenarios) make the design process teachable, repeatable, and collaborative.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most business planning assumes one model, builds a case for it, and commits. The design approach inverts this: generate many possibilities first, then narrow. This chapter provides the toolkit for doing exactly that. Customer Insights ground the work in reality. Ideation generates quantity. Visual Thinking makes ideas tangible. Prototyping creates low-cost experiments. Storytelling communicates to stakeholders. Scenarios stress-test against alternative futures. Each technique is a skill that improves with practice, not a talent you either have or don't.

## Core Concepts

### Customer Insights

Building deep understanding of customers to inform business model design. Techniques include empathy maps (what customers see, hear, think, feel, say, do), customer journey mapping (the end-to-end experience), and ethnographic research (observing behavior in context). The core principle: customers are the only credible judge of value, so every design decision must trace back to customer insight, not internal assumption. The most dangerous phrase in design is "I know what customers want" — without evidence it's just a guess.

### Ideation

A creative process for generating a large number of business model ideas and isolating the best ones. Two phases: divergent (generating quantity, deferring judgment — no idea is too wild) and convergent (synthesizing, selecting, refining). Techniques include brainstorming, what-if questions, customer-insight-driven ideation, and using the Canvas as a divergent thinking tool. The goal is not to find the single best idea on the first try — it's to explore the space of possibilities thoroughly.

### Visual Thinking

Using visual tools — sketches, post-it notes, diagrams, drawings — to make business model concepts tangible and discussable. Visuals make complex ideas comprehensible, reveal relationships between elements, and enable shared understanding in groups. A sticky note on a wall is worth a thousand words in a deck.

### Prototyping

Creating low-cost, quick representations of potential business models. Prototypes range from napkin sketches (1 minute, 1 person) to detailed Canvases, to field tests. Each prototype is a hypothesis, not a commitment. The goal is to explore many possibilities before betting on one. The cost of a bad prototype is negligible; the cost of a bad business decision made without prototyping is enormous.

### Storytelling

Using narrative to communicate a business model's logic, engage emotions, and make abstract concepts concrete. Two perspectives: company perspective (how the business creates value — the origin story) and customer perspective (how the customer experiences that value — the journey). Stories make business models memorable and compelling in ways spreadsheets cannot.

### Scenarios

Developing detailed, plausible stories about how the future might unfold, to inform and stress-test business model design. Two types: customer-driven scenarios (placing a business model in specific customer contexts — "how would this model work for a small business owner vs. an enterprise?") and future-environment scenarios (imagining different competitive, regulatory, or technological futures — "what if a competitor does X?"). Scenarios don't predict the future; they prepare you for it.

## Key Arguments

### Design is not a linear process — it is iterative, messy, and requires moving between techniques

The six techniques are used together and iteratively. A team might gather customer insights, ideate alternatives, prototype the most promising ones, use visual thinking to discuss them, tell stories to communicate to stakeholders, and use scenarios to stress-test. The order varies; the combination is what matters. No single technique is sufficient alone.

### Prototyping replaces "the single business plan" with an exploration of many possibilities

Traditional business planning commits to one model based on analysis. The design approach commits to many cheap prototypes, letting evidence and learning narrow the options. This reduces the cost of being wrong and increases the chance of finding a model that actually works.

## Real-World Cases

### Nespresso: Prototyping in Practice

Nestlé's Nespresso went through many business model iterations before finding the one that worked. Early prototypes targeted offices (B2B). Later iterations pivoted to consumers (B2C) with the Nespresso Club model. Each prototype was a learning experiment — the product (capsule + machine) remained the same, but the business model (B2B vs. B2C, retail vs. subscription) made the difference between failure and a multi-billion-euro business.

## Connecting to Practice

For your next business model design session, start with Customer Insights: interview 5-10 potential customers using the empathy map, not a survey. Then Ideate: in a 60-minute session with a multi-disciplinary team, generate at least 20 different business model configurations on the canvas using Post-it notes. Pick 3-5 to Prototype at the napkin-sketch level. Use Visual Thinking throughout — everything on the wall, nothing in your head. Tell the Story of each prototype from the customer's perspective. Finally, use Scenarios to ask: what if our biggest competitor copies us? What if regulation changes?

## Key Takeaways

1. Business model design uses six learnable techniques. None is optional, and none works alone — the combination is the method.
2. Prototyping is the cheapest insurance against committing to a broken business model. Every major decision should be informed by at least one prototype.
3. The most dangerous assumption in design is "I know what customers want." Every technique in this chapter is designed to replace that assumption with evidence.
