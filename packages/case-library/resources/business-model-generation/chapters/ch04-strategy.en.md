# Strategy: Environment, Evaluation, and Portfolio

> **Core argument:** A great business model in a declining environment may fail; a mediocre model in a rising environment may succeed — strategy is the discipline of placing your model in context, evaluating it systematically, and managing multiple models when needed.

## Why This Chapter Matters

The canvas describes your model. Strategy evaluates whether it will survive. This chapter provides four strategic lenses: the Business Model Environment (mapping external forces), evaluating business models (SWOT applied to each building block), Blue Ocean Strategy integration (creating new value curves), and managing multiple business models (the ambidexterity challenge). These are not separate tools — they are layers of analysis that add context, rigor, competitive logic, and portfolio thinking to what would otherwise be a static canvas.

## Core Concepts

### Business Model Environment

A framework for mapping the external context: four areas with detailed forces. Market Forces: segments, needs, switching costs, revenue attractiveness. Industry Forces: competitors, new entrants, substitutes, suppliers, stakeholders. Key Trends: technology, regulatory, societal, socioeconomic. Macroeconomic Forces: global market conditions, capital markets, commodities, infrastructure. The environment framework surfaces what to monitor — it doesn't predict, but it prepares.

### Evaluating Business Models

A structured assessment combining SWOT analysis with forward-looking questions. Two perspectives: overall assessment (SWOT of the entire model as a system) and building-block assessment (SWOT applied to each of the nine blocks individually). Good evaluation goes beyond financial projections: Are the building blocks mutually reinforcing? Is the model hard to copy? Does it have network effects or switching costs?

### Blue Ocean Strategy Integration

Applying Kim and Mauborgne's framework to business model design through two tools: Value Innovation (simultaneously pursuing differentiation and low cost to create new demand) and the Four Actions Framework (eliminate, reduce, raise, create). Rather than competing head-to-head, Blue Ocean asks: what can be eliminated that the industry takes for granted? What can be reduced below standard? What should be raised above standard? What can be created that the industry has never offered?

### Managing Multiple Business Models

Operating more than one business model simultaneously within the same organization. The key challenge: models conflict because they have different cost structures, customer segments, and cultures. Solutions include separate business units with independent P&Ls, or phased integration where one model transitions to the next. Most organizations fail here because they try to manage the new model with the old model's metrics and processes.

## Key Arguments

### Context matters enormously for business model success

A strong business model in a declining environment faces headwinds no internal optimization can overcome. A mediocre model in a rising environment benefits from tailwinds. The Business Model Environment framework makes external forces explicit and mappable — before committing resources, map the environment and check for both tailwinds and headwinds.

### Evaluating business models requires looking beyond financial projections to structural logic

Financial projections are the output of a good model, not the input to evaluating one. A good evaluation asks: are the building blocks self-reinforcing? Is the model difficult to copy? What are the switching costs for customers? Does the model benefit from network effects? These structural questions matter more than the discounted cash flow model.

## Real-World Cases

### Southwest Airlines: Environment + Blue Ocean

Southwest created a fundamentally different business model by applying the Four Actions Framework to the airline industry. They ELIMINATED meals, lounges, and seat choice; REDUCED ticket prices; RAISED on-time performance and friendly service; CREATED point-to-point routes and a standardized fleet (all 737s). The result was a cost structure and value proposition that competitors couldn't match without abandoning their own models. This is Value Innovation: lower cost AND higher perceived value, achieved by changing what was offered, not by doing the same things cheaper.

## Connecting to Practice

When you've completed a first-draft canvas, run these four strategy checks: (1) Map the Business Model Environment — what are the 2-3 most significant external forces affecting each area? (2) Evaluate each building block with SWOT — which blocks are strongest? Where are the fatal weaknesses? (3) Apply Blue Ocean questions — which factors can you eliminate or reduce that your industry takes for granted? Which can you raise or create? (4) If you have more than one business model, map them on a portfolio — do you have today's growth AND tomorrow's options?

## Key Takeaways

1. Strategy is not separate from the canvas — it's additional layers of analysis (environment, evaluation, competitive logic, portfolio) applied to the same nine-block model.
2. Blue Ocean Strategy's Four Actions Framework translates directly into business model innovation: eliminate, reduce, raise, and create building block elements.
3. Managing multiple business models is the hardest strategic challenge. Most organizations kill the new model before it can prove itself by applying old metrics.
