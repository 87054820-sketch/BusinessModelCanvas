# Canvas: The Value Proposition Canvas

> **Core argument:** Great value propositions are designed outward from what matters to customers — their jobs, pains, and gains — not inward from what you think matters about your product.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most teams build before they understand. They fill whiteboards with features and then look for a problem to justify them. The Value Proposition Canvas (VPC) inverts this: it forces you to map the customer's world first — what they need to accomplish, what frustrates them, what outcomes they desire — and only then design your offering to address those specific elements. Without this discipline, value propositions become generic claims like "better user experience" that sound good but relieve no real pain. This chapter introduces the VPC as the operational tool that makes customer insight actionable.

## Core Concepts

### Value Proposition Canvas

The VPC zooms into two BMC building blocks: Value Propositions and Customer Segments. Right side: Customer Profile — a structured description of what you observe about customers. Left side: Value Map — what you design for them. The entire purpose is Fit: when the Value Map addresses the Customer Profile in a way customers recognize as valuable and pay for. Every element on the Value Map side should trace back to a specific job, pain, or gain on the Customer Profile side.

### Customer Profile

Three components. **Jobs**: what customers are trying to accomplish — functional (a task), social (how they want to be perceived), personal/emotional (how they want to feel), supporting (ancillary tasks like comparison shopping). Context matters: a haircut before a wedding is a different job than a haircut before a regular Tuesday. **Pains**: bad outcomes, obstacles, and risks related to jobs. Categories: undesired outcomes, obstacles (things that prevent starting), risks (potential negative outcomes). Severity ranges from moderate to extreme. **Gains**: outcomes customers want. Categories: required (baseline), expected (basic improvement), desired (goes beyond), unexpected (would surprise positively). Relevance ranges from nice-to-have to essential.

### Value Map

Also three components. **Products & Services**: what you sell — tangible, intangible, digital, financial. **Pain Relievers**: how your offerings address specific customer pains by reducing or eliminating them. Must link to specific pains. **Gain Creators**: how your offerings create specific customer gains. Must link to specific gains. A Value Map has meaning only in the context of matching a specific Customer Profile.

### Fit

Three stages of Fit, each requiring different evidence. **Problem-Solution Fit**: on paper — evidence you've identified real jobs/pains/gains and your solution logically addresses them. **Product-Market Fit**: in the market — evidence customers actually want your solution (traction, adoption, willingness to pay). **Business Model Fit**: in the bank — evidence the model is scalable and profitable.

### B2B Stakeholder Mapping

For B2B, a single customer segment may include multiple stakeholders — influencers, recommenders, economic buyers, decision-makers, end users, saboteurs — each with different jobs, pains, and gains. The job of purchasing software is performed by a procurement manager whose pains (legal risk, budget overrun) are different from the end user's pains (slow interface, missing features).

## Key Arguments

### Great value propositions are designed around what matters to customers, not what you think matters

The Customer Profile forces design outward from the customer. Pain Relievers and Gain Creators have meaning only relative to specific jobs, pains, and gains. A feature that doesn't relieve a real customer pain or create a real customer gain is irrelevant — regardless of how clever the engineering is.

### Fit is not binary — it evolves through three distinct stages with different evidence standards

Problem-Solution Fit requires low-cost evidence (interviews, observations). Product-Market Fit requires market signals (traction, early sales). Business Model Fit requires financial evidence (unit economics, scalability). Each stage demands the right kind of evidence, not just more of the same.

## Real-World Cases

### Movie Theater: Job Context Changes Everything

The same person buying a movie ticket has entirely different jobs, pains, and gains depending on context. Date night: social job (impress partner), pain (long queue kills the mood), gain (great seats, memorable experience). Kids' afternoon: functional job (entertain children for 2 hours), pain (kids get bored during ads), gain (convenient timing, kid-friendly snacks). You cannot design a value proposition for "the moviegoer" — you must design for specific segments in specific contexts.

## Connecting to Practice

When building your first Customer Profile, interview 5-10 real customers — not colleagues, not friends — and map their jobs, pains, and gains on sticky notes. Then rank every item: which jobs are crucial vs. trivial? Which pains are extreme vs. moderate? Which gains are essential vs. nice-to-have? The ranking step is the most important and most frequently skipped. Only then draft your Value Map.

## Key Takeaways

1. Design from the customer outward — map Jobs, Pains, Gains before designing any feature or solution.
2. Ranking matters more than listing. Not all jobs are crucial, not all pains are extreme, not all gains are essential. Prioritize ruthlessly.
3. Fit has three stages (Problem-Solution, Product-Market, Business Model). Match your testing approach and evidence standard to the stage you're in.
