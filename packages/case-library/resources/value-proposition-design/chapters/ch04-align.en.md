# Align: From Value Proposition to Business Model

> **Core argument:** A validated value proposition without a viable business model is a hobby, not a business. Alignment bridges the VPC to the BMC by translating customer validation into operational and financial viability.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Many startups achieve Product-Market Fit (customers want this) but fail on business model (unit economics don't work, cost structure too heavy, no competitive moat). This chapter provides the framework for that transition — the seven business model questions, the improve-vs-invent spectrum, and practical guidance for running innovation in established organizations.

## Core Concepts

### 7 Business Model Questions
(1) Switching costs — how easy to leave? (2) Recurring revenue — earn before you spend? (3) Cash flow cycle — earn vs. spend timing? (4) Game-changing cost structure — structurally better than competitors? (5) Others who do the work — do partners/customers do your heavy lifting? (6) Scalability — grow without proportional cost increase? (7) Protection from competition — moats?

### Improve vs. Invent Spectrum
Not every value proposition is a new invention. Many are improvements to existing models. Understanding where your work sits on this spectrum determines your organizational approach and risk profile.

### Designing in Established Organizations
Existing companies face "organizational antibodies" — processes, metrics, and culture that protect the current model and kill new ones. Solutions: separate units with different success metrics, senior sponsorship, and explicit protection from the core business's resource allocation process.

## Real-World Cases

### Hilti: Fleet Management Model
Hilti shifted from selling power tools to providing a tool fleet management subscription — construction companies pay monthly and always have the right tools on site. This value proposition pivot required a complete business model change: new Revenue Stream (subscription), Customer Relationships (account manager, not retailer), Key Resources (fleet tracking software), and Key Activities (maintenance logistics). You cannot sell a subscription value proposition through a one-time-sale business model.

## Connecting to Practice

When you achieve Product-Market Fit, run the 7 Questions before scaling. If you score poorly on multiple questions, redesign the business model before pouring fuel on the growth fire. An unprofitable model at small scale will be unprofitable at large scale too.

## Key Takeaways
1. Value proposition validation and business model validation are separate activities. Both are required.
2. The 7 Questions are a quick diagnostic. Poor scores mean the model, not the product, needs fixing.
3. Established organizations kill innovation not from malice but from immune system reflex. Protect new ventures structurally.

## Alignment Mechanics

A value proposition becomes a business only when every promise to the customer has a corresponding operating and economic answer. The Value Proposition Canvas tells the team whether the customer profile and value map fit; the Business Model Canvas asks whether the fit can be delivered repeatedly, profitably, and defensibly. The seven questions are therefore not a generic checklist. They reveal whether the validated value proposition creates switching costs, recurring revenue, a favorable cash-flow cycle, a structurally better cost base, leverage from partners or customers doing part of the work, scalability, and protection from competition.

## Workshop Design for Innovation

The chapter also treats alignment as a workshop and governance problem. Teams need a clear challenge, a mix of inside and outside perspectives, visible canvases, rapid prototypes, explicit assumptions, and quick escalation of decisions. A workshop that produces sticky notes but no model decision is innovation theater. A useful workshop links each customer insight to a business-model implication: which revenue stream changes, which key activity becomes critical, which partner must be recruited, which cost driver will break the model, and which metric will tell the team whether to scale.

## Nuance: Improve or Invent

Not every opportunity requires a new business model. Some value propositions fit the current model with small improvements; others require invention. The danger is not choosing one side or the other, but failing to name the choice. Established organizations are especially prone to the improvement trap: existing metrics reward the core model, so invented models are judged too early by the wrong criteria. Alignment means explicitly deciding what can stay, what must change, and what must be protected from the core business while it is still learning.
