# Test: Reducing Uncertainty Through Experiments

> **Core argument:** Testing is not optional — it's the difference between building something nobody wants versus something customers love. The experiment library, test cards, and progress board make testing systematic.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most new ventures fail because they build first and test later — or never test at all. This chapter provides the operational toolkit for validating value propositions before committing significant resources. It covers what to test, how to test, what kind of evidence each test produces, and how to avoid the five data traps that fool even experienced innovators.

## Core Concepts

### 10 Testing Principles
(1) Evidence trumps opinion. (2) Learn faster by embracing productive failure. (3) Test early, refine later. (4) Experiments are reality checks. (5) Balance learning with vision. (6) Find idea killers — locate fatal flaws fast. (7) Test desirability before feasibility or viability. (8) Make it measurable. (9) Not all evidence is equal — some is stronger. (10) Test twice, launch once.

### Three Testing Tiers
**Inner circle**: Customer Jobs, Pains, Gains — test desirability. **Middle square**: Value Proposition — test whether your solution addresses what customers need. **Outer rectangle**: Business Model — test viability at scale.

### Test Card & Learning Card
**Test Card** structure: Hypothesis (we believe that...), Test (to verify we will...), Metric (and measure...), Criteria (we are right if...). **Learning Card**: Hypothesis (we believed), Observation (we observed), Insight (from that we learned), Action (therefore we will).

### 5 Data Traps
False Positive (test says pass but it's false), False Negative (test says fail but it's true), Local Maximum (optimizing within current frame, missing bigger opportunity), Exhausted Maximum (market saturated), Wrong Data (measuring something irrelevant).

### Experiment Library
Techniques from low to high certainty: Call to Action, tracking ads, unique links, datasheet MVP, brochure, storyboard, landing page, product box, video MVP, learning prototype, Wizard of Oz MVP, Concierge MVP, crowdfunding, pre-sale.

## Key Arguments

### Match experiment type to certainty stage, not budget
Early-stage (Problem-Solution Fit) needs low-cost experiments (interviews, landing pages). Late-stage needs higher-certainty experiments (pilots, pre-sales). Don't use expensive experiments for early questions, and don't use cheap experiments for late-stage decisions.

### Testing can become procrastination — the goal is reducing major risks to the point of commitment, not eliminating all uncertainty
Not every hypothesis needs a formal test. Trivial hypotheses should be tested; established facts don't need testing. The progress board shows when enough evidence has accumulated to commit.

## Real-World Cases

### Kickstarter as Testing Platform
Crowdfunding combines demand validation (will people pay?), price discovery (at what price?), and early customer acquisition — all before manufacturing. A successful campaign validates desirability and viability in a single experiment.

## Key Takeaways
1. Test desirability first, then feasibility, then viability. Jumping to viability testing before proving desirability wastes resources.
2. Match evidence strength to decision risk. A customer saying "I would buy" is weak evidence. A customer paying for a pre-sale is strong evidence.
3. The five data traps are predictable. Name them and check for them in every experiment.
