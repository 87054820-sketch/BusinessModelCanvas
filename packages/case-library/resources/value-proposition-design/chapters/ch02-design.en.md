# Design: From Customer Profile to Value Map

> **Core argument:** Designing value propositions is a skill that improves with deliberate practice — structured prototyping, push-pull innovation logic, and customer understanding techniques make the design process systematic rather than intuitive.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Chapter 1 gave you the VPC structure. This chapter gives you the design process — how to go from observing customers to creating a compelling value proposition. It introduces ten prototyping principles, the push vs. pull framework, six customer understanding techniques, and the Ad-lib template. These are the operational tools that turn customer insight into designed value.

## Core Concepts

### 10 Prototyping Principles

(1) Make it visual and tangible. (2) Embrace a beginner's mind — assume you don't know. (3) Don't fall in love with first ideas — create alternatives. (4) Be comfortable in uncertainty. (5) Start low fidelity, iterate higher — napkin sketch before polished deck. (6) Expose early to criticism, not praise. (7) Fail fast, forward — each failure is a learning step. (8) Use techniques from other creative disciplines. (9) Shape ideas with business model thinking. (10) Everything is a prototype — mindset shift.

### Push vs. Pull Innovation

**Push**: technology or capability pushes a new solution into the market (invention-driven). **Pull**: customer need pulls a solution into existence (market-driven). Most successful innovations combine both — a capability looking for a real customer need, or a customer need that finds an enabling capability. Pure push without pull produces solutions looking for problems. Pure pull without push leaves needs unmet.

### 10 Characteristics of Successful Value Propositions

Successful VPs are: (1) embedded in great business models, (2) focused on jobs/pains/gains customers care about most, (3) focused on unsatisfied jobs and unresolved pains, (4) targeting few elements but doing them extremely well, (5) going beyond functional to emotional and social jobs, (6) aligned with how customers measure success, (7) addressing needs many people have — or a few will pay a lot for, (8) differentiated from competition on factors customers care about, (9) outperforming on at least one dimension, (10) difficult to copy.

### 6 Customer Understanding Techniques

**Data Detective**: analyze existing data. **Journalist**: interview customers. **Anthropologist**: observe in context. **Actor**: role-play as customer. **Co-creator**: involve customers in design. **Scientist**: experiment with customers. Use multiple techniques — what customers say (Journalist) often differs from what they do (Anthropologist).

### Ad-lib Value Proposition

A fill-in-the-blanks formula: "Our (products/services) help (customer segment) who want to (jobs) by (pain reliever) and (gain creator), unlike (competitor alternative)." This template forces specificity.

## Key Arguments

### Fast feedback is more important than initial idea quality

Low-fidelity prototypes (napkin sketches, Ad-libs) are designed to seek criticism early. The goal is to find why an idea WON'T work as fast as possible, so you can pivot to one that will, rather than investing months in a single unvalidated concept.

## Real-World Cases

### Azuri/Eight19: Solar for Rural Africa

Eight19 developed low-cost solar technology, but the business model was the innovation. Pay-as-you-go solar electricity for rural African households without grid access. The job: light homes at night. The pain: spending money on kerosene that pollutes and is expensive. The gain: safe, clean, affordable light. The business model (pay-as-you-go via mobile payments) made the value proposition work. Technology was the easier part; the business model made it accessible.

## Connecting to Practice

After drafting a value proposition, use the Ad-lib template to force it into a single sentence. If you can't fill the blanks with specifics, your proposition is too vague. Then build a napkin-sketch prototype and show it to 3 potential customers for feedback. Fix what they criticize, then show it to 3 more.

## Key Takeaways

1. Design is systematic, not mystical. The Ad-lib template, prototyping principles, and customer techniques form a repeatable toolkit.
2. Prototype low and fast. The goal of a prototype is criticism, not approval.
3. Push without pull builds things nobody needs. Pull without push misses what technology makes newly possible.
