# Reading map — Value Proposition Design

Read this right after a first BMC draft, especially when `Customer Segments` and `Value Proposition` still sound generic. It turns “who is the customer?” and “what value do we provide?” into a structure that can be designed, tested, and evolved.

## What problem it solves

- Value propositions become generic claims such as faster, cheaper, or better experience.
- Customer research collects opinions but does not become product, service, or business-model hypotheses.
- Teams treat pains as facts without separating observation, interpretation, and assumption.
- Experiment results do not feed back into the VPC and BMC.

## Full-book reading map

1. **Canvas** — Customer Profile: Jobs, Pains, Gains; Value Map: Products & Services, Pain Relievers, Gain Creators; Fit across customer and value sides.
2. **Design** — prototype many value propositions, use design constraints, research customers, compare competitors, and connect the proposition to the business model.
3. **Test** — extract hypotheses, prioritize what could kill the idea, design experiments, capture learning, and avoid data traps.
4. **Evolve** — align the organization, measure and monitor fit, improve relentlessly, and reinvent when customer behavior or context shifts.

## Case index from the full book

- `nespresso` — appears as a razor-blade / recurring consumable design constraint. It teaches that a value proposition can be inseparable from the revenue model and switching/lock-in mechanism.
- `airbnb` — appears as a platform design constraint. It teaches that each side of a platform needs its own value proposition and that the model only works when both sides are present.
- `alibaba-group` / Taobao — appears in the Evolve chapter. It teaches how a platform value proposition changes as trust, payment, logistics, sellers, and buyers evolve.
- **Azuri / Eight19** — strong candidate for a future case. It teaches the back-and-forth between solar technology, rural customer pains, payment design, and viable business model.
- **Owlet** — strong candidate for a future case. It teaches systematic testing, pivoting from hospital/nurse buyers to worried parents, and using interviews, landing pages, and price tests.
- **Hilti, Swatch, Southwest, Apple, Dropbox, Marriott** — useful examples for design constraints, competitor comparison, or testing methods, but not enough to become PinGarden cases in this pass.

## What to read first

- If you are **defining customers**, start with Customer Profile and avoid jumping to features.
- If you are **designing a product**, read Value Map and check whether each feature relieves a pain or creates a gain.
- If you are **connecting value to business model**, read the Azuri section in Design 2.5.
- If you are **validating fit**, read the Test chapter and the Owlet section.
- If you are **managing an evolving platform**, read the Taobao section in Evolve.

## Common misreadings

- **VPC is not a feature list.** It is a fit map between customer work and value creation.
- **Customer statements are not enough evidence.** Interviews and surveys are often weak evidence and need behavioral tests.
- **One product may need many VPCs.** Platforms, enterprise services, and ecosystems usually serve multiple customer profiles.
- **Case mentions are not automatically case-library entries.** A brand becomes a PinGarden case only when it can teach a method with enough context and public evidence.
