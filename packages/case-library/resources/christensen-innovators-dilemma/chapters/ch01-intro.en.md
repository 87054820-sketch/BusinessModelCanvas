# Why Great Companies Fail: The Innovator's Dilemma

> **Core argument:** The very management practices that make companies industry leaders — listening to best customers, investing in highest-margin opportunities, pursuing larger markets — also cause them to miss disruptive technologies. Good management can be the root cause of failure.

## Why This Chapter Matters

This chapter poses the book's foundational puzzle: why do well-managed companies systematically fail when confronted with certain types of technological change? Christensen disproves the "technology mudslide hypothesis" (that relentless change makes it impossible to stay on top). The real cause is more specific and more disturbing: leading firms fail precisely when they do everything their best management practices tell them to do. Understanding this paradox is the prerequisite for the entire book.

## Core Concepts

### Technology Mudslide Hypothesis
The belief that relentless technological change makes it impossible for firms to stay on top. Christensen disproved this: neither the pace nor the difficulty of change caused failures.

### Sustaining Technologies
Technologies that sustain established performance trajectories. They make good products better in ways existing customers value. Incumbents nearly always lead in sustaining innovation.

### Disruptive Technologies
Technologies that are simpler, cheaper, and initially underperform on dimensions mainstream customers value. They offer a different value proposition for emerging or overlooked markets. Entrants nearly always lead here.

### The Innovator's Dilemma
The paradox: the same management practices that make companies leaders (customer listening, margin discipline, market size focus) cause them to miss disruptive technologies. This is not a failure of execution — it's a structural feature of good management.

### RPV Theory
An organization's capabilities are defined by Resources (what it has), Processes (how it works), and Values (what it prioritizes). These define what it can and cannot do — far more than resources alone.

## Key Arguments

### Good management — not bad management — causes failure at disruptive technology
Firms listened to their best customers (who didn't want the simpler tech), invested in highest-margin opportunities (disruption starts low-margin), and pursued larger markets (disruption starts small). Each decision was individually rational. Collectively, they were fatal.

### The technology mudslide hypothesis is wrong
In every sustaining technology change (radical or incremental), established firms led. In every disruptive change, entrants won. The pattern was consistent across 5 HDD architectural generations. The cause was structural, not competence-based.

## Cases

### HDD Industry 1965-1995
Five architectural generations (14" → 8" → 5.25" → 3.5" → 2.5"). Each generation's leaders never won the next. Incumbents led in sustaining innovation (better drives for existing customers). Entrants led in disruptive innovation (smaller drives for new applications). No exceptions across 5 generations — the pattern is structural.

## Connecting to Practice

When you hear about a new technology that underperforms on the dimensions your best customers care about, don't dismiss it. Ask: who might value its different attributes? Is it improving on a trajectory that could eventually meet mainstream needs? The most dangerous dismissal is "our customers don't want it."

## Key Takeaways
1. Disruption is not about bad management — it's a paradox where good management causes failure.
2. The HDD industry pattern (5 generations, no exceptions) proves this is structural, not accidental.
3. Your best customers can give you the worst strategic advice when a disruptive technology emerges.
