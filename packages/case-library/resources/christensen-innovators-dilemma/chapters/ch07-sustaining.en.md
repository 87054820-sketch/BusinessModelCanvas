# Sustaining vs. Disruptive: The Crucial Distinction

> **Core argument:** Sustaining and disruptive innovations require opposite managerial responses. Incumbents should use the mainstream organization to win sustaining battles, but must not manage disruptive opportunities by the same customers, metrics, and processes.

## Why This Chapter Matters

The word disruption is often used too loosely. Christensen's distinction is sharper. A sustaining innovation can be technologically radical; if it helps existing customers get better performance on dimensions they already value, it is sustaining. A disruptive innovation can look crude and unimpressive; if it creates a foothold outside mainstream demand and improves toward the mainstream, it is disruptive. Confusing the two produces bad strategy.

## Core Concepts

### Sustaining Innovation

Sustaining innovation improves established products for mainstream customers. It may be incremental or breakthrough. The defining feature is not technical novelty but fit with the existing value network. Incumbents usually win here because their resources, processes, customer relationships, and profit logic are designed for exactly this contest.

### Disruptive Innovation

Disruptive innovation begins with a different value proposition. It is initially inferior on the performance attributes mainstream customers demand, but attractive to a foothold market. It then improves enough to invade larger segments. Incumbents usually lose if they force it through mainstream priorities.

### Strategic Misclassification

Misclassification is dangerous in both directions. Treating every threat as disruption wastes resources and creates panic. Treating a true disruption as ordinary sustaining competition lets it grow below the radar until response is too late.

## Key Arguments

### Incumbents usually win sustaining battles

When customers ask for better performance, incumbents have advantages: installed relationships, engineering depth, capital, channels, and organizational focus. The mainstream business is the right vehicle for these battles.

### Incumbents usually lose disruptive battles inside the mainstream business

The disruptive project looks small, low-margin, and technically inferior. Mainstream processes ask mainstream customers, and those customers reject it. Mainstream values demand margins and growth that the foothold cannot offer. The project dies of rational neglect.

## Real-World Cases

### Sustaining improvement vs. disruptive entry

In disk drives, better heads and capacity improvements were sustaining; smaller form factors were disruptive. In excavators, improvements to cable machines were sustaining; hydraulics began disruptively. In steel, integrated mills improved high-grade output while mini-mills entered below. The same industry can contain both kinds of innovation at once.

## Connecting to Practice

Before choosing a response, classify the threat. If it improves what your best customers already value, run it through the mainstream business and compete hard. If it is worse on mainstream metrics but better for a foothold, protect it from mainstream evaluation. Give it customers who need it, a cost structure that fits it, and metrics appropriate to its stage.

## Key Takeaways

1. Radical technology is not automatically disruptive.
2. Sustaining innovation improves along mainstream customer dimensions; incumbents usually win.
3. Disruptive innovation begins outside mainstream acceptance; incumbents usually lose if they manage it conventionally.
4. Correct classification determines the organizational response.
5. Loose use of the word disruption destroys its usefulness.
