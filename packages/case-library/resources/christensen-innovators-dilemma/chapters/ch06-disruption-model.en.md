# The Disruption Model: Three-Part Test

> **Core argument:** Disruption is a narrow process, not a synonym for success. A true disruptive threat starts in a foothold market, is initially inferior on mainstream performance, and improves fast enough to become good enough for mainstream customers.

## Why This Chapter Matters

After several cases, the book needs a diagnostic. Without one, every surprising competitor, new technology, or fast-growing company gets called disruptive. Christensen's model is stricter. It asks where the entrant begins, how mainstream customers judge the offering at first, and whether the entrant's performance trajectory can intersect mainstream demand. This chapter turns stories into a test managers can actually use.

## Core Concepts

### Three-Part Disruption Test

A case counts as disruption only if three conditions are present. First, the entrant begins in a foothold market: low-end overserved customers or nonconsumers. Second, the offering is initially inferior on the attributes mainstream customers value. Third, the technology or business model improves along a trajectory that eventually satisfies mainstream needs.

### Foothold Market

The foothold is where the entrant can survive before the mainstream wants it. It may be a low-end segment that does not need the incumbent's full performance, or a new market of customers who previously could not consume. The foothold gives the entrant revenue, learning, and a protected space.

### Performance Provided vs. Market Demand

Technologies often improve faster than customers can use. Once performance overshoots demand, customers become willing to accept simpler, cheaper, or more convenient alternatives. This is the opening through which disruptive entrants move upward.

## Key Arguments

### Missing one condition means it is not disruption

A better product sold to the same customers may be sustaining competition. A breakthrough invention may be radical but sustaining. A cheap competitor with no path upmarket may remain low-end competition. The disruption label is useful only when the process conditions exist together.

### Performance oversupply creates opportunity

When incumbents continue improving beyond what many customers can use, they create space for entrants that are worse on the old metric but better on simplicity, cost, access, or convenience. The market slowly changes the basis of competition.

## Real-World Cases

### HDD / excavator / steel synthesis

Smaller disk drives, hydraulic excavators, and steel mini-mills look different on the surface, but they share the same structure. Each began in a context mainstream leaders discounted. Each underperformed on mainstream requirements. Each improved until it could attack markets the incumbent cared about. The synthesis is what makes the model predictive.

## Connecting to Practice

Use the three-part test before allocating resources. Identify the potential foothold. Write down the mainstream performance dimension on which the entrant is weak. Estimate whether its improvement trajectory can reach what mainstream customers need. If the answer is yes, the correct response is not to force the project through mainstream metrics; it is to create a context where the foothold is attractive.

## Key Takeaways

1. Disruption is a process with three conditions, not a compliment for a successful company.
2. The foothold market is where the entrant can survive while the mainstream rejects it.
3. Initial inferiority on mainstream dimensions is part of the signal, not a reason to ignore the threat.
4. Performance trajectories matter because technologies can become good enough faster than incumbents expect.
5. The model's value is diagnostic discipline: do not overuse the word disruption.
