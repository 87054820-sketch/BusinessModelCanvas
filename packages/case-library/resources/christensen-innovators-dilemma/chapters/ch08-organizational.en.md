# Organizational Dimensions: Structure Follows Strategy

> **Core argument:** Successful companies do not fail at disruption because they lack resources. They fail because their processes and values reject small, low-margin, uncertain opportunities; disruptive projects need an organizational home whose customers, cost structure, and metrics fit the opportunity.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Once managers diagnose disruption, the question becomes organizational: where should the project live? Christensen's answer is uncomfortable. Putting a disruptive project inside the mainstream business usually kills it, even with executive sponsorship. The parent can supply money, talent, and technology, but its processes and values still define what is worth doing. A project serving a tiny, low-margin foothold cannot compete fairly against sustaining projects serving powerful customers.

## Core Concepts

### RPV Theory: Resources, Processes, Values

A firm's capabilities and disabilities come from **Resources**, **Processes**, and **Values**. Resources are people, technology, cash, brand, data, relationships, and equipment. Processes are how work gets done: budgeting, development, sales, planning, hiring, and decision routines. Values are the criteria by which the organization decides which customers, margins, markets, and projects are attractive. Resources are easiest to move; processes and values are hardest.

### Separate Organization

A disruptive opportunity often needs a separate organization whose customers actually need the new technology. Separation is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It protects the project from parent processes that ask the wrong customers and parent values that demand the wrong margins.

### Match Organization Size to Market Size

A $50 million emerging market is meaningless to a $5 billion company but transformative for a small unit. If the organization is too large, the opportunity cannot move its growth needle and will be starved. The unit pursuing disruption must be small enough that small wins matter.

## Key Arguments

### Mainstream units kill disruptive projects rationally

Mainstream units rank opportunities by current customers, near-term revenue, margins, and scale. A disruptive foothold fails those tests. The project is delayed, underfunded, or redirected toward mainstream requirements that ruin its original advantage.

### Processes and values matter more than resources

A company can assign excellent engineers and capital and still fail. If the budgeting process rejects small markets, the sales process cannot reach foothold customers, and the values demand high margins, resources cannot save the project. The organization is optimized for a different job.

## Real-World Cases

### Separate unit prescription

Christensen recommends giving disruptive technologies to organizations whose customers need them. The parent may fund and protect the unit, but the unit must have its own customers, cost structure, decision criteria, and growth expectations. The goal is to create a context in which the disruptive business looks attractive rather than defective.

## Connecting to Practice

For every disruptive opportunity, diagnose conflict with the parent organization. Do existing customers want it? Does the margin fit? Is the market big enough to matter? Do existing processes know how to sell, build, and support it? If several answers are no, do not solve the problem with more executive attention. Create a unit, partnership, or acquisition structure whose values fit the opportunity.

## Key Takeaways

1. Capabilities and disabilities come from resources, processes, and values.
2. Resources can move; processes and values often cannot.
3. Disruptive projects usually need customers who already need the new value proposition.
4. The unit must be sized so that small emerging markets matter.
5. Separation works only when the new organization has appropriate metrics, cost structure, and autonomy.
