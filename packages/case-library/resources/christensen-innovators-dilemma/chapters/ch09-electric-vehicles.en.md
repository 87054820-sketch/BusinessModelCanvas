# Electric Vehicles: The Disruption Lens Applied

> **Core argument:** The electric-vehicle case demonstrates how disruption theory should be used prospectively: identify the first market that values the new attributes, accept initial inferiority on mainstream metrics, and build a trajectory before attacking the core.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most theories look persuasive after the fact. Christensen's electric-vehicle discussion is different because it applies the framework to a live, uncertain technology. At the time, electric vehicles were limited by range, batteries, speed, and mainstream customer expectations. The point is not to predict every winner. The point is to show how a manager should reason before the historical data is complete.

## Core Concepts

### Disruption Lens Applied Prospectively

A prospective diagnosis uses theory before the outcome is obvious. Instead of asking whether electric vehicles can immediately satisfy mainstream car buyers, the disruption lens asks where they might be good enough now, what dimensions they improve on, and how their trajectory could evolve.

### EV Foothold Markets

Early EV footholds are not necessarily highway commuting or long-distance family cars. They may be local delivery, fleet routes, urban use, regulated zones, or other jobs where short range, lower emissions, mechanical simplicity, or depot charging matter more than maximum range and refueling convenience.

### Entrant Trajectory vs. Incumbent Metrics

Internal-combustion vehicles define mainstream metrics: range, acceleration, refueling speed, durability, dealer service, and total convenience. EVs may initially look inferior on these dimensions. But if batteries, cost, reliability, and charging improve, the trajectory can shift what customers consider good enough.

## Key Arguments

### Good theory helps before proof is obvious

If managers wait until disruptive evidence is obvious, entrants may already own the foothold and learning curve. The theory's value is to ask disciplined questions early: Where is the foothold? What makes the new product inferior? What improvement trajectory might close the gap?

### Do not force a disruptive technology into the mainstream first

A technology that is weak for long-distance highway commuting may be strong for short-range fleet use. Forcing it into mainstream expectations too early produces disappointment and underinvestment. Starting where it is already useful creates learning and momentum.

## Real-World Cases

### Electric Vehicles (1997 prediction)

The EV case was written before the modern EV market was visible. Christensen examines whether electric vehicles might be disruptive relative to internal combustion. The likely answer depends on whether they can find early markets that value their distinctive attributes and whether their performance can improve fast enough to reach broader demand. The lesson is not that a specific company is guaranteed to win; it is that the framework guides market entry and organizational design.

## Connecting to Practice

When applying the theory to an emerging technology, resist the mainstream comparison as the only test. Write the mainstream metrics, then write the new technology's different strengths. Find customers whose job makes those strengths matter now. Create a business model and organization sized to that foothold. Then track the improvement trajectory honestly. If the trajectory cannot reach mainstream demand, the opportunity may remain niche; if it can, prepare for upmarket movement.

## Key Takeaways

1. Disruption theory is most valuable before the outcome is obvious.
2. EVs should be judged first by the job and foothold market that values their attributes.
3. Initial inferiority on mainstream metrics does not end the analysis.
4. Market entry, metrics, and organization must fit the foothold.
5. The theory predicts a process and set of questions, not a guaranteed company winner.
