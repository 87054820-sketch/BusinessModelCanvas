# Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance

Porter's 1985 sequel builds directly on the structural foundation of *Competitive Strategy* (1980). The pivot: from industry structure → firm-level activity analysis. The book's central claim is that competitive advantage grows out of the *discrete activities* a firm performs — not from seeing the firm as a black box or an abstract "position."

## The Value Chain (Ch. 2)

The value chain decomposes every firm into strategically relevant activities:

**Primary Activities:**
1. **Inbound Logistics** — receiving, storing, distributing inputs
2. **Operations** — transforming inputs into final product
3. **Outbound Logistics** — collecting, storing, distributing the product to buyers
4. **Marketing & Sales** — inducing purchase and enabling it
5. **Service** — maintaining or enhancing product value after sale

**Support Activities:**
1. **Firm Infrastructure** — general management, planning, finance, legal, quality
2. **Human Resource Management** — recruiting, hiring, training, compensation
3. **Technology Development** — R&D, process design, product design
4. **Procurement** — purchasing inputs used in the firm's value chain

**Margin** — the difference between total value and the collective cost of performing all activities.

The chain reveals *linkages* — how one activity's cost or performance affects another's. Optimising individual activities in isolation often destroys margin; the real lever is managing the linkages. The `porters-value-chain` canvas in PinGarden maps these exact linkages visually.

## Cost Advantage (Ch. 3)

Cost advantage is not about the whole firm — it's about the cost *drivers* of each discrete activity:
- **Economies of scale** — spreading fixed costs over higher volume
- **Learning and spillovers** — cumulative volume reduces unit cost
- **Capacity utilisation** — running closer to full capacity
- **Linkages** within the value chain and with suppliers/channels
- **Interrelationships** — sharing activities across business units
- **Vertical integration** — make vs. buy decisions
- **Timing** — first-mover advantages or disadvantages
- **Location** — factor costs, logistics, government, infrastructure
- **Institutional factors** — regulation, unions, tariffs

A firm achieves cost leadership by configuring its value chain so that the cumulative cost of performing all activities is lower than competitors'. This is *not* the same as "being the cheapest" — it's about activity-level economics.

## Differentiation (Ch. 4)

Differentiation arises from *how* a firm performs its value chain activities, not from "marketing" or "brand." Every activity is a potential source of uniqueness. Porter distinguishes:

- **Use criteria** — what makes the product actually more valuable to the buyer (quality, features, delivery time, service)
- **Signalling criteria** — what the buyer uses to *infer* value (reputation, advertising, packaging, appearance, price itself)

A differentiation strategy must narrow the gap between signalling and actual use criteria — otherwise it become hollow.

## Technology and Competitive Advantage (Ch. 5)

Technology affects advantage through its impact on *cost drivers* and *uniqueness drivers* at the activity level. Porter introduces the technology value chain — every activity employs some technology, and the *pattern* of technological change within the industry value chain is what determines whether technology shifts competitive advantage.

## Competitor Selection (Ch. 6)

Not all competitors are bad. "Good competitors" can:
- Absorb demand fluctuations
- Increase perceived differentiation of the leader
- Serve unattractive segments the leader doesn't want
- Provide a cost umbrella
- Improve bargaining position with labour and government
- Motivate internal effort in the firm

The strategist's job is not to eliminate competitors but to *select* which ones to coexist with and which to attack.

## Substitution (Ch. 8)

Substitution is the process by which one product or service replaces another in performing a particular buyer function. Porter provides the most rigorous framework for analysing substitution threats ever written — go read Ch. 8 before using the Five Forces' "threat of substitutes" as a throwaway bullet point.
