# Foundations of Scenario Planning

> **Core argument:** Scenario planning is not prediction. It is a disciplined performance intervention that expands mental models, improves decisions under uncertainty, and helps organizations rehearse multiple plausible futures before they are forced to act.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Thomas Chermack's contribution is to bring scenario planning out of the realm of inspirational foresight stories and into performance-based organizational practice. Popular accounts often emphasize memorable cases or creative future narratives. This chapter asks a harder question: what does scenario planning actually do inside people, teams, and systems? The answer begins with cognition. Organizations perform through decisions; decisions are shaped by mental models; mental models are often too narrow for uncertainty. Scenario planning improves performance by stretching those models before events make the old assumptions obsolete.

## Core Concepts

### Scenario Planning

Scenario planning is a structured process for exploring multiple plausible futures. It does not attempt to predict the future or pick the most likely forecast. Instead, it asks managers to examine alternative futures that are internally coherent, strategically relevant, and different enough to challenge current assumptions. The product is not merely a set of stories; it is a better conversation about what could happen and how the organization might respond.

### Performance-Based Scenario Planning

Performance-based scenario planning links the process to organizational outcomes. It asks what performance issue the scenario project is meant to address, what learning is required, how decisions should improve, and how results will be assessed. This turns scenario work from a one-off workshop into an intervention aimed at better strategic agility, decision quality, and preparedness.

### Mental Models

A mental model is an internal representation of how the world works. Leaders use mental models to filter signals, interpret change, and select action. Scenario planning deliberately challenges these models by making assumptions visible and placing leaders inside several plausible futures. The value appears when people can see options, risks, and weak signals they previously ignored.

## Key Arguments

### Scenario planning improves performance by changing thinking first

Strategic action follows interpretation. If managers interpret the environment through a narrow model, they will miss weak signals or overcommit to a single future. Scenarios improve performance by expanding perception before action is required. This cognitive shift becomes organizational value when it changes decisions, investments, and monitoring.

### Uncertainty requires multiple futures, not a single forecast

Forecasting often collapses uncertainty into one expected path. Scenario planning preserves uncertainty by asking teams to work with several plausible futures. The goal is not to be right about one future; it is to be less surprised, more adaptive, and better able to recognize which future is emerging.

## Real-World Cases

### Royal Dutch Shell / Wack tradition

The Shell tradition, associated with Pierre Wack and later scenario practitioners, demonstrated that scenarios can change executive perception. The famous value was not that Shell possessed a perfect forecast, but that its managers had rehearsed discontinuity and could interpret change faster. Scenario planning became a way to prepare minds and organizations for strategic surprises.

## Connecting to Practice

Begin every scenario project with a performance question. What decision, capability, or strategic uncertainty requires better thinking? Then identify the assumptions currently shaping the organization's view of the future. The first deliverable is not a polished scenario story; it is a map of mental models and uncertainties. The scenarios come later as tools to challenge those assumptions and improve decisions.

## Key Takeaways

1. Scenario planning is not prediction; it is structured learning under uncertainty.
2. The deepest output is changed mental models, not beautiful story documents.
3. Performance-based scenario planning links foresight to measurable organizational value.
4. Multiple plausible futures help managers rehearse surprise before reality forces action.
5. A scenario project should begin with a decision or performance question, not with storytelling for its own sake.
