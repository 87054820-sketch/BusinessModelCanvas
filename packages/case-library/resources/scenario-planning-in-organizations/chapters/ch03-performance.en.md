# Performance-Based Scenario Planning

> **Core argument:** Scenario planning becomes managerial discipline when it is tied to performance diagnosis, implementation, assessment, and monitoring — not when it merely produces compelling future stories.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Chermack's original contribution is the Performance-Based Scenario System. It asks organizations to treat scenario work as a performance intervention with phases, outputs, measures, and responsibilities. This matters because scenario projects often stop too early. Teams produce narratives, feel energized, and then return to old routines. A performance-based system forces the project to begin with a performance question and end with assessment and monitoring.

## Core Concepts

### Performance-Based Scenario System

The system moves from preparation and diagnosis through exploration, development, implementation, assessment, and continuous monitoring. Preparation defines the project and performance issue. Exploration surfaces forces and assumptions. Development creates scenarios. Implementation uses them in decisions. Assessment examines satisfaction, learning, and performance. Monitoring keeps the work alive as signposts change.

### Predetermined Elements and Critical Uncertainties

Predetermined elements are forces treated as relatively certain for the planning horizon. Critical uncertainties are high-impact forces whose outcome is unclear. Separating the two is central to disciplined scenario development: predetermined elements provide continuity; critical uncertainties structure the scenario matrix and logics.

### Results Assessment Domains

Chermack argues that assessment should include satisfaction, learning, and performance. Participant satisfaction is easy to measure but insufficient. Learning asks whether mental models changed. Performance asks whether system or financial outcomes improved, recognizing that some effects are delayed and indirect.

## Key Arguments

### Scenario planning should be assessed as a performance intervention

Organizations spend significant time and money on scenario projects. It is therefore irresponsible to avoid assessment. The measures must be adapted to the nature of scenario work, but the project should still ask whether it improved thinking, decision quality, readiness, or organizational outcomes.

### Scenario development moves from forces to logics to implications

Good scenarios are not random stories. The process identifies driving forces, ranks uncertainty and impact, separates predetermined elements from critical uncertainties, constructs logics, writes scenario plots, and creates a research agenda. This sequence keeps creativity connected to strategic relevance.

## Real-World Cases

### Performance diagnosis matrix

A performance diagnosis matrix links scenario work to capacity, stakeholder needs, and outcome questions. Instead of asking "What futures are interesting?" the team asks "What performance problem makes uncertainty consequential?" That shift keeps scenario work from becoming an academic exercise.

## Connecting to Practice

Run a scenario project as a closed loop. Begin with the performance issue and intended decisions. During development, document assumptions, predetermined elements, critical uncertainties, scenario logics, and signposts. During implementation, wind-tunnel current strategies. During assessment, capture satisfaction, learning, behavior, system, and financial indicators where feasible. During monitoring, revisit signposts and refresh scenarios when needed.

## Key Takeaways

1. Performance-based scenario planning starts with a performance or decision question.
2. Scenario development requires disciplined movement from forces to logics to implications.
3. Predetermined elements and critical uncertainties play different roles.
4. Assessment should include satisfaction, learning, and performance outcomes.
5. Monitoring makes scenarios part of ongoing management rather than a one-off workshop.
