# Methods and Techniques: A Comprehensive Review

> **Core argument:** Scenario planning is not one method. Different techniques make different trade-offs among participation, rigor, data intensity, narrative richness, and decision usefulness; the right method depends on organizational context.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Many teams reduce scenario planning to a two-by-two matrix workshop. Chermack shows that the field is broader. There are intuitive-logics traditions associated with Shell and GBN, quantitative methods such as trend-impact and cross-impact analysis, morphological approaches, field anomaly relaxation, French school variants, Futures Group techniques, TAIDA-style processes, and many procedural hybrids. This chapter matters because method choice shapes what the organization learns.

## Core Concepts

### Scenario Method Taxonomy

A scenario method is a disciplined way of moving from uncertainty to plausible futures. Some methods emphasize expert judgment and narratives. Others emphasize quantitative relationships among variables. Some privilege broad participation; others require technical expertise. Treating them as interchangeable weakens practice.

### Intuitive Logics

Intuitive logics is the Shell/Schwartz/GBN family. It identifies driving forces, ranks them by uncertainty and impact, chooses critical uncertainties, builds scenario logics, and writes narratives that help managers discuss implications. Its strength is strategic conversation and assumption testing; its weakness is dependence on facilitation quality and participant discipline.

### Method-Context Fit

No method is universally best. The right approach depends on the decision type, time horizon, data availability, expertise, time, culture, and the group's readiness to discuss uncertainty. A technically rigorous method may fail if it cannot engage decision makers; a participatory method may fail if the decision requires deeper quantitative modeling.

## Key Arguments

### Method selection is strategic design

Choosing a method is not an administrative detail. It determines who participates, what evidence counts, how uncertainty is structured, how stories are built, and how decisions are tested. A scenario project should therefore choose method after clarifying the performance question.

### The two-by-two matrix is useful but insufficient

The matrix of two critical uncertainties is powerful because it creates four memorable futures. But scenario planning cannot be reduced to drawing quadrants. Teams must still identify driving forces, judge uncertainty and impact, construct logics, write coherent plots, and test implications.

## Real-World Cases

### Shell / GBN intuitive logics

The Shell tradition uses narrative scenarios to support strategic conversation. Teams identify forces, cluster uncertainties, and build stories that challenge executive assumptions. The method works best when the point is not to compute a forecast but to help leaders recognize possible discontinuities and rehearse action.

## Connecting to Practice

Before selecting a method, write the decision context. Is the question exploratory or operational? Is the time horizon short or long? Are decision makers willing to participate? Is data rich or sparse? Does the organization need a shared language, quantitative sensitivity, or creative reframing? Then choose a method whose strengths fit the job. Do not default to the two-by-two matrix simply because it is familiar.

## Key Takeaways

1. Scenario planning includes multiple methods, not one canonical workshop.
2. Intuitive logics is powerful for narrative, participation, and strategic conversation.
3. Quantitative and morphological methods may fit different data or decision contexts.
4. Method choice should follow the decision and performance question.
5. The matrix is a tool, not the whole practice.
