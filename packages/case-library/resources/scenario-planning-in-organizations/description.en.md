# Reading map — Scenario Planning in Organizations

This book is best read after a team understands the basics of scenario thinking. It shifts the focus from “how do we create plausible futures?” to “how do we run scenario planning as an organizational project that affects decisions and performance?”

## What problem it solves

- Scenario work produces interesting stories but no organizational decision.
- Teams jump into scenario workshops without clarifying purpose, sponsor support, or assessment criteria.
- Scenarios are developed but not deployed into strategy, portfolios, business models, or design rules.
- Scenario projects end without documenting learning, impact, or follow-up signals.

## Contents / reading map

1. **Foundations** — why scenario planning helps organizations learn under uncertainty and why it should be treated as a performance system.
2. **Performance-based scenario system** — separates scenario building from scenario deployment and connects both to organizational inputs and outputs.
3. **Project preparation** — clarify purpose, scope, stakeholders, sponsorship, and readiness before workshops begin.
4. **Scenario exploration** — collect signals, driving forces, trends, stakeholder views, and uncertainties.
5. **Scenario development** — build internally consistent scenarios that challenge mental models.
6. **Scenario implementation** — use scenarios for strategy discussion, wind-tunneling, decisions, portfolios, and action design.
7. **Project assessment** — document results, learning, changes in perception, decision impact, and next monitoring needs.

## How it maps to PinGarden

- `business-model-environment` — feeds signals, trends, forces, and uncertainty candidates into the scenario project.
- `scenario-matrix` — turns the most important uncertainties into scenario stories and robust moves.
- `business-model-canvas` — pressure-tests the current or proposed model in each scenario.
- `portfolio-map` — compares which bets survive multiple futures.
- `design-criteria-canvas` — turns robust moves into decision criteria.

## How it differs from The Art of the Long View

`The Art of the Long View` is the classic mindset source for scenario thinking. `Scenario Planning in Organizations` is more operational: it explains project preparation, stakeholder involvement, scenario deployment, assessment, and the human side of perception and organizational learning.

## Common misreadings

- Treating scenario planning as a workshop artifact rather than a project system.
- Skipping project preparation because the scenario matrix seems easy to draw.
- Forgetting deployment: scenarios must change strategy conversations and decisions.
- Failing to assess results, so the organization cannot learn whether scenario planning helped.
