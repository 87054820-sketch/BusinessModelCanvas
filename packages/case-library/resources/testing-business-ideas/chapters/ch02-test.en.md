# Test: The Experiment Library

> **Core argument:** Experiments are not interchangeable tactics. A good testing team chooses the experiment that matches the hypothesis, the uncertainty stage, and the evidence required for the next decision — starting cheap and fast, then increasing fidelity as confidence grows.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Chapter 1 made assumptions visible. This chapter answers the next question: **how do we test them?** The book's experiment library is not a bag of growth hacks; it is a decision system for reducing risk with the right level of evidence. When uncertainty is high, cheap discovery experiments help the team learn directionally without wasting money or brand trust. When the team is closer to an execution decision, stronger validation experiments ask customers for behavior, commitment, money, or reputation. The skill is not running the fanciest test; it is choosing the minimum experiment that can produce evidence strong enough for the next decision.

## Core Concepts

### Experiment Library

The experiment library contains 44 experiment types described by purpose, cost, setup time, run time, capabilities required, risk tested, and evidence strength. This structure lets a team compare tests before it spends effort. A customer interview, a search trend analysis, a clickable prototype, a Wizard of Oz test, a concierge experiment, a letter of intent, and a pre-sale all reduce risk — but they answer different questions and produce different evidence.

### Discovery Experiments

Discovery experiments are used when uncertainty is high and the team needs to learn whether its general direction is right. They test basic assumptions and produce first insights for course correction. Examples include customer interviews, discussion forums, search trend analysis, online surveys, paper prototypes, storyboards, data sheets, brochures, explainer videos, and product boxes. They are usually cheaper and faster, but evidence is weaker because much of it comes from what people say or from low-commitment behavior.

### Validation Experiments

Validation experiments confirm that a chosen direction is very likely to work. They ask for stronger behavior or commitment: a click, a sign-up, a simulated purchase, a letter of intent, a pre-sale, or real use of a manually delivered service. Wizard of Oz and concierge tests are especially powerful because the team can test demand and service fit before building automation or full infrastructure.

### Experiment Selection Criteria

Choosing an experiment starts with three questions: What type of hypothesis are we testing? How much uncertainty remains? How urgent is the decision? Early desirability questions often call for interviews, search trends, or surveys. Later commitment decisions need smoke tests, pre-sales, letters of intent, or concierge/Wizard of Oz experiments. Bigger budget is not better if the test is mismatched to the question.

### Experiment Card Anatomy

Each experiment has a repeatable anatomy: cost, setup time, run time, capabilities, risk type, and evidence strength. These attributes make experiments comparable. A team can see whether it needs research skills, design skills, marketing capacity, or technical support; whether the test can run in hours, days, or weeks; and whether the evidence is weak discovery or stronger validation.

### Experiment Sequences

The book repeatedly emphasizes that teams do not usually run one experiment and then build a huge business. They run **sequences**. Each experiment reduces uncertainty enough to justify the next one. Evidence fidelity rises over time: interviews shape the hypothesis, search data checks scale, a prototype tests interaction, a concierge test reveals service economics, and a pre-sale or letter of intent tests commitment.

## Key Arguments

### Match the experiment to the uncertainty stage

A pre-sale is too expensive and risky if the team still does not know whether customers have the problem. A customer interview is dangerously weak if the team is deciding whether to manufacture, hire, or scale. The right experiment is the one whose evidence strength matches the decision at hand.

### Start cheap and increase fidelity

The science-kit example illustrates the progression. First, interview parents to understand jobs, pains, and gains. Then search for online demand around science fair ideas. Then manually curate kits for 20 children with a concierge test, measuring creation time, cost, shipping, and satisfaction. None of these alone validates the model; together they form a path from weak insight to stronger evidence.

### Different experiments create different evidence

An interview creates quotes; a search trend analysis creates volume data; a concierge test creates operational and satisfaction data. The team needs to know what each evidence type can and cannot prove. Testing is a portfolio of learning bets, not a quest for one magic experiment.

## Real-World Cases

### Science-kit experiment sequence

The running case asks whether millennial parents prefer curated science projects that fit their children's education level. The team first interviews 20 parents and seeks an 80% accuracy ranking of top jobs, pains, and gains. It then analyzes domestic search volume and sets a threshold such as 10,000 monthly searches. Finally, it manually curates kits for 20 children and measures time to create, cost to create, cost to ship, and satisfaction. The lesson is to earn stronger experiments with earlier learning.

### Customer Interview

A customer interview is ideal for exploring jobs, pains, gains, and willingness to pay, but it is not a substitute for observing what people will do. The source recommends scripts, a narrow target segment, 15–20 interviews, exact notes, a 15-minute debrief, affinity sorting, and updating the Value Proposition Canvas. Its value is qualitative insight; its limit is behavioral proof.

### Wizard of Oz and Concierge

Wizard of Oz makes the front-end look automated while the back-end is manual. Concierge is even more explicit: the team manually delivers the service. Both let the team test whether customers value the experience before investing in automation. Manual delivery is a learning shortcut, not a fake product strategy.

## Connecting to Practice

When choosing a test, start from the hypothesis, not from the experiment name. If the hypothesis is about customer jobs, run interviews or forums. If it is about search demand, run trend analysis or ads. If it is about usability, use storyboards or clickable prototypes. If it is about willingness to pay, use a smoke test, letter of intent, pre-sale, concierge, or Wizard of Oz. Write down what evidence the experiment can produce, what it cannot prove, and what the next stronger test would be if the result is promising.

## Key Takeaways

1. The experiment library is a decision system, not a menu of random tactics.
2. Discovery experiments are cheap and fast, but usually produce weaker evidence.
3. Validation experiments ask for behavior, commitment, or money and support bigger decisions.
4. Select experiments by hypothesis type, uncertainty, urgency, cost, time, capability, and evidence strength.
5. A sequence of experiments beats a single impressive experiment.
6. Interviews tell you what people say; stronger tests reveal what they do.
7. Manual tests like concierge and Wizard of Oz are legitimate ways to learn before automation.
