# Evidence: Measuring Learning, Not Just Outcomes

> **Core argument:** An experiment does not produce truth by itself. It produces evidence; the team must judge the strength of that evidence, turn it into insight, and update confidence before deciding what to do next.

## Why This Chapter Matters

After a team runs experiments, the dangerous temptation is to declare victory or failure too quickly. A customer quote, a survey answer, a search-volume number, a click, a pre-order, and a purchase are all evidence — but they do not deserve the same confidence. This chapter teaches the discipline of learning: classify what kind of evidence you have, separate weak signals from strong ones, synthesize raw data into insights, and decide how confident you should be. Without this discipline, experimentation becomes theater: lots of tests, little learning.

## Core Concepts

### Evidence

Evidence is data generated by an experiment or collected in the field that supports or refutes a business hypothesis. It can come as quotes, observed behavior, conversion rates, orders, purchases, search volume, satisfaction scores, or operational measurements. Evidence is not just any data; it is data connected to a specific hypothesis.

### Strength of Evidence

Evidence strength is the reliability with which evidence supports or refutes a hypothesis. The book evaluates strength along four dimensions. Stronger evidence is based on **facts rather than opinions**, **what people do rather than what they say**, **real-world settings rather than lab settings**, and **large investments rather than small investments**. An interview quote is useful but weak; a customer pre-purchase or public professional commitment is much stronger.

### Insights

Evidence does not speak on its own. An insight is what the team learns from studying evidence: whether a hypothesis is supported or refuted, what new direction emerged, and what action now makes sense. The book distinguishes looking at something from looking for something: analysis must connect raw evidence back to the business risk.

### Confidence Level

Confidence level is how much the team believes evidence is strong enough to support or refute a specific hypothesis. It depends on three factors: the type and strength of evidence, the number of data points per experiment, and the number/type of experiments conducted for the same hypothesis. Confidence rises when the team runs several experiments of increasing strength, not when it repeats weak tests and calls the count proof.

### Learning Card

The Learning Card turns test results into a reusable decision artifact: **We believed… We observed… From that we learned… Therefore we will…** It forces the team to connect hypothesis, evidence, insight, and action rather than leaving learning scattered in meeting notes.

## Key Arguments

### Experiments produce evidence, not automatic answers

The source is explicit: evidence does not speak on its own. A team must collect evidence from different experiments, distinguish strong from weak signals, and synthesize insights. Without interpretation, teams either overreact to noise or ignore uncomfortable results.

### Behavior beats stated intention

The evidence-strength ladder moves from opinions to facts, from saying to doing, from lab to real-world settings, and from small to large investment. "I would buy" is weak; a purchase, pre-sale, or letter of intent is stronger because the customer has given up money, time, or reputation.

### Confidence grows through varied, stronger tests

Five interviews may be useful, but they do not equal a simulated sale. A strong learning path might start with interviews to find jobs/pains/gains, scale the insight with a survey, then test real interest with a simulated sale. The number of data points matters, but evidence type matters just as much.

## Real-World Cases

### Science-kit customer interviews

For the science-kit subscription, interviews generate quotes: parents want unique projects, grade-appropriate materials, clear instructions, and might pay for a complete kit. These quotes help the team understand jobs, pains, gains, and language. But they remain what people say; they are good for discovery, weak for predicting purchase.

### Search trend data

Search trend analysis checks actual search behavior around phrases such as "science fair ideas" and grade-specific science-fair terms. Search volume is more factual than stated interest and can reveal whether there is enough demand to pursue. It does not prove willingness to pay, but it improves the team's view of market size and topic intensity.

### Concierge science-kit data

A concierge experiment manually curates kits for 20 children and measures time to create, cost to create, cost to ship, and parent satisfaction. This evidence is stronger because it touches real behavior and operational feasibility. It also shows that one experiment can reduce several risks at once: desirability (satisfaction), feasibility (time and process), and viability (cost and shipping economics).

## Connecting to Practice

After every experiment, do not ask only "Did it work?" Ask four better questions. What evidence did we collect? How strong is it on the opinion/fact, say/do, lab/real-world, small/large-investment dimensions? What insight does it create about the specific hypothesis? What confidence level now makes sense? Capture the answer in a Learning Card and decide whether the next step should be stronger testing, a new hypothesis, a pivot, or a kill decision.

## Key Takeaways

1. Evidence is data connected to a hypothesis; random metrics do not count as learning.
2. Evidence strength rises from opinions to facts, say to do, lab to real world, and small to large investment.
3. Insights are what you learn from evidence; evidence alone does not decide.
4. Confidence level depends on evidence strength, data points, and the variety of experiments for the same hypothesis.
5. More weak data is not the same as strong evidence.
6. Refuting a hypothesis can be a successful experiment if it prevents building the wrong thing.
7. Use Learning Cards to connect belief, observation, insight, and action.
