# Manage: Building a Testing Culture

> **Core argument:** Individual experiments are tactics; a repeatable evidence-based operating system is the strategy. Teams must turn insights into action through clear decisions, ceremonies, flow management, leadership behavior, and ethical guardrails.

## Why This Chapter Matters

A team can design good hypotheses, run appropriate experiments, and still fail if learning never changes decisions. Markets and technology move quickly; insights can expire in months, weeks, or days. This chapter therefore moves from experiment execution to experiment management. It defines how teams decide to persevere, pivot, or kill; how ceremonies create a repeatable rhythm; how leaders protect a testing culture; and why ethics matter when experiments touch real customers.

## Core Concepts

### Decide: Persevere, Pivot, Kill

After learning, the team must act. **Persevere** means continue testing the idea based on evidence and insights — either by moving to the next important hypothesis or by testing the same hypothesis with a stronger experiment. **Pivot** means make a significant change to the idea, value proposition, or business model. Because the trajectory changes, earlier evidence may become irrelevant and parts of the model must be retested. **Kill** means stop pursuing the idea because evidence shows it will not work in reality or profit potential is insufficient.

### Evidence-to-Action Decision Logic

The decision logic is explicit. If evidence supports the hypothesis, test the next critical hypothesis or run a higher-fidelity experiment on the same one. If insight is unclear, continue testing. If evidence refutes the hypothesis, pivot or kill. If a new insight appears, decide whether it opens a better direction, requires retesting, or invalidates the opportunity.

### Experiment Ceremonies

Ceremonies turn experimentation into a repeatable system. In one month the book recommends weekly planning, daily standups, weekly learning, biweekly retrospectives, and a monthly deciding or stakeholder review. Planning sets learning goals and priorities; standups surface blockers; learning sessions synthesize evidence and actions; retros improve the process; deciding meetings align stakeholders on learnings, blockers, and investment decisions.

### Experiment Flow

Experiments must move through a visible flow: hypothesis, design, run, learn, decide. Managing flow prevents the team from starting too many tests, losing evidence, or confusing busyness with progress. Limiting work in progress matters because every unfinished experiment is trapped learning.

### Testing Culture and Leadership

Culture is what gets rewarded. A testing culture values evidence over opinion, rewards learning even when it invalidates an idea, and treats experiments as normal work rather than special projects. Leaders create this culture by protecting budgets, granting customer access, asking questions instead of dictating answers, and making investment decisions based on evidence.

### Ethics and Experiment Pitfalls

Experiments involve real people and real trust. Smoke tests, vaporware, simulated sales, and manual back-ends can be legitimate, but they can also mislead or damage a brand if used carelessly. Teams must avoid testing trivial assumptions, designing confirmation-biased experiments, declaring victory too early, ignoring negative results, or manipulating customers. The practical rule is simple: learn fast, but do not be evil.

## Key Arguments

### Learning has an expiration date

The book warns that learning faster than everyone else is no longer enough; the team must put learning into action. Markets, customer behavior, and technology move so quickly that insights can expire. Evidence that does not change action is inventory, not progress.

### Ceremonies make experimentation repeatable

A one-off test depends on heroic effort. A ceremony system creates an operating cadence. Weekly planning identifies the learning goal and priorities. Daily standups unblock work. Weekly learning converts evidence into insights and actions. Retrospectives improve how the team tests. Monthly deciding gives stakeholders a forum for evidence-based investment choices.

### Leaders build culture through incentives

If leaders say "experiment" but punish invalidated ideas, teams will hide evidence or design tests that prove what leaders want to hear. If leaders ask for evidence, reward learning, and fund teams incrementally based on what they discover, testing becomes a habit.

## Real-World Cases

### Monthly experiment ceremony system

The source gives a concrete operating model for one month: four weekly planning meetings, twenty daily standups, four weekly learning meetings, biweekly retros, and one monthly deciding meeting. Each has attendees and agenda. The case shows that experimentation is not just creativity; it is calendar discipline.

### Science-kit decision cycle

The science-kit team combines interview quotes, search trend data, and concierge operating data. If evidence supports the hypothesis, the team might test payment with a stronger CTA. If results are unclear, it continues testing. If parents like the idea but economics fail, it may pivot the offer or kill the model. The point is that every result must produce an action, not just a slide.

## Connecting to Practice

Create a visible experiment board with columns for hypothesis, designed, running, evidence collected, learned, and decided. Run weekly planning to choose only a few tests, daily standups to remove blockers, weekly learning sessions to complete Learning Cards, retros to fix the system, and monthly stakeholder reviews to decide funding and direction. In every review, ask: What did we believe? What did we observe? What did we learn? What will we do now — persevere, pivot, or kill?

## Key Takeaways

1. Experiments are tactics; the management system is what turns them into capability.
2. Decide means turning insight into action: persevere, pivot, or kill.
3. Evidence that supports, refutes, is unclear, or creates new insight calls for different actions.
4. Experiment ceremonies create rhythm: planning, standup, learning, retros, deciding.
5. Manage experiment flow so unfinished tests do not trap learning.
6. Testing culture is built by leadership behavior and incentives, not slogans.
7. Ethical guardrails matter: learn fast without deceiving or harming customers.
