# Design: From Assumptions to Testable Hypotheses

> **Core argument:** Every business idea is a bundle of untested assumptions. The discipline of innovation is to make those assumptions explicit, turn them into falsifiable hypotheses, and attack the riskiest ones with cheap experiments — before betting real resources on a vision that may turn out to be a hallucination.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Too many entrepreneurs and corporate innovators execute ideas prematurely because they look great in presentations, make perfect sense in the spreadsheet, and read irresistibly in the business plan — only to discover later that the vision was a hallucination. *Testing Business Ideas* inserts a distinct phase between the idea and the business: **Search & Testing**, the activity of reducing the risk of pursuing ideas that won't survive contact with reality. This opening chapter sets up the entire method: the two loops of design and test, the three risks every idea carries, the team and environment that make testing possible, and the move from belief to hypothesis. Everything later in the book — the 44 experiments, the evidence rules, the management ceremonies — is in service of this one shift.

## Core Concepts

### The Two Iterative Loops (Design + Test)

Innovation runs as two interlocking loops. The **Business Design loop** — Ideate → Business Prototype → Assess — turns vague ideas, market insights, and evidence into concrete value propositions and business models. The **Test loop** — Hypothesize → Experiment → Learn → Decide — reduces the risk that the design won't work. The loops feed each other: your first design iteration runs on intuition; every subsequent one runs on evidence and insights produced by testing. Design without testing is fiction; testing without design has nothing to test.

### Search & Testing vs. Execution

Testing is what happens in the uncertain space *before* execution. An idea looks good in theory; an experiment reveals whether it works in practice. The entrepreneur's and innovator's number-one task is therefore to reduce risk and uncertainty — to run rapid experiments that let you learn and adapt rather than scaling a company that isn't ready.

### The Three Risks: Desirability, Feasibility, Viability

To test a big idea you break it into smaller testable hypotheses covering three risks. **Desirability** (market risk): the market is too small, too few customers want the value proposition, or you can't reach, acquire, and retain them. **Feasibility** (infrastructure risk): you can't build, scale, or access the key resources, key activities, and key partners. **Viability** (financial risk): you can't generate more revenue than cost, or customers won't pay enough. Each risk lives in specific blocks of the Business Model Canvas and Value Proposition Canvas, which lets a team locate exactly where its idea is fragile.

### Team Design, Behavior, and Environment

Behind every successful new venture is a great team — but a team is three things, not one. **Team Design**: a cross-functional core (design, product, engineering, plus legal, data, sales, marketing, research, finance as needed), with diversity of experience so you don't bake your biases into the business, and ideally some entrepreneurial scar tissue. **Team Behavior**: six leading-indicator behaviors — data-influenced, experiment-driven, customer-centric, entrepreneurial, iterative, and willing to question assumptions. **Team Environment**: the team must be **Dedicated** (not multitasking across projects), **Funded** (incrementally, VC-style, based on what they learn), and **Autonomous** (given space to own the work), supported by facilitative leadership, coaching, customer access, and a clear strategy.

### Business Hypothesis

A business hypothesis is an assumption your value proposition, business model, or strategy builds on — the thing you must learn about to know whether the idea might work. You write it starting with "**We believe that…**" (e.g. "We believe that millennial parents will subscribe to monthly educational science projects for their kids"). Because phrasing everything as a belief invites confirmation bias, you deliberately also write **refuting** hypotheses ("We believe parents *won't* subscribe…") and test the competing claims together.

### Assumptions Mapping

Assumptions Mapping is a team exercise (adapted from Gothelf & Seiden's *Lean UX*) that makes every desirability, feasibility, and viability hypothesis explicit on sticky notes, then prioritizes them on two axes: **Importance** (y) and **Evidence** (x). The result is four quadrants. The **top-right** — important hypotheses with little or no evidence — defines your near-term experimentation, because those are the assumptions that, if false, will kill the business.

### Experiment and the Test Card

An experiment is a procedure to reduce risk; it produces weak or strong evidence that supports or refutes a hypothesis, and can be fast/slow and cheap/expensive. A well-formed experiment has **four components**, captured on the **Test Card**: (1) the **Hypothesis** (pulled from the top-right quadrant), (2) the **Experiment** description, (3) the **Metrics** you'll measure, and (4) the **Criteria** — the success threshold defined *before* you run. A **Call-to-Action experiment** is a special form that prompts a test subject to take an observable action, so you measure behavior, not opinions.

## Key Arguments

### From "I think" to "We believe that…, and we'll know we're right when…"

The single most powerful habit in innovation is replacing opinion with a falsifiable claim plus an explicit success criterion. A hypothesis you can't invalidate isn't an experiment — it's a faith statement. Defining the criteria up front is what stops a team from rationalizing whatever data arrives, and it is what separates systematic entrepreneurship from expensive gambling.

### Sequence the risks: desirability first, then feasibility, then viability

Desirability is the most common killer: if nobody wants it, how well you could build it or monetize it is irrelevant. So the book explores desirability hypotheses first, feasibility second, viability third — mapping each onto canvas blocks so the team can see where the leap of faith really lies.

### A great team still fails in the wrong environment

Team composition is necessary but not sufficient. The best cross-functional team, exhibiting all six behaviors, will still stall if it is part-time, unfunded, micromanaged, or walled off from customers. Leaders must *design* the Dedicated/Funded/Autonomous environment — otherwise teams stop generating evidence and quietly start guessing and building anyway.

## Real-World Cases

### The science-project subscription (the running example)

A team weighs a monthly subscription of curated science projects for millennial parents. The chapter shows the hypothesis being sharpened from a vague "millennials will spend a lot on science projects" into "millennial parents with kids aged 5–9 will pay $15 a month for curated projects matched to their child's education level," plus operational hypotheses like "we can buy materials wholesale for under $3 a box." Each is rewritten until it is Testable, Precise, and Discrete. The lesson: precision is what makes a belief checkable.

### Rovio and the 51 failed games

Rovio's blockbuster *Angry Birds* came after six years and 51 games that flopped. The case underlines why entrepreneurial experience and iteration tolerance belong on a team: success usually arrives on the far side of many invalidated attempts. Early failures are paid-for learning, not verdicts.

## Connecting to Practice

Start any new venture by running an Assumptions Mapping workshop with the core and supporting team. Extract every assumption from your Business Model and Value Proposition Canvases onto sticky notes, color-coded by desirability/feasibility/viability. Place each on the Importance–Evidence grid. Take the top-right quadrant and, for each hypothesis, fill a Test Card — hypothesis, experiment, metric, criteria — choosing the cheapest, fastest experiment that can still produce relevant evidence. Run discovery experiments to course-correct, then validation experiments to confirm. And before any of this, make sure the team is dedicated, funded, autonomous, and has direct access to customers.

## Key Takeaways

1. Every business idea is a bundle of untested assumptions; testing exists to find the hallucinations before you scale.
2. Run two loops — design (Ideate→Prototype→Assess) and test (Hypothesize→Experiment→Learn→Decide) — feeding evidence from one into the other.
3. Classify risk as desirability, feasibility, or viability, and explore them in that order.
4. A hypothesis must be Testable, Precise, and Discrete, and written as "We believe that…" — with refuting versions to fight confirmation bias.
5. Assumptions Mapping prioritizes hypotheses by importance and evidence; the top-right quadrant is where you experiment first.
6. A good experiment has four parts — hypothesis, experiment, metric, criteria — with success defined before you run.
7. Teams need the right design *and* behavior *and* a Dedicated/Funded/Autonomous environment, or even a strong team stalls.
