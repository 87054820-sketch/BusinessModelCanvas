# Reading map — Platform Revolution

This is a foundation for **Platform Strategy**. It helps readers decide whether a case is a normal BMC or a platform built around multi-sided networks, core interactions, and governance mechanisms.

## What problem it solves

- Teams define platform too loosely as an online marketplace or any product with network effects.
- Analysis names supply and demand sides but does not identify the core interaction or value unit.
- Cold start, governance, openness, and monetization trade-offs are not discussed together.
- Pipeline companies try to become platforms by changing channels without changing value creation.

## Contents / reading map

1. **Pipeline vs Platform** — platforms enable external producers and consumers to interact, rather than pushing value through a linear chain.
2. **Core Interaction** — the minimum viable unit usually includes participants, value unit, filtering/matching, and consumption.
3. **Network Effects** — distinguish same-side and cross-side effects and assess whether growth reinforces the system.
4. **Architecture & Governance** — openness, access rules, quality control, trust, and incentives.
5. **Monetization** — who pays, when they pay, and whether payment harms the core interaction.
6. **Competition** — platform competition often centers on ecosystems, data, rules, and multi-homing, not only features.

## How it maps to PinGarden

- `platform-ecosystem-map` — maps participants, value flows, governance points, and network effects.
- `business-model-canvas` — turns platform mechanisms into customers, activities, resources, revenue, and cost.
- `value-proposition-canvas` — each side of the platform may need its own VPC.
- `customer-journey` — finds friction from onboarding to matching, transaction, and retention.
- `portfolio-map` — compares multiple ecosystems or business lines in a platform company.

## What to read first

- For **platform identification**, start with Pipeline vs Platform and Core Interaction.
- For **cold start**, read network effects, subsidies, pricing, and which side to activate first.
- For **governance**, read openness, rules, trust, quality, and low-quality participation.
- For **monetization**, ask whether the charging point weakens supply, demand, or matching quality.

## Common misreadings

- **Serving two customer groups is not enough.** A platform needs repeatable core interaction and network effects.
- **Network effects are not automatically good.** Negative effects, congestion, and low quality can hurt the system.
- **More open is not always better.** Openness must be designed with quality, trust, safety, and monetization.
