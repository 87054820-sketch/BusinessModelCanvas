# Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors

Michael Porter's first book (1980) established the modern field of competitive strategy. Before Porter, strategy was largely about generalship, case-by-case intuition, and experience-curve heuristics. After Porter, strategy became a discipline of structural analysis with repeatable frameworks.

The book's argument unfolds in three parts:

### Part I: General Analytical Techniques

**Industry Structure Analysis (Ch. 1)** — The famous Five Forces:
1. **Threat of new entrants** — barriers to entry (economies of scale, product differentiation, capital requirements, switching costs, access to distribution, government policy)
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — supplier concentration, switching costs, threat of forward integration
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — buyer concentration, product standardisation, threat of backward integration
4. **Threat of substitute products or services** — relative price-performance, switching costs, buyer propensity to substitute
5. **Rivalry among existing firms** — industry growth, fixed costs, product differentiation, exit barriers

The Five Forces collectively determine the profit potential of an industry. This is the structural foundation every business-model analysis must rest on.

**Generic Competitive Strategies (Ch. 2)** — Three internally consistent ways to achieve above-average performance:
- **Cost leadership** — lowest-cost producer serving a broad market
- **Differentiation** — uniquely valued product/service serving a broad market
- **Focus** — serving a narrow segment with either cost focus or differentiation focus

The central warning: "stuck in the middle" — trying to pursue more than one generic strategy simultaneously without making the necessary organisational trade-offs almost guarantees low profitability.

**Competitor Analysis Framework (Ch. 3)** — Four diagnostic components:
- **Future goals** — what drives the competitor
- **Current strategy** — what the competitor is doing and can do
- **Assumptions** — what the competitor believes about itself and the industry
- **Capabilities** — the competitor's strengths and weaknesses

### Part II: Generic Industry Environments

Chapters 9–12 apply the analytical framework to five archetypal industry structures:
- **Fragmented industries** — no dominant firm (strategy: consolidation)
- **Emerging industries** — newly formed or re-formed (strategy: shape structure)
- **Maturing industries** — slowing growth, experienced buyers (strategy: cost or differentiation)
- **Declining industries** — shrinking demand (strategy: leadership, niche, harvest, or divest)
- **Global industries** — cross-border competition (strategy: global vs. multi-domestic)

### Part III: Strategic Decisions

The closing chapters address vertical integration, capacity expansion, and entry into new businesses. These are framed as structural decisions that change the industry's Five Forces — not just the firm's position within them.

### Why This Book Matters for PinGarden

The Five Forces are the "pre-BMC" step — before you map a business model, you must understand the structural economics of the industry it operates in. Use the `business-model-environment` canvas to map the forces, then move to the BMC to see how the business model positions against them. When the forces are unfavorable, no business model can sustainably outperform.
