# P&G Connect & Develop: outside-in at full corporate scale

In June 2000, A.G. Lafley took over as CEO of Procter & Gamble in the middle of a multi-quarter share-price slide. The company was running a textbook closed-innovation operation — about 7,500 internal scientists across 30 R&D facilities, ~$2B per year in R&D spend, ~3.4% of revenue. The model had worked for fifty years. By the late 1990s it had stopped scaling: R&D productivity was declining, the launch pipeline was thinning, and the share price had halved.

Lafley's intervention was structural. Instead of asking R&D to spend more or hire harder, he set a goal that **50% of P&G's innovations would come from outside the firm**, up from roughly 15% historically. The label for the programme — "Connect & Develop" — was designed to displace the older "Research & Develop" mental model.

## Two BMCs because the *boundary* IS the model

*Business Model Generation (BMG 2010)* uses Connect & Develop as the outside-in paradigm at pp. 110-111. The reason the case is taught with two BMCs and not one is the same reason all open-business-models cases are: **the boundary between the firm and its partners is a load-bearing part of the model**. Hiding it inside one BMC misses what's structurally new.

The closed-R&D BMC reads like a generic Fortune-50 consumer-goods firm. Internal scientists invent products; patents defend them; supermarket distribution + TV advertising sell them. The pink stickies in `Cost Structure` and `Customer Relationships` mark where the model was breaking: declining R&D productivity, no direct consumer relationship, share price falling. Notice the pink sticky in `Key Partners` — *(NO innovation partners — closed by design)*. That single absence is the whole pattern shift.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{canvasId="f3483670-281f-4ec2-87f5-f124ebca56e1"}

The Connect & Develop BMC keeps almost all the right-side blocks (Customer Segments, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams) the same — open innovation isn't about changing what you sell, it's about changing how you source what you sell. The transformation lives in `Key Resources`, `Key Activities`, and especially `Key Partners`.

The blue "cross-firm dependency" stickies in `Key Partners` are the new structure: InnoCentive (the challenge platform — see `innocentive` in this library), YourEncore (re-engaged retired scientists from companies like P&G, Eli Lilly, Boeing), NineSigma + yet2.com (technology brokers), university tech-transfer offices, independent inventors. None of these existed as Key Partners on the closed BMC. Every one of them is a *bridge* — Larry Huston's term in the 2006 HBR article *(Huston & Sakkab 2006 HBR)* — that brings external IP, talent, or technology into P&G.

## Three flagship outside-in launches

What Connect & Develop actually shipped:

- **Mr Clean Magic Eraser** (2003). The melamine-foam material was developed by BASF in Germany; P&G's Connect & Develop scout team found it, P&G commercialised it under the Mr Clean brand. P&G never invented the material — it bought / licensed and packaged.
- **Olay Regenerist** (2003). The peptide ingredient (palmitoyl pentapeptide-3) was developed by a small French firm called Sederma. P&G licensed it, formulated around it, and Regenerist became a $1B+ brand within three years. Olay's overall market position was rescued by ingredient outside-sourcing.
- **Crest SpinBrush** (2001). A $5 battery-powered toothbrush originally invented by a Cleveland-based small business (Dr. John's). P&G acquired the inventor + brand and scaled it through P&G distribution. Within two years it was the #1 toothbrush in the US.

The pattern across all three: P&G is no longer the inventor. It's the integrator + commercialiser. The R&D productivity gain (+85% by 2007 on flat spend) is real because each launched product carries a much smaller share of internal-invention cost.

## What goes wrong (and what P&G did about it)

*(Chesbrough 2006)* warns that outside-in breaks down in three places, all of which P&G hit:

- **NIH ("Not Invented Here") rejection.** Internal scientists rejected external ideas as inferior or threatening. P&G's structural fix: the 50% goal made open-innovation a measured KPI for R&D leaders, not an optional initiative. The pink sticky in `Key Activities` — *NIH-culture management (internal advocacy)* — is the active programme, not an aspiration.
- **Search cost dominates.** Without aggregator platforms, scouting external IP across the world's R&D ecosystem would cost more than internal R&D. Connect+Develop's bridges (InnoCentive in particular) exist exactly to push search cost down. Note the `Key Resources` sticky — *Curated network of ~1.5M external scientists* — that network IS the search-cost reduction.
- **Integration cost.** Even after finding external IP, integration (legal, technical, brand, manufacturing) has overhead. P&G's structural fix: a dedicated Connect+Develop team (~75 people, integration specialists, not scouts) whose Key Activity is to land external IP into P&G's manufacturing and commercialisation backbone. This is the part most copycats skip — and most copycats fail without it.

## Why this case sits next to GSK and InnoCentive

P&G is the *outside-in* paradigm of Open. GSK Patent Pools is the *inside-out* paradigm. InnoCentive is the connector platform that runs outside-in *as a service* for many seekers. Reading them together makes Open's two-sub-type structure legible and forces the right question when a user proposes a new "open" business: **which direction is value flowing across the firm boundary?**

- *External ideas / IP / talent flowing IN to be used internally* → outside-in (P&G, GE Ecomagination, LEGO Ideas).
- *Idle internal IP flowing OUT to be used externally* → inside-out (GSK patent pools, IBM licensing, Tesla open patents).
- *Both directions in significant volume* → bilateral; most mature open-innovation firms (P&G included) actually do both.
