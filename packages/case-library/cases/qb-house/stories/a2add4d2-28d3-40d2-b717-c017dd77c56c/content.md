# QB House: the ten-minute haircut as a functional service system

## Context and tension

Before QB House, a typical Japanese men’s haircut often came bundled with a broader barbershop experience: conversation, shampoo, shave, massage, hot towel, and a sense of personal service. That emotional orientation created value for some customers, but it also created waiting time, price, labor intensity, and operational variability. Many customers did not need the full ritual. They needed a clean, predictable haircut during a commute or lunch break.

QB House is a Blue Ocean Strategy case because it reframed the job. The question was not “how can a barbershop feel more luxurious?” but “what if the real job for many men is simply to get a reliable haircut with minimum time uncertainty?” By narrowing the job, the company could redesign the whole service system.

## The strategic move

QB House eliminated or reduced most nonessential emotional-service elements: shampoo, shaving, long consultation, beverage-like hospitality, and extended conversation. It raised speed, predictability, cleanliness, and transparent waiting. It created a 10-minute haircut process supported by ticket vending, standardized workflow, air-wash cleanup, and traffic-light waiting signals outside the store.

The strategic insight is that functional speed is not just a lower-price version of the same service. It changes site selection, staffing, process design, customer expectations, and capacity utilization. A small shop in a train station can work because the service unit is short and standardized.

## Read the Business Model Canvas first

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{}

The BMC shows a narrow but coherent model. The customer segment is time-constrained men who value convenience more than a full grooming ritual. The value proposition is not “best haircut” in an artistic sense; it is quick, clean, predictable, low-price hair maintenance.

Key activities are process standardization, station-level throughput, hygiene control, and queue visibility. Key resources are trained staff, compact store design, ticket machines, and air-wash equipment. Cost structure benefits from small footprints, high turnover, and reduced service steps. Revenue depends on many short visits rather than high ticket value per visit.

## Then read the Strategy Canvas

::canvas[blue-ocean-strategy-canvas]{}

The Strategy Canvas contrasts emotional barbershop factors with QB House’s functional curve. Traditional barbershops score high on service ritual, conversation, shampoo, shave, and personalization. QB House deliberately scores low on those. It scores high on speed, price clarity, wait-time visibility, cleanliness, and location convenience.

This is a different shape, not a “cheaper barbershop” line. If QB House tried to keep all traditional rituals and also promise 10 minutes and low price, the model would break. The blue ocean depends on making the narrow job explicit and refusing to serve every grooming need.

## ERRC logic

- **Eliminate:** shampoo, shave, massage, long consultation, and social ritual.
- **Reduce:** personalization depth, store size, and time spent per customer.
- **Raise:** speed, cleanliness, queue transparency, convenience, and price certainty.
- **Create:** a standardized 10-minute haircut system for people who treat grooming as a quick maintenance task.

## Risks and limits

QB House sacrifices customers who want emotional service, detailed styling, or a relationship with a barber. That is not a failure; it is the strategic boundary. The risk is operational: if wait times become unpredictable or haircut quality drops below an acceptable threshold, the value proposition collapses quickly because the model has fewer emotional buffers.

Expansion also depends on traffic density. The model fits stations, malls, and office areas better than low-footfall neighborhoods.

## Transfer lesson

The transferable lesson is to separate the core job from the inherited service ritual. Many service industries bundle attention, ceremony, and customization because the category historically did so. A blue ocean may appear when a company identifies a large segment that wants the outcome without the ceremony, then designs operations around that narrower promise.
