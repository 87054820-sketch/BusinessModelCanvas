# Strategy lens: Porter Five Forces

Swiss private banking is tagged with **Porter's Five Forces** because unbundling is not just an internal operating choice. It is a response to industry pressure: specialized infrastructure providers, product boutiques, changing client expectations, and regulatory transparency all weaken the old integrated model.

Industry rivalry increases when many banks promise similar discretion and wealth expertise. Supplier power appears in product manufacturers, technology platforms, and compliance infrastructure. Buyer power rises as wealthy clients compare global managers and demand independent advice. Threat of substitutes comes from family offices, low-cost wealth platforms, and specialist boutiques. Threat of entrants is lower for full private banks but higher for narrow specialists that attack one activity at a time.

Maerki Baumann responds by specializing in the customer-relationship business and outsourcing the other logics. Pictet responds by remaining integrated but investing in trade-off management. Five Forces explains why both positions can be coherent: they are different answers to the same structural pressures.

The framework should not reduce the case to “competition is high.” The important insight is where pressure enters the BMC: key activities, key partners, cost structure, customer relationships, and value proposition all change when the industry unbundles.
