# Drybar: blowouts without the salon haircut bundle

## Context and tension

Hair salons traditionally bundled cuts, color, treatments, and styling. Customers who only wanted a polished blowout for work, events, or confidence had to buy into the broader salon model: appointments, higher prices, stylist hierarchy, and uneven experience.

This is why the case belongs in the Blue Ocean Strategy library. The company did not merely improve within the old rules. It changed which buyers mattered, which factors deserved investment, and which inherited assumptions could be removed. A newcomer should read the case as a market-reconstruction story rather than a normal product-success story.

## The strategic move

Drybar eliminated cuts and color, reduced technical salon complexity, and raised speed, ambience, consistency, and occasion-based accessibility. It created the blowout bar as a focused service category.

The strategic move combines differentiation with a cost or complexity break. The company raises a few factors that matter strongly to overlooked buyers, while reducing or eliminating factors that incumbents treat as mandatory. That asymmetry is what makes the move teachable: the curve is not higher everywhere; it is meaningfully different.

## Then read the Strategy Canvas

::canvas[blue-ocean-strategy-canvas]{canvasId="1655b85f-a2f1-4224-b8ec-ec8784f3d416"}

The Strategy Canvas should be read as a shape. The red-ocean baseline scores higher on legacy complexity and expertise-heavy factors. Drybar scores higher on access, usability, personalized value, cost efficiency, or new-demand creation, depending on the case. That shape is the strategic argument. If the company tried to score high on every traditional factor and every new factor, it would likely recreate the cost structure of the old industry.

## ERRC logic

- **Eliminate:** cuts, color, and the full salon bundle.
- **Reduce:** technical consultation complexity and price uncertainty.
- **Raise:** speed, consistency, ambience, and occasion readiness.
- **Create:** a focused blowout bar with menu-like simplicity.

## Risks and limits

The model depends on repeat occasions and operational consistency. It can be vulnerable to fashion cycles, labor availability, and imitation by salons.

The case therefore should not be copied mechanically. The transferable insight is the discipline of fit: the offer, channel, operations, revenue model, and cost model must all support the same value curve.

## Transfer lesson

Unbundling a high-friction service can create demand when many customers want one repeatable outcome without buying the full professional bundle.
