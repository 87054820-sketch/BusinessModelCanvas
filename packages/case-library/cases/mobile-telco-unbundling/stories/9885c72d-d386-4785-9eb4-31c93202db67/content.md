## The premise

Mobile telecom operators have traditionally competed on **network quality**. Bigger towers, better coverage, faster speeds. But network quality is now a commodity — every major carrier in a developed market hits roughly the same dB at the same density.

So the smart operators are striking **network-sharing deals** with competitors. France Telecom, KPN, and Vodafone outsourced network operations and maintenance to equipment manufacturers (Nokia Siemens Networks, Alcatel-Lucent, Ericsson). India's Bharti Airtel went further: network ops to NSN + Ericsson, IT to IBM. Why? Because their leadership realised the **key asset is no longer the network** — it is **brand and customer relationships**.

Under the hood, this is the same Hagel & Singer (1999) argument we've already seen for Swiss private banking: most companies are three businesses tangled together — infrastructure management (driven by **scale**), customer relationship (driven by **scope**), product innovation (driven by **speed**). Forced under one roof, they undermine each other.

Mobile telcos are picking apart that tangle.

## Unbundled #1 — Equipment manufacturers run the network

Network operations was always a scale game, but it was a *single-tenant* scale game. NSN, Alcatel-Lucent, and Ericsson cracked the multi-tenant version: same engineering team, same NOC, same operations playbook — applied to many telcos at once. Each individual telco gets a lower per-byte cost than they could have managed in-house, even after the supplier's margin.

France Telecom outsourced. KPN outsourced. Vodafone outsourced. Bharti was the most aggressive — they outsourced before they hit critical mass on subscriber count, betting that focusing internal capex on customer growth would compound faster than running their own towers.

## Unbundled #2 — The pure customer-relationship telco

With the network outsourced, the telco can sharpen its focus on **branding and segmenting customers**. Customer relationships become the key asset and the core business. By concentrating on customers and increasing share of wallet with current subscribers, the telco can leverage investments made over years acquiring and retaining them.

Bharti Airtel is the canonical example. Today it's one of India's largest telcos — and structurally, it is closer to a consumer-marketing company than a network operator. The marketing budget dominates the P&L. The retail footprint dominates the balance sheet. The network is somebody else's problem.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{canvasId="c74d45db-efa1-4908-b4d1-973fb1ffbdca"}

## Unbundled #3 — Innovation comes from outside

For product and service innovation, the unbundled telco turns to **smaller, creative firms**. Innovation requires creative talent, and small dynamic organisations attract that talent better than large telcos can. Telcos work with multiple third parties for a constant supply of new technologies, services, and media — mapping, games, video, music.

Mobilizy of Austria built location-based service solutions for smartphones — including a popular mobile travel guide. Sweden's tat focused on advanced mobile user interfaces. Telcos license these IP packages and ship them under their own brand. The creative firm gets distribution; the telco gets innovation it couldn't have built in-house at the same speed.

## What it teaches

The Strategyzer point isn't that unbundling is automatically right. It's that **the bundled business model is the unconscious default**, not a choice. Once you make the three economic logics explicit — scale, scope, speed — you can decide which ones you actually want to own and which you'd rather buy from a specialist.

The parallel with Swiss private banking (the other unbundling case in this library) is exact: in both industries, the integrated form was a 20th-century historical accident, and the 21st century is sorting them into specialised players plus a small handful of consciously-integrated ones (Pictet in banking; vertically-integrated holdouts in telecom).
