# Strategy lens: Porter Five Forces

Carvana is a **Porter Five Forces** case because online used-car retail changes the pressure structure of a fragmented, trust-poor industry.

Rivalry is intense among dealers, marketplaces, auctions, and online retailers. Buyer power is high because customers compare inventory, price, financing, delivery, and return policies. Supplier power appears in vehicle acquisition, reconditioning capacity, logistics, financing partners, and inspection data. Substitutes include traditional dealers, peer-to-peer marketplaces, leasing, ride-hailing, and delaying purchase. New entrants can launch digital storefronts, but scale logistics and reconditioning are harder to replicate.

The strategic question is whether Carvana's model reduces customer pain enough to offset capital intensity. The BMC must therefore be read through Five Forces: transparent pricing and returns reduce buyer anxiety, while logistics and reconditioning are both advantage sources and cost risks.

Five Forces does not prove Carvana is attractive. It shows why the business model must win simultaneously on trust, inventory velocity, cost of capital, and operational scale.
