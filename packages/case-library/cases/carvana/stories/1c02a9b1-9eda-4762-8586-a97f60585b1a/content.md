## The premise

For a hundred years, buying a used car in the US meant the same ritual: drive to a lot, find a salesperson, haggle for hours, accept whatever the dealer's financing arm offered, drive away vaguely sure you'd been outmanoeuvred. Carvana's bet, founded in 2012 in Phoenix by Ernest Garcia III, was that the entire experience could be flipped: pick the car online (with 360° photos and a real Carfax), get a no-haggle price, finance in the same flow, and have it delivered to your driveway in 24 hours — or, if you wanted the photo opportunity, drive to the company's signature glass car-vending-machine and watch your purchase descend on a Hot Wheels-style elevator.

It worked. By 2021, Carvana was a $40B-market-cap public company. Then 2022 happened.

## Where the model is unusual

Three differentiators are baked into the canvas. First, **fully online flow**: every step from vehicle selection to title transfer happens on the website. The user never speaks to a salesperson unless they want to. Second, **no-haggle pricing + 7-day return**: the price you see is the price; if you don't like the car, return it within seven days, no penalty. This was a frontal attack on the dealership's information-asymmetry margin — and it created enormous trust premium. Third, **the car vending machines** — 33 glass towers nationwide that double as marketing infrastructure (every time someone uploads their pickup video to TikTok, Carvana gets free distribution).

## The growth engine

The acquisition Carvana never gets enough credit for is **ADESA**, North America's #2 wholesale auto auction, bought from KAR Global for $2.2B in May 2022. ADESA gives Carvana 56 physical sites with reconditioning capacity already in place — replacing what would have taken a decade to build organically. The flywheel works like this: customers trade in their old cars to Carvana, those cars feed ADESA, ADESA wholesales them to *other* dealers (Carvana's competitors!), and the spread plus Carvana's own retail margin compound. Every traded-in car becomes either a retail sale or a wholesale revenue line — never a dead asset.

## Industry baseline

The blue stickies cover what every used-car retailer must do regardless of channel: source vehicles, inspect, recondition, finance, register, deliver. CarMax does all of this from physical lots. Franchise dealers do it through OEM relationships. Carvana does it digitally — but the underlying operations are the same shape. The 150-point inspection isn't a differentiator; it's a price of entry.

## Open questions

The ADESA acquisition was financed with high-yield debt at the worst possible time — interest rates spiked, used-car wholesale prices collapsed from their 2022 peak, and Carvana's stock fell 99% from $370 to under $4 by December 2022. Bankruptcy was widely expected. What happened instead is one of the more interesting corporate restructurings of the decade: the company negotiated a 2023 debt exchange that pushed maturities out, focused operations on per-unit profitability over volume, and by Q4 2024 was reporting record gross profit per unit. The pink stickies on the canvas mark what's still unresolved: the post-restructure debt is still substantial, used-car cycles will turn again, and franchise dealers (the ones Carvana threatened) are now mature enough at e-commerce to fight back.

## What the case teaches

Carvana's BMC is a study in the difference between a great Value Proposition and a great Cost Structure. The yellow stickies — fully online, 7-day return, vending machines — are unambiguously customer-loved. The blue stickies are operationally fine. The pink stickies are where reality bites: a beautiful customer experience financed on the wrong cost curve almost killed the company. The post-2023 Carvana is the same VP, recalibrated against what the unit economics actually allow.
