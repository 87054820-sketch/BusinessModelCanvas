## The premise

When Alibaba spun out logistics in 2013, the conventional wisdom was that e-commerce eventually has to own its delivery — Amazon was already 19 years into building its own fulfilment network, and JD.com had famously chosen the same heavy-asset path in China. Jack Ma's Cainiao took the opposite bet: **don't own the trucks**. Instead, Cainiao would coordinate the existing third-party courier network — the so-called "Three-Company" express companies (STO, YTO, Best, Yunda) and dozens of regional players — by giving them a shared data layer and a unified electronic-waybill standard. Whatever the network couldn't do, Cainiao would build itself, but only at the leverage points: smart warehouses, neighbourhood Cainiao Stations, and global EWTP e-hubs.

A decade later, Cainiao is the logistics backbone for Alibaba's $1T+ GMV ecosystem and the operational reason AliExpress can promise 5-10 day delivery from China to most of the world.

## Where the model is unusual

Three differentiators define Cainiao versus its competitors. First, **asset-light coordination**. Cainiao doesn't own a courier fleet; it owns the data platform that makes the existing fleets interoperate. Routing decisions, electronic waybills, package tracking — all standardised across partners. SF Express owns its own trucks. JD Logistics owns its own warehouses end-to-end. Cainiao's bet is that data + standards captures more value than capital + fleet, and that being asset-light makes the platform faster to scale internationally. Second, **EWTP global e-hubs**. The Electronic World Trade Platform is Cainiao's six-hub network — Kuala Lumpur, Dubai, Liège, Hong Kong, Bangkok, Mexico City — that consolidates Chinese cross-border outbound at hub-and-spoke scale. AliExpress Choice's 5-10 day delivery promise is technically possible because of these hubs. Third, **Cainiao Station**. The neighbourhood-pickup network in China (over 100,000 stations) solves last-mile cost in the cheapest way: humans in convenience stores collecting packages on behalf of dozens of recipients per drop.

## The growth engine

Cainiao's growth runs on **embedded demand from Alibaba's own platforms**. Taobao + Tmall ship hundreds of millions of parcels daily; AliExpress + Lazada + Trendyol + Daraz add cross-border volume. Each of those platforms defaults to Cainiao for logistics — there's no "vendor evaluation" step the way an external client would conduct. The captive demand makes the unit economics work: warehouses, e-hubs, and station networks all amortise across volume Alibaba's commerce stack delivers automatically. The 3PL partners (STO, YTO, etc.) get steady, predictable volume in exchange for accepting Cainiao's electronic-waybill standard — a bargain neither side can easily walk away from.

## Industry baseline

The blue stickies cover what every logistics company has to do regardless of asset model: sortation centres, line-haul transport, customs clearance, last-mile dispatch, dispute resolution, customer service. Cainiao does these — usually through partners, not directly — but the operational quality has to be there or shippers lose trust. There is no innovation hiding in sortation; it's a price of entry.

## Open questions

The pink stickies describe a strategic environment under sustained pressure. **SF Express and JD Logistics** continue to outperform Cainiao on premium speed (next-day delivery) and high-value categories — domains where heavy-asset control matters more than coordination. **Pinduoduo and Temu** have been quietly building their own logistics arms, threatening to make Cainiao's captive-demand advantage fade. **The 2023 HKEX IPO was retracted in early 2024** — Cainiao filed, then withdrew, and Alibaba ultimately re-folded the business back into the parent. The independent equity story is on hold. **Geopolitics** — US-China cross-border tariff scrutiny, EU's Digital Services Act, sanctions complexity — creates execution risk on every EWTP route. **De minimis tariff exemption** in the US, which lets sub-$800 parcels enter duty-free, is structurally important for AliExpress Choice — and politically vulnerable.

## What the case teaches

Cainiao's BMC is the cleanest illustration of the **platform-vs-fleet** strategic choice in logistics. The yellow stickies (asset-light coordination + EWTP) are not just operational decisions — they're a bet on what "owning logistics" actually means in an internet era. SF Express bet on owning trucks. JD bet on owning warehouses. Cainiao bet on owning the protocol. All three companies are large and successful; the question is which strategy compounds best as e-commerce volumes saturate domestically and the next decade of growth moves to cross-border. Cainiao's EWTP positioning suggests Alibaba thinks the answer is the protocol play — but the IPO retraction signals that even the protocol play has financial-market questions to answer.
