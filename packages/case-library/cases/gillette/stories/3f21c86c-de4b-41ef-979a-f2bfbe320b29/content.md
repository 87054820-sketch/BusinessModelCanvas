# Gillette 1901: the urtype of bait-and-hook

King C. Gillette filed US Patent 775,134 in November 1901, granted November 1904: a safety razor with disposable double-edge blades. The mechanical innovation was real — safer than straight razors that had to be sharpened on a strop — but the *business* innovation was bigger and was easy to miss at the time. Gillette had figured out that selling someone a razor was the *less profitable* half of the business. The far more profitable half was selling them blades, every week, for the next forty years.

## Two BMCs because the asymmetry IS the model

*Business Model Generation (BMG 2010)* uses Gillette as the bait-and-hook paradigm case at pp. 100-103. The reason the case is taught with two BMCs and not one is exactly the same reason Multi-Sided Platforms is taught with two — the asymmetry IS the model. Mashing the razor side and the blade side into one BMC hides what makes the pattern work.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{canvasId="83deac35-a56b-4c3b-adab-8e4426337e15"}

The razor BMC reads strangely until you see the third sticky in `Revenue Streams`: "REAL revenue is from blade business" (cross-side dependency, blue). The razor's per-unit margin is low — sometimes negative when bundled into cereal boxes or distributed through Army contracts during WWI (Gillette signed a 3.5 million-razor military contract in 1917 that put the brand into nearly every American man's pocket within two years). The pink "sometimes negative" sticky in Revenue Streams is correct: this side is a *loss leader* in many distribution channels.

What the razor BMC really captures is *customer acquisition*. Every razor in circulation is a long-term blade customer. The patent enforces the lock-in: only Gillette blades fit Gillette razors.

::canvas[business-model-canvas]{canvasId="a19db9b8-62ab-4438-b3e7-96604b607680"}

The blade BMC is where the money is. High gross margin (~50-70% in the 20th century, similar today). Recurring revenue from every razor in circulation. The cross-side dependency on the installed razor base (blue sticky in Customer Segments) makes the connection legible: blades only sell because razors got distributed first.

## What goes wrong (and how Gillette fought it)

*(Anderson 2009)* notes that bait-and-hook businesses are particularly vulnerable to **cheap-substitute attack** — once the lock-in becomes visible, third parties try to break it. Gillette's history is a 120-year list of those attacks and the defenses:

- **Patent expiry → generic blades**. The original 1904 patent expired in 1921. Gillette responded by introducing a steady stream of *new* blade designs (each newly patented) and discontinuing the old razors that fit older blades. This treadmill — Mach 3 (1998), Mach 3 Turbo, Fusion, Fusion ProGlide, ProShield, etc. — is the bait-and-hook pattern's standard counter-attack.
- **Direct-to-consumer disruption (Dollar Shave Club, 2011-2016)**. DSC bypassed retail distribution, sold subscription blades by mail at half the price, and was acquired by Unilever for $1B in 2016. Gillette's market share fell from ~70% to ~50% in the US during that period; it had to launch its own DTC channel (Gillette Shave Club, then Gillette On Demand) and cut prices. Pink sticky in `Channels` and `Cost Structure` on the blade BMC.
- **Acquired by P&G ($57B, 2005)**. The acquisition itself is a sign that the business had ceased to compound the way it had — P&G bought Gillette into the consumer-packaged-goods playbook because the standalone blade-attack runway had been thinned by DSC and the patent treadmill was getting expensive to run.

## Why this case sits next to Spotify in the library

Gillette is the *bait-and-hook* sub-type of Free; Spotify is the *freemium* sub-type. Reading them side by side makes the three sub-types of Free legible and forces the right question when a user proposes a new "free" business: **which sub-type are you actually building?**

- *Subsidising across time, recovered through consumables on the same customer* → bait-and-hook (Gillette, HP printers, Nespresso).
- *Subsidising across users on the same platform, recovered through paid tier conversion* → freemium (Spotify, Skype, Dropbox).
- *Subsidising across two sides of a platform, recovered from advertisers* → ad-supported (Google Search, free TV, free metro newspapers — see `google-multi-sided`).

Different BMC shapes. Different failure modes. Different pricing strategies. Same Free pattern label.
