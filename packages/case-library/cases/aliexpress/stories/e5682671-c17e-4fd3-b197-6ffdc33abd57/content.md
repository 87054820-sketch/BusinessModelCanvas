## The premise

AliExpress launched in 2010 as Alibaba's English-facing version of Taobao — a way for Chinese sellers to find buyers outside China without the minimum-order-quantity barriers of B2B Alibaba.com. For a decade, it was the secret weapon of every dropshipper and Etsy reseller in the world: zero MOQ, 14-30 day shipping, prices that made eBay look expensive. Russia, Brazil, Spain, and France became disproportionately large markets — places where mainstream e-commerce was either expensive (Brazil) or sanctioned (Russia, post-2014). And then in 2022, Temu launched.

Temu — Pinduoduo's overseas arm — copied AliExpress's deepest moat (China-direct supply) and added what AliExpress hadn't bothered to invest in: **fast delivery + clean UX + a fully-managed merchant model where the platform sources, prices, and ships, and the seller just provides goods**. AliExpress's 2023-2024 strategic response is **AliExpress Choice**: same idea, applied to AliExpress's existing seller and Cainiao infrastructure.

## Where the model is unusual

Three differentiators define AliExpress today. First, **direct access to China's manufacturing base**. No middlemen, no licensed importers — the seller of the $3 phone case is a Shenzhen factory or a Yiwu trader, and you ship from there. Second, **no MOQ, dropshipper-friendly**. AliExpress made its early reputation as the supplier-of-supply for thousands of small Western e-commerce shops; that's a structural strength competitors took years to copy. Third, **the Choice program** — fully-managed, 5-10 day delivery, after-sales guaranteed. Choice is AliExpress's bet that the platform can compete with Temu by leveraging Cainiao's logistics + Alibaba's existing seller relationships, without requiring sellers to learn a new operating model.

## The growth engine

The flywheel runs on **Cainiao's logistics network + Alibaba's adjacent platforms**. Cainiao's six EWTP e-hubs (Kuala Lumpur, Dubai, Liège, Hong Kong, Bangkok, Mexico City) make 5-10 day delivery to most of the world feasible at AliExpress's price points. Sister platforms Lazada (Southeast Asia), Trendyol (Turkey), and Daraz (South Asia) provide regional muscle. And the seller base — hundreds of thousands of Chinese SMEs already accustomed to the AliExpress UX — is a moat: they can't all simultaneously pivot to Temu, so AliExpress retains a structural catalog advantage even when Temu out-spends it on consumer marketing.

## Industry baseline

The blue stickies cover what every cross-border marketplace has to do: a multi-language platform, payments in 50+ currencies, fraud detection, dispute resolution, regional VAT compliance, customer support in customer-language. Amazon does these. eBay does these. Temu does these. There's no differentiation here — only execution quality, and AliExpress has a 14-year head start on most of it.

## Open questions

The pink stickies describe a strategic landscape that's never been more contested. **Temu** is winning on consumer marketing in the US and Europe, spending Pinduoduo-scale ad budgets that Alibaba is reluctant to match. **The US de minimis tariff exemption** (which lets shipments under $800 enter duty-free, the structural enabler of AliExpress and Temu's US economics) is under active legislative review. **Counterfeit and IP issues** continue to drag both regulatory scrutiny and brand-owner lawsuits. **Geopolitics** — China-US tensions, EU's Digital Services Act enforcement, India's outright ban on Chinese apps — define the operating environment. **Shein's fast-fashion category** captures the Gen-Z fashion budget AliExpress used to access via long-tail trend items.

## What the case teaches

AliExpress's BMC is interesting because the strategic-differentiation cells (yellow) and the open-question cells (pink) point at the same underlying fact: **direct access to China's supply chain is no longer a unique moat**. Temu has it. Shein has it. TikTok Shop is acquiring it. The question becomes: when several players have the same supply base, which one wins on logistics reliability (Cainiao's bet), brand trust (Choice's promise), or pure price (Temu's strategy)? AliExpress is betting on the first two; the strategic move worth watching is whether buyer trust at 5-10 day delivery actually beats Temu's pure-price urgency for enough categories to shift category share.
