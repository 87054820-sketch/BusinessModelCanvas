## The premise

For most apparel brands, the question is **how do we sell more**. For Patagonia, the question is closer to **how do we sell better** — fewer, longer-lasting items, while paying the planet for the privilege of doing business. The result is a company that, in its 50-year history, has run anti-consumption ad campaigns ("Don't Buy This Jacket" in the 2011 *New York Times*), pioneered a paid repair program (Worn Wear), and in 2022 transferred all ownership to a trust whose only beneficiary is Earth itself.

## Where the model is unusual

Three things mark Patagonia apart from the outdoor-apparel industry baseline. First, the **1% for the Planet pledge**: 1% of revenue (not profit — that distinction matters; it's a tax even in years the company loses money) goes to environmental causes. Yvon Chouinard founded the cross-industry network to encourage other brands to do the same. Second, **lifetime warranty + free repairs**: the brand asks customers not to throw away clothes; it pays Worn Wear repair technicians to fix what comes back, and operates roving repair trucks that show up at outdoor events. Third, the **2022 ownership restructuring**: Chouinard gave away 100% of the company to the Patagonia Purpose Trust + Holdfast Collective — an explicit anti-IPO that locks in the mission against shareholder pressure. The *New York Times* headline read "Billionaire No More".

## The growth engine

Patagonia doesn't grow by maximising new-customer acquisition. It grows by maximising customer lifetime value among environmentally-aligned buyers — REI shoppers, climbers, fly-fishermen, surfers. The brand integrity itself is the moat: a customer who's seen "Don't Buy This Jacket" and the 2022 ownership announcement believes the company means what it says, and that belief commands a price premium of 30-50% over comparable technical apparel. The premium funds R&D, repair programs, and the 1% donation — closing the loop. Forty thousand customers attended in-store Worn Wear repair clinics in 2023; each one is a touchpoint that competing brands can't replicate without bearing the same cost.

## Industry baseline (the table-stakes layer)

The blue stickies on the canvas are the parts every outdoor-apparel brand must execute well: design, supply chain (Patagonia uses Tier-1 Fair-Trade-certified factories in Vietnam, Thailand, Sri Lanka, Mexico), retail + DTC + wholesale channels, customer support. There is no innovation hiding in the table stakes — Patagonia just does them with above-average craft.

## Open questions

The pink stickies acknowledge the tensions. Worn Wear and the lifetime-repair promise are in active conflict with revenue from new sales — the more durable the gear, the longer between repurchases. Patagonia's bet is that brand premium plus new tribes joining offsets the slowdown, but the math is delicate. The 2022 ownership transfer locks in the mission but raises an unanswered question: with Chouinard now in his 80s, who carries the cultural torch when he's gone? And the high price point that funds the 1% donation also caps Gen-Z penetration in a generation that's broke and increasingly buys vintage on Depop.

## What the case teaches

Patagonia's BMC is interesting precisely because the strategic-differentiation cells (yellow) are also the **cost** cells — the differentiation isn't free. The brand has decided, explicitly, to spend its margin on integrity rather than growth. That's a coherent business model, and it works because every part of the canvas has been brought into alignment with that single decision. Most brands that try to copy Patagonia copy the marketing surface (the "purpose" campaigns) but skip the cost; Patagonia's edge is that it's willing to pay the cost.
